# Section 1: Why Testing Matters for the Laws of the Spirit Project

**A Note on the Registry and the Calibration Cycle.** The testing protocols in this volume are governed by Volume 6’s calibration provisions. Every testable hypothesis here (V4.LotS-H1 through V4.LotS-I3 in vol4-claims.yml) inherits its confidence from a specific Volume 1 or 2 claim via the registry’s confidence_inherited_from field. Testing results flow upward: when Volume 4 data bears on a Volume 1 or 2 claim, the Council’s annual calibration cycle formally adjusts that claim’s confidence up or down based on the evidence. The disconfirmability requirement (V4.M3, held at 95% confidence — the highest in the corpus) is the threshold for such adjustments. A proposed increase in confidence requires evidence consistent with the claim’s stated disconfirming conditions not being met; a proposed decrease requires evidence that matches the original claim’s flip_conditions field. Ad hoc confidence adjustments unsupported by disconfirmability-compliant evidence are not permitted. This discipline is how the whole work is meant to stay honest over time.

## The Workshop Group as the Original Testing Community
The Laws of the Spirit project began with a testing community before it had any laws to test. When Amanda published the article in the church newsletter in January 2003, inviting people to join a workshop to explore whether God's spiritual world operates according to discoverable principles, three men responded: Tom, Larry, and John. The four of us committed to meeting regularly and to doing something unusual for a church Bible study: we agreed that we would not accept any principle as a Law of the Spirit unless it could be demonstrated — shown at work in our own lives and in scripture, discussed honestly, and refined through shared experience. If one of us proposed a principle and the others could not confirm it from their own experience or from the text, it went back into the exploration pile.

That discipline was uncomfortable. It meant saying things like, "I don't see that" or "That hasn't been my experience" in a setting where we were all trying to grow spiritually. It meant being willing to be wrong in front of each other. But it produced something that a less disciplined exploration would not have produced: a set of claims that had survived genuine scrutiny from four different men with four different life histories.

That workshop group was, in the language of research methodology, a purposive sample with a structured peer-review process. I did not use those words at the time. But looking back, that is what it was. And it teaches me something important about how to build Volume 4: good testing of spiritual principles requires community. It cannot be done alone. The lone spiritual seeker, reading, praying, and journaling in isolation, may arrive at deep personal experience, but they cannot test whether that experience reflects a pattern that holds across persons, contexts, and time. Testing requires at least two witnesses (Matt. 18:16). It requires the friction of different perspectives. It requires being willing to be wrong.

## Three Kinds of Testing — and Why All Three Are Needed
When I say that the LotS project needs testing, I mean three different things, and I want to be clear about which is which.

**The first kind is personal and experiential testing.** This is what the original workshop group did. It is what happens when someone reads Rom. 10:17, "faith comes from hearing, and hearing through the word of Christ,” and then pays close attention to whether that is actually how faith arrives in their own life. It is subjective. It is vulnerable to confirmation bias. It cannot be generalized beyond the person. But it is irreplaceable, because the Laws of the Spirit, if they are real, should be visible first in the lives of people who are seriously trying to live by them.

**The second kind is small-group communal testing.** This is what the workshop group was doing when it functioned at its best. It is what happens when five or six people commit to the same formation process — the same structured engagement with scripture, the same framework for understanding where they are in the journey — and then compare notes. Communal testing can detect patterns that personal testing cannot. If one person says, "I've noticed that my trust in scripture deepens after every significant trial," that is personal experience. If five people independently report the same pattern, and if that pattern maps onto Jas. 1:2–3's description of how tested faith produces steadfastness, something more interesting is happening. Communal testing is the LotS project's natural habitat.

**The third kind is formal academic research.** This is what I am building toward in the doctoral work at Liberty University. It requires operational definitions of constructs, validated instruments, controlled conditions, sufficient sample sizes, and statistical analysis. It is the slowest and most demanding kind of testing, and it cannot do everything; it cannot measure the movement of the Holy Spirit in a person's heart. But it can measure behavioral outcomes, stage progression, and the correlation between specific practices and specific results over time in ways the other two kinds of testing cannot. All three kinds are needed. They are not in competition; they are in conversation. The formal research should confirm what personal experience and communal discernment have already seen. And where it does not confirm them, that is data worth examining carefully.

## What Would Count as Evidence Against a Claim?
The hardest question in the LotS testing program is the falsifiability question. A claim that cannot, even in principle, be shown to be false is not a testable claim. It is a statement of faith. Some of the LotS claims are, honestly, closer to statements of faith than to testable propositions, and I think that is appropriate. The existence of God, the reality of the Holy Spirit, the trustworthiness of scripture: these are presuppositions of the LotS project, not conclusions from it. I am not going to test whether God exists. I am going to test whether, given that God exists and that the Spirit is active, the specific mechanisms proposed in Volumes 1–3 produce the outcomes those volumes predict.

Let me give some concrete examples of what disconfirming evidence would look like for three specific claims.

Claim LotS-H3 states that heart formation is progressive and follows the five stages of the Affective Taxonomy. Disconfirming evidence would include: consistent failure to observe identifiable stage transitions in groups following the PROAPT model over time; random distribution of observable behaviors across stages with no directional pattern; or a pattern showing that people who engage in reflective bible study show no more stage progression than people who do not. If such patterns were consistently observed across multiple groups and multiple contexts, the claim would need revision.

Claim LotS-H4 states that reflective bible study is the primary discipline that advances heart formation at every stage. This claim draws from the MOVE study's finding (Hawkins & Parkinson, 2011) that reflective bible study is the one discipline present at every stage and every transition. Disconfirming evidence would include: a comparison study showing that groups engaged in other spiritual disciplines (prayer, service, worship) without reflective bible study show equal or greater stage progression than PROAPT groups; or evidence that the PROAPT model's specific structure (as opposed to any structured scripture engagement) makes no difference to outcomes.

Claim LotS-F3 states that trials are not interruptions to faith formation but the primary mechanism for stage transitions. Disconfirming evidence would include longitudinal data showing that people in stable, low-trial life circumstances exhibit equal or greater stage progression than those experiencing significant challenges; or narrative data from spiritual formation participants describing their most significant growth moments as exclusively associated with peaceful, undisturbed periods rather than with testing.

I am not expecting that evidence to appear. The claims are grounded in scripture and confirmed by the workshop group's experience and the academic literature. But I want the LotS project to be willing to look honestly at contrary evidence when it appears. A testing protocol that is designed only to confirm what it already believes is not a testing protocol. It is propaganda.

## What Volume 4 Will and Will Not Do
Volume 4 will build a testing scaffolding for the spiritual formation claims at the heart of the LotS project. It will propose specific protocols for individual and small group testing. It will draft a design of a formal research program that could be implemented in a multi-site church study. It will draft the assessment instruments needed to operationalize the key constructs. And it will map the testing protocols back to the Open Trails identified in Volume 3, showing which trails this research program begins to explore.

Volume 4 will not prove that God is real, that the Spirit is active, or that scripture is trustworthy. Those are the foundations, not the conclusions, of the project. It will not claim that the proposed instruments are fully validated — they are drafts, ready for piloting and refinement. And it will not pretend that the spiritual formation journey can be fully captured in a measurement framework. Some of what is most important in a person's growth with God is precisely what resists quantification: the moment of hearing the word with a new clarity, the quiet turning of the will in prayer, the sudden recognition of a character of Christ reflected in a neighbor's face. The measurement scaffolding is there to capture the traceable shadow of those moments, not to replace them.

With those limits stated honestly, let us build.

> Discussion Questions for Small Groups

**Section 1 Discussion Questions**

**1. **The original workshop group committed to not accepting any principle unless it could be demonstrated through shared experience and scripture. What would it mean to bring that same discipline to your current small group or Bible study? What would have to change?

**2. **I distinguish three kinds of testing: personal/experiential, small group communal, and formal academic research. Which of the three have you experienced in your own spiritual formation? Which feels most foreign, and why?

**3. **What would count as disconfirming evidence for a spiritual principle that you currently hold? Is there a belief about how God works that you would be willing to revise if the evidence pointed in a different direction? What would that evidence need to look like?

**4. **I say that testing God (Deuteronomy 6:16) and testing a framework for understanding how God works are different things. Do you agree with that distinction? Where is the line between demanding that God perform and honestly testing whether a proposed mechanism produces the outcomes it predicts?

**5. **Volume 4 acknowledges that some of the most important moments of spiritual formation resist quantification. How do you hold together the discipline of honest testing with the humility required to recognize that some of what God does cannot be measured?
