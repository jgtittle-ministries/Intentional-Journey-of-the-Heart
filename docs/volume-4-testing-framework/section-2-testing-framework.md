# Section 2: Testing Framework — Epistemological Foundations

## Four Sources, One Exploration
The Laws of the Spirit project has always drawn from four sources: scripture, reason, experience, and community/tradition. Readers who are familiar with the Wesleyan tradition will recognize the structure — it maps closely onto what has come to be called the Wesleyan Quadrilateral (scripture, tradition, reason, experience), with the LotS substitution of community for tradition. That substitution is intentional and worth explaining because it affects how we design the testing framework.

Tradition is the accumulated wisdom of the church across centuries, a powerful resource for any theological exploration. But tradition has a particular limitation for a project like the LotS: it can tell us what others have believed about the spiritual life, but it cannot show us the spiritual life happening in the room tonight, in this group, with these people. The community can do that. The workshop group's willingness to bring their own experience to the table, to say "here is what I observed in my own life this week," gave the exploration a living laboratory that no amount of reading the Fathers could provide. And community also provides the accountability that personal experience alone lacks: other people can see things in us that we cannot see in ourselves.

What does each source contribute to the testing framework, and what are its limits without the others?

**Scripture** is the non-negotiable foundation. Every proposed Law of the Spirit must be grounded in the biblical text, not just plausibly consistent with it, but clearly taught by it. Scripture alone, however, is not enough for testing: the history of the church is littered with interpretive disagreements among people who were all reading the same text with equal sincerity. Scripture needs reason, experience, and community to be received rightly.

**Reason** provides the interpretive and analytical tools to handle scripture carefully, to build coherent frameworks, and to identify logical inconsistencies in proposed laws. The Affective Taxonomy is a product of reason applied to human learning, and its application to heart formation in the LotS project is itself a reasoned move, not a scriptural one. Reason alone, however, is the most dangerous source in spiritual exploration: it can construct beautiful, internally consistent systems that are completely disconnected from the actual life of the Spirit. My engineer's instinct, that the system should be tight and elegant, needs to be constantly checked against the messiness of real human experience.

**Experience** is where the rubber meets the road. A proposed law that is scripturally grounded and logically coherent but that nobody has ever actually observed operating in human life should be held with suspicion. Experience is the first testing community. But experience is also the most vulnerable to distortion, to confirmation bias, to the human tendency to notice what we are looking for, to the difficulty of distinguishing the Spirit's movement from our own emotional state. Experience needs community to correct it and scripture to anchor it.

**Community** provides the interpersonal friction that the other three sources cannot provide on their own. Community is where proposed laws are tested against multiple life histories simultaneously. It is where the person who has not experienced what the law predicts can honestly say so. It is where the person who has experienced something dramatic can have that experience examined by others who love them enough to ask hard questions. The PROAPT model's 'Tell' step, where each member shares what happened and who they will tell, is a community testing mechanism built into the formation process itself.

I want to be clear about one risk in my substitution of community for tradition. Tradition’s particular value is that it cannot be captured by the blind spots of a present community, it reaches across centuries and carries the weight of correction that our particular time and context cannot generate on its own. Grudem’s concern about charismatic communities that trust their own Spirit-witness without sufficient engagement with historical orthodoxy is a fair warning, and the original workshop group was not immune to it. I am not reversing my substitution, but I want to add a methodological note: the testing protocol should include a deliberate step in which proposed laws are checked not just against scripture and community experience but against the major streams of the historical church’s formation theology, particularly the Desert Fathers, the medieval mystics, the Reformation tradition, and the Wesleyan and Pentecostal streams. If a proposed law has no recognition anywhere in two thousand years of Christian experience, that absence is data worth examining seriously before proceeding.

## Quantitative and Qualitative: Choosing the Right Tool
One of the most important early decisions in designing the LotS testing program is distinguishing which claims are primarily quantitative and which are primarily qualitative. This is not a binary; most complex human phenomena yield both kinds of data. But the distinction shapes instrument design, analysis strategy, and the kind of conclusions that can legitimately be drawn.

Some LotS claims predict outcomes that are, at least in principle, countable or rankable: stage transitions on the Affective Taxonomy, frequency of scripture engagement, measurable changes in epistemic trust scores over time, and behavioral changes observable to external witnesses. These are quantitative in nature. A person who moves from Stage 2 (Responding) to Stage 3 (Valuing) of the Affective Taxonomy should show observable behavioral changes — not just reporting that they value scripture more highly, but organizing their schedule differently, making costly choices in alignment with biblical principles, bringing the word to bear on real life decisions in a way that others around them can observe. That kind of change can be assessed, at least approximately, with well-designed instruments.

Other LotS claims predict outcomes that resist quantification: the deepening of discernment, the growing clarity of the image of God in a person as experienced by those around them, and the quality of a person's relationship with God. These are qualitative in nature. A person at Stage 4 (Organization) on the Affective Taxonomy is not just doing more of what they were doing at Stage 2, they are operating from a reorganized value system, seeing the world through a different lens, making meaning differently. That kind of change requires narrative data to capture: stories told in the person's own words, observed by people who know them well, documented over time.

The proposed testing program for the LotS project is therefore a mixed-methods design by necessity, not by methodological fashion. To illustrate what that looks like in practice: a mixed-methods study of LotS-H3 (heart formation is progressive and follows the Affective Taxonomy stages) would combine a quantitative component, administering the Personal Spiritual Formation Assessment at baseline, six months, and twelve months, looking for statistically significant stage progression in PROAPT groups compared to control groups, with a qualitative component: monthly narrative reflection entries from each participant, coded by trained researchers for evidence of stage-characteristic language, behaviors, and meaning-making patterns. The quantitative data would tell us whether movement is occurring. The qualitative data would tell us what that movement actually looks like from the inside, and would capture the transition moments, the specific experiences of testing, conviction, and surrender that mark the shift from one stage to the next.

## The Hidden Variable: The Holy Spirit
Every serious researcher of Christian spiritual formation faces a methodological problem that secular developmental psychology does not: the agent of transformation in the LotS framework is not primarily the PROAPT model, the Affective Taxonomy, or the facilitator's skill. It is the Holy Spirit, active, personal, and uncontrollable. John 3:8 "The wind blows where it wishes, and you hear its sound, but you do not know where it comes from or where it is going" is not just a theological statement; it is a methodological warning. The Spirit is the primary independent variable in the formation process, and it is not a variable any researcher can control, manipulate, or measure directly. Make no mistake, the Holy Spirit is a person who chooses to act as he wills, even though I’m using the language of a variable in testing. He is a participant in our work, rightly done.

This is a genuine problem. But it is a manageable one, if approached honestly. I want to propose three ways of handling the Holy Spirit as a research variable.

**First, acknowledge it as a foundational presupposition.** The LotS testing program does not attempt to explain the Spirit's role away by attributing all formation outcomes to psychological or social mechanisms. The claim is not that PROAPT produces heart formation through purely natural mechanisms. The claim is that PROAPT creates the conditions in which the Spirit characteristically works, conditions of attentiveness, humility, community, and engagement with the word. The research tests whether those conditions produce the predicted outcomes. It leaves the causation of those outcomes, at the deepest level, appropriately open.

**Second, treat Spirit-prompted events as data rather than noise.** In a conventional experimental design, dramatic unexpected events, sudden convictions, powerful emotional responses to scripture, experiences of hearing God's voice, would be treated as outliers or confounds. In the LotS testing framework, they are among the most interesting data points. Open Trail OT-5 asks whether revivals and dramatic conversion experiences require some prior heart preparation. The Narrative Reflection Protocol is designed, in part, to capture these moments and examine what preceded them. Did the person experience a period of unusual attentiveness to scripture? Was there a specific trial that had been softening the ground? What were the precursors? The Spirit is not a random variable, he is a person who acts. I’m suggesting that He tends to work in the context of the conditions the LotS project is trying to understand.

**Third, use the researcher's own spiritual formation as a calibration instrument.** This is unconventional in academic research, but it is entirely consistent with the LotS tradition. The original workshop group was testing the laws from the inside, all four members were themselves living the journey they were studying. A researcher who is personally engaged in PROAPT practice, who has a spiritual director or supervisor, and who is paying close attention to their own formation is better positioned to recognize what they are observing in their research participants. This is not a license for the researcher to project their own experience onto the data. It is a recognition that some things in the spiritual life are only visible to someone who has been there.

## Construct Validity: Measuring What We Mean
A testing program is only as good as the clarity of its constructs. If we say we are measuring "heart formation," we need to be precise about what that means, precise enough that two different researchers, using the same definition, would make the same assessment when observing the same participant. This is the construct validity problem, and it is particularly acute for spiritual formation research because the key constructs (heart, spirit, faith, trust, love) have rich theological meanings that resist simple operationalization.

The LotS project has several advantages here that most spiritual formation research frameworks do not. The Affective Taxonomy provides a detailed, multi-stage model of affective development with specific behavioral indicators for each level. The theological anthropology of Volumes 1–3 provides a definition of the heart as the affective center of the person, the site of emotions, will, moral choices, and desire for God, corresponding to the Hebrew leb, which grounds the construct in both scriptural and anthropological terms. And the Image of God framework provides four concrete developmental domains (Reason, Rulership, Relationship, Righteousness) that can be assessed individually.

The epistemic trust research of Campbell et al. (2021) is particularly useful here. Their work defines and validates a multi-dimensional construct of trust in communicated knowledge, with three dimensions: trust, mistrust, and credulity. They connect these dimensions to attachment theory: a person with a secure relational attachment style tends to engage with trusted sources (including scripture) with clear, balanced trust, neither defensively rejecting nor uncritically accepting. The LotS Scripture Trust Index, adapted from their instrument, will measure the trust dimension specifically, tracking movement along the continuum from "this is just a collection of old fables" to full integration into a personal biblical worldview, which is exactly Barna's (2003) Stage 5 endpoint.

Hall's (2017) Spiritual Transformation Inventory (STI), administered to over 28,000 students in more than forty Christian colleges, organizes spiritual formation into five domains: connection to God, connection to self and others, connection to a spiritual community, connection to spiritual practices, and connection to God's Kingdom. These five domains map closely onto the LotS framework and provide a validated reference structure. The LotS instruments will borrow selectively from the STI while adding the Affective Taxonomy stage structure and the Image of God 4Rs as additional dimensions.

Hawkins and Parkinson's (2011) MOVE study contributes one of the most important construct validity anchors in the LotS testing program: the empirical identification of four distinguishable growth stages with three observable transition movements. Their finding that reflective bible study is the one discipline present at every stage and every transition gives us both a quantitative prediction (PROAPT groups should show stage progression; non-PROAPT groups, holding other variables constant, should show less) and a qualitative prediction (stage transitions should be associated with deepened engagement with scripture, not merely with increased time in any spiritual activity).

The NCD Church Survey, developed by Christian Schwarz through NCD International, provides a third empirical reference point that fills a gap left open by the STI and MOVE studies: community-level health measurement. The Survey has been administered to more than 70,000 churches across 84 countries and 112 denominations, making it the most internationally and interdenominationally validated instrument in Christian community health research. Its construct validity methodology is worth examining closely: the Survey diagnoses health across eight quality characteristics, identifies the single “minimum factor” the weakest characteristic that functions as a rate-limiting variable and tracks improvement after targeted intervention in that area. This is a different kind of construct validity claim from the LotS instruments, but a complementary one. Where the MOVE study validates the existence of distinguishable individual growth stages and the STI validates the epistemic trust construct, the NCD Survey validates the claim that community health qualities are empirically distinguishable, measurable, and responsive to targeted intervention.

The limitation is important to name honestly: the NCD Survey is designed for the church organism as a whole, not for small groups or individual formation. It cannot be imported directly into the Vol 4 protocol. But the minimum-factor diagnostic logic, which identifies the rate-limiting variable, intervenes there first, tracks whether removing that constraint produces disproportionate gains, is worth borrowing for the small-group health assessment in Section 4, and the Survey’s international validation record provides the closest existing model for the eventual cross-cultural reach of the LotS testing program.

Hodge's (2005) multidimensional framework for spiritual assessment, developed for social workers, rounds out the epistemological foundation by providing a model of holistic spiritual well-being that integrates cognitive, affective, behavioral, and relational dimensions. His review of both qualitative and quantitative assessment tools gives the LotS testing program a methodological bridge between the clinical research tradition and the theological formation tradition.

## The Researcher's Own Formation as a Methodological and Ethical Consideration
I want to close this section with something that is not standard in academic research methodology but that belongs in any honest account of how to test the Laws of the Spirit: the requirement that the researcher be on the journey.

The LotS project has always insisted that you cannot study the spiritual life from the outside. This is not just a theological claim, it is a phenomenological one. There are things about Stage 4 of the Affective Taxonomy, about what it looks like and feels like when a person begins to organize their entire value system around biblical principles, that are only fully intelligible to someone who has been through that transition. There are things about hearing the voice of God, about the difference between the Spirit's whisper and one's own wishful thinking, that require a calibrated interior instrument to detect. That instrument is a human being who is themselves practicing attentiveness to the Spirit, who is receiving formation, who has a community that tells them the truth.

This creates both a methodological advantage and an ethical responsibility. The methodological advantage is that formed researchers bring a kind of pattern recognition to the data that untrained observers cannot. The ethical responsibility is that this same proximity creates the risk of confirmation bias and projection. The safeguard is not to remove the researcher from their own formation journey, that would be both impossible and counterproductive. The safeguard is to name the assumption explicitly, to build in external checks (peer review, supervision, the External Witness Interview), and to treat the researcher's own formation journey as a variable to be disclosed and accounted for, not hidden.

This is, finally, why the LotS project has always needed a community to carry it. No single researcher, not even one who is a serious practitioner, can hold all the necessary perspectives simultaneously. The testing program proposed in the sections that follow is designed for a team: a formation facilitator, a research coordinator, a group of participants willing to be observed, and a set of external witnesses who can see what those on the inside cannot. That is not a research design constraint. That is the LotS project being true to itself.

> Discussion Questions for Small Groups

**Section 2 Discussion Questions**

**1. **The LotS project draws from four sources: scripture, reason, experience, and community. Which of the four do you personally lean on most heavily? Which do you most neglect? What would it look like to bring them into better balance in your own spiritual exploration?

**2. **The author argues that the Holy Spirit should be treated as a foundational presupposition of the research, not a confounding variable to be explained away. How do you respond to that framing? Does it seem like intellectual honesty or intellectual evasion?

**3. **Construct validity requires that we be precise about what we mean by "heart formation" or "trust in scripture." How would you define one of these constructs in your own words — precisely enough that someone who did not know you could use your definition to observe it in another person's life?

**4. **The author argues that researchers of the spiritual life need to be on the journey themselves — that you cannot study spiritual formation from the outside. Do you agree? What are the advantages and risks of that posture?

**5. **Sections 1 and 2 together lay the epistemological foundation for the testing program. Before moving to the specific protocols in Sections 3–5, what questions do you most want those protocols to answer? What would you most need to see — or not see — in the data, for the LotS framework to earn your trust?
