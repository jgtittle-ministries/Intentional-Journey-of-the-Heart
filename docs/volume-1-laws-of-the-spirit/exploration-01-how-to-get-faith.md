# First Exploration: How to Get Faith


![](images/image-014.jpeg)

## The Starting Point: Rom. 10:17
This is where I started the explorations, and it is still the clearest single operational law I have found in scripture. Rom. 10:17 is a genuine if-then chain with a defined input, a defined output, and a specified mechanism connecting them.

***Rom. 10:17 (ESV)***

*"So faith comes from hearing, and hearing through the word of Christ."*

Let me state this as a law with the causal arrows explicit: the Word of Christ is the input. Hearing — genuine, attentive, and willing-to-obey hearing is the conversion mechanism. Faith (which I define throughout as trust, the Greek pistis) is the output. The chain runs in one direction: Word → Hearing → Faith. You cannot get to genuine trust by skipping the hearing, and you cannot get to the hearing by ignoring the Word.

## What "Hearing" Actually Means
The Greek in Romans 10 distinguishes between akouo (the physical act of perceiving sound) and the kind of hearing that produces faith. I have come to understand the conversion mechanism this way: a personal word to me about or concerning Christ, when I hear it with a genuine willingness to obey, willing to change, will increase my trust level (faith) and result in changed actions. This is rhema (a living word spoken to me in this moment) rather than logos (the general, permanent, unchanging Word of God), though the rhema always comes through or aligns with the logos.

A word of caution about the rhema/logos distinction I am using here: Grudem rightly notes in Systematic Theology that the Greek words themselves are not used with this theological consistency in the New Testament — both words appear for both the written Word and personal divine speech, with no clear pattern supporting a firm categorical distinction. My use of the distinction is functional and experiential rather than lexical: I am describing a difference in how the Word arrives and operates — the general scriptural text versus the specific personal application of it to this moment — not a claim about two categorically different kinds of divine speech or two tiers of divine authority. A word heard in quiet time that feels personally addressed is not a different kind of word from scripture; it is scripture landing with personal specificity. Grudem’s correction is a useful guardrail: if you find yourself treating rhema impressions as having independent authority from scripture, the distinction has been pushed further than it should go, and you are headed into dangerous territory.

The implied converse is equally important: if I hear but am not willing to obey, my heart can grow harder rather than softer. This is the parable of the Sower read as a feedback law, my response to the word I have heard determines the soil condition for the next word. Disobedience is not neutral; it degrades the channel.

## The Feedback Loop
What emerges from this exploration is not just a one-way chain but a feedback system. Faith, once generated, makes me more willing to hear, which generates more faith. Conversely, hardness of heart suppresses hearing, which prevents faith from growing, which produces more hardness. This is a reinforcing loop running in both directions. The direction it runs depends largely on my willingness to obey what I have already heard.

There is one more dimension of this worth naming, because it has changed how I pray with people. Dmitri Bilgere, drawing on years of men’s group facilitation, made an observation that I think is simply true: the moment of transformation is not when the tears come or the anger breaks. It is the moment when you allow yourself to risk believing that what the Spirit has been saying to you is actually real.

That sounds obvious until you sit with it. Think about what the Word → Hearing → Faith chain actually feels like from the inside. The Word arrives. Something in you responds — there is a flicker, a recognition. But then, at exactly the point where trust would be the next move, something in you holds back. You are still managing the risk. What Bilgere observed is that the actual transition point — the moment the chain completes and faith is genuinely generated — is the moment you drop that management and let the word land as true. Not intellectual assent. Not emotional performance. Just: I actually believe this, right now, for this moment.

The process itself — whether it is the “Any Doubts?” exercise, a TPM session, or quiet time with scripture — does not give you faith. It creates the conditions in which you are finally willing to risk believing what was already true. The faith was available; you were holding back from it. The obedience condition I described above is partly about this: the willingness to obey is the willingness to believe that what you heard was real enough to act on. That willingness is the threshold.

Here is the practical application: when the faith chain is stalled, the question is not “what more information do I need?” It is “what am I not yet willing to believe is true?” That is the door. The Spirit is standing at it.

I connect this to the new Sixth Exploration (the Obedience Channel): obedience to the last word I received is the condition for the channel to be open to receive the next word. The two explorations are closely linked.

**Proposed Law (Operational): Faith (trust) is generated by hearing the Word of Christ with a genuine willingness to obey. The quality of hearing is modulated by prior obedience. Obedience keeps the channel open and reinforces the loop; disobedience degrades the channel and initiates a hardening process.**
