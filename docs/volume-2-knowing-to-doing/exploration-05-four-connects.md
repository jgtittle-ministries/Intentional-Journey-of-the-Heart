# Fifth Exploration: The Four Connects — A Causal Sequence


![](images/image-009.jpeg)

## Not a Workshop Label — A Logical Architecture
The Four Connects — Connecting with Self, Connecting with Others, Connecting with God, Connecting with Mission — came out of the Band of Brothers work I have been doing with men for many years. We used it as an organizing framework for workshops and small group sessions. What I want to do here is elevate it from an organizational label to what I now understand it actually is: a causal sequence with directional dependencies that matter. The four connects come from the two most important commandments as Jesus highlighted (Matt. 22:37-39).

The sequence is not arbitrary. I cannot genuinely connect with Others if I do not know myself — if I have not done the interior work of Exploration 1 and 2 and understand what is going on inside me. I cannot genuinely connect with God through mere religious activity if I am not honest with myself and am carrying uncleared blockages. And I cannot find and live my Mission unless I am genuinely connected with all three of the above. The dependencies run in one direction: Self precedes Others; Others and Self together create the conditions for genuine Connection with God; and all three together make Mission sustainable rather than performative.

## Connecting with Self

![](images/image-004.png)

The starting point is always honest self-observation. "How converted is my heart?" is the diagnostic question I keep returning to, because it refuses the religious short-circuit of treating profession as equivalent to transformation. The heart is the soil. The state of the soil determines what the Word can produce. And I have a hard time addressing a soil condition I have not accurately named.

The tools for connecting with Self include: telling my story (chronologically first, then spiritually, then in terms of wounds and battles and victories), the Garden of Your Heart exercise which I will describe fully in Exploration 7, the Loss Graph from Grief Recover Handbook (GRH), the “Who Are You?” process, and the Footprints of God exercise that maps where I can see God’s hand in my life history looking backward.

Let me expand on the story-telling piece, because I have come to believe it is more important than its position on that list suggests.

Dave Smith and our Band of Brothers developed a practice of telling our stories to each other — not just once, but repeatedly. The full version would take thirty minutes or more. The ten-minute version, told as though to someone who has never heard it before, is the more instructive exercise, because you discover what you lead with when you only have time for what matters most. Dave identified four questions that give the story its shape: Where are the footprints of God in this life? Where are the wounds? Where are the battles? Where have there been victories and losses? These four questions pull a chronology out of the realm of autobiography and into the realm of spiritual narrative. The same events look different under each question.

What surprised us — and this is still true for me every time I do it — is that the story comes out differently each retelling. Not wrong the last time; differently. What you lead with shifts with where you are spiritually and what you are currently being asked to see. The story seems to know what you need to hear before you do. One of our brothers said it well: something new always shows up.

The implication for Connecting with Self is that storytelling is not the opening activity before the real work begins. It is the beginning of the self-connection work, and it is never finished. A man who has told his story a dozen times in a group he trusts knows himself in a qualitatively different way from a man who has told it only once to a questionnaire. The story told in community is tested, heard, reflected back, and clarified. The story told alone stays inside the assumptions you already have. This is one reason I pushed back earlier in this volume against the individualistic reading of spiritual formation — the self you are connecting with is partly constructed through being witnessed by others. You cannot see all of yourself from inside yourself.

***Ps. 139:23-24 (ESV)***

*"Search me, O God, and know my heart! Try me and know my thoughts! And see if there **be** any grievous way in **me, and** lead me in the way everlasting."*

This is the invitation to God to do the diagnostic work of Connecting with Self on my behalf — because my self-perception is limited and distorted. The prayer is for God’s search to find what I cannot or will not find on my own. This is exactly what TPM facilitates: I identify the presenting emotion; the Spirit identifies the root.

## Connecting with Others
Genuine connection with others requires two things that are in tension: enough safety to be honest, and enough accountability to be corrected. The container protocol (Exploration 6) is the practical implementation of the safety side. The community law (Exploration 7) is the practical implementation of the accountability side.

What I have seen in the Band of Brothers work is that men who are genuinely connected with at least a few other men who know their story are measurably more able to hear God, more resilient under pressure, and more fruitful in their mission than men who are doing the journey alone. This is not a sociological observation; it is a spiritual law. We are designed for community. Isolation is not just uncomfortable; it is a condition that degrades spiritual hearing.

***Eccl. 4:12 (ESV)***

*"And though a man might prevail against one who is alone, two will withstand him—a threefold cord is not quickly broken."*

## Connecting with God
This is the center of everything — but it comes third in the sequence for a reason. Connecting with Self and with Others clears the ground. A man who has not done honest self-examination and does not have at least one person who knows his actual interior life tends to perform his connection with God rather than living it. The performance looks like a genuine connection, but does not bear the scriptural harvest fullness.

The "Any Doubts?" process (more fully developed in Exploration 7) is designed to address the gap between professed belief and actual trust. The premise is that I may be saying I believe a scripture while simultaneously holding a doubt about it that I have never named or examined. Until that doubt is named and brought to the Spirit, it operates as an underground brake on the faith that scripture should produce.

**ELDREDGE CONNECTION — The Question at the Center: **John Eldredge argues in Wild at Heart (2001, pp. 46–52) that every man carries a core question — most often some version of Do I have what it takes? — and that this question is the primary target of both the wound and the enemy’s long campaign against the heart. The God-Connect is the only place that question gets a final answer, because the Father is the only one with the authority to give it.

** **This maps precisely onto what I observe in Band of Brothers work. A man can connect with Self and connect with Others and still find that the God-Connect remains strangely formal — correct, even sincere, but not settled in the way the sequence requires before Mission becomes sustainable. When I probe that flatness, I almost always find an unanswered question underneath: something the man has never actually brought to the Father directly, because bringing it feels too risky, or because he doesn’t really believe the Father will answer it, or because he has answered it himself (negatively) and stopped asking.

** **The practical work of the God-Connect includes exactly this: naming the question out loud — in prayer, in community — and then sitting with the Father long enough to hear His specific answer to it. Not the doctrinal answer (which the man already knows). The personal answer. Isaiah 43:1 is the template: I have called you by name, you are mine. That is the Father answering the question — not in a category, but to a person.

** **Eldredge’s Waking the Dead (2003, pp. 37–50) adds the complementary point: the enemy’s strategy is not primarily to prevent this conversation from happening but to convince the man that if he brings the question honestly, what he will hear from the Father is confirmation of the wound’s verdict. This is the lie underneath the flatness of many men’s prayer lives. The God-Connect work is, at its core, the willingness to bring the question anyway, and to trust that what comes back will be better than the verdict the wound installed.

## Connecting with Mission
Mission is not a strategy I adopt; it is something I discover through the first three connects. My Mission is the unique intersection of my gifts, my wounds, my history with God, and the needs of the world around me. Satan tries to wound me in the very place I have been gifted so that I will not live out that gifting. If I am willing to go into that wounding, I can find the gold that the Father has for me. You cannot find it by thinking harder; you find it by knowing yourself, being known by others, and hearing from God what He has prepared for you specifically.

***Eph. 2:10 (ESV)***

*"For we are his workmanship, created in Christ Jesus for good works, which God prepared beforehand, that we should walk in them."*

The good works were prepared beforehand. They exist. My job is not to invent them but to discover and walk into them — which requires enough self-knowledge, relational honesty, and spiritual hearing to recognize them when they appear.

Let me add a distinction here that I wish I’d had earlier in my journey, because I wasted a lot of energy not having it. Dmitri Bilgere, who has done significant work in men’s formation, describes two kinds of mission: uphill and downhill.

Your uphill mission is what you do. It has a goal, a plan, and measurable steps. “I want to help men get unstuck in their spiritual lives, so I’m going to build a small group process and train facilitators.” That’s an uphill mission — important, real work, and it requires effort to climb toward. Most of the mission language in Christian circles is uphill language.

Your downhill mission is what you are. It’s the effect you have on people simply by being fully present and fully yourself. When you are most aligned with the Spirit and most genuinely who God made you to be, what happens in the room? What do people get to be when you show up that way? That is your downhill mission, and it flows — it doesn’t have to be pushed.

I notice this distinction is actually embedded in the passage I’ve been sitting with for decades: “For we are his workmanship, created in Christ Jesus for good works, which God prepared beforehand, that we should walk in them” (Eph. 2:10 ESV). The works were prepared. We walk in them — not strain toward them, not engineer them. Walking in what’s already prepared sounds a lot more like downhill than uphill. The uphill work is clearing out the blockages so the downhill becomes possible.

Here’s a practical question to sit with, when you are at your most alive and your most genuinely yourself — not performing, not managing your image, just present and open — what do the people around you get to be? What does the room feel like? The answer to that is probably closer to your downhill mission than any mission statement you’ve labored over. Both matter. But the downhill is the one that flows from who you are rather than what you do, and it is what sustains the uphill when the climbing gets hard.

One more thing about mission, and this one is uncomfortable enough that I almost left it out. Almost. Every genuine calling has a shadow mission — its exact dark mirror. The shadow mission wastes the same gifts your real mission would deploy, absorbs the same energy, and sends it in the opposite direction. It is where you live when you are not being intentional. And it has a deep emotional charge, which is actually how you can recognize it: when someone describes your shadow mission to you accurately, something in you knows it immediately, even though you have probably never said it out loud.

My calling, for as long as I can remember, has been to help men walk more deeply with God — to take what I’ve been given and pass it on. My shadow mission, if I’m honest, is to control and manipulate others for my own selfish ends and be recognized as someone who knows things. The gifts are the same. The direction is opposite. One serves; one performs. One builds the kingdom; one builds the resume.

The reason this matters in the context of the Four Connects is that a man who is living his shadow mission can feel like he is living his calling. The energy feels real. The drive feels real. It can take years — and sometimes a good brother who will say the uncomfortable thing — to see the difference.

Here is the test: does the mission go outward, serving people beyond yourself and your immediate circle? Or does it draw inward, collecting people in service of something that ultimately serves you? The real mission goes out. The shadow mission draws in. Both can feel, from the inside, like passion. Ask the brothers who know you well which one they’re watching.

**Proposed Law (Structural): Connecting with Self, Others, God, and Mission ****is**** a causal sequence with directional dependencies. Each layer creates the conditions for the next. Attempts to connect with God while bypassing honest self-examination and genuine community tend to produce performance rather than transformation. Attempts to find and live a mission without the first three connections tend to produce activity without fruit.**

**Certainty: 80****%  ***The** sequence and its dependencies are confirmed by observation over many years of working with men. The specific mechanisms are still being developed — this is the area where the community dimension of **IJH** testing will be most valuable.*

Connecting with Self requires **at** minimum SST soul **Level** 1 (Awareness of Soul-**Disorder) —** a person who has not yet honestly named their interior fragmentation cannot connect with self in the sense this law intends. Connecting with God requires SST spirit Stage 2+ (Learning to Follow the Spirit) as a developmental prerequisite — not merely the initial regeneration of Stage 1 but the developing discipline of recognizing and following the Spirit’s lead. The sequence in this law and the developmental architecture in SST **are** describing the same reality from different angles: SST explains why the dependencies in this law exist.*
