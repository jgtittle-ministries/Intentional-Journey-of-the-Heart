# Tenth Exploration: The Training Plan — Operating in Faith as a Learnable Skill


![](images/image-003.jpeg)

## Why a Training Plan Is Not Unspiritual
There is a kind of piety that resists the idea of training, as though the Holy Spirit’s gifts are too sacred to be practiced deliberately. I have come to believe this resistance is itself a subtle form of unbelief. Every human faculty that develops — musical skill, athletic ability, mathematical reasoning, surgical precision — develops through intentional, focused practice. Why would the spiritual life be different? Jesus Himself told His disciples what to do, showed them how to do it, sent them out to practice it, and debriefed them when they came back (Luke 10:1-20). That is a training sequence, not just an inspirational experience.

***1 Tim. 4:7-8 (ESV)***

*"...train yourself for godliness; for while bodily training is of some value, godliness is of value in every way, as it holds promise for the present life and also for the life to come."*

Paul uses the athletic training metaphor deliberately. Train yourself — gumnaze, the same root as gymnasium. This is intentional, repeated, progressive practice. The result, godliness, is not a state I arrive at through insight; it is a capacity I develop through training. The writer of Hebrews 12 recognizes that training is painful by using the Greek word for agony.

## The Components of a Sustainable Training Plan
Based on everything I have developed in this volume, here is a starting draft of what a sustainable training plan for operating in the Laws of the Spirit might look like:

### Daily: The Hearing Practice
- PROAPT scripture engagement — minimum fifteen minutes, ideally thirty. The goal is not to cover ground but to actually hear. A single verse that I genuinely hear is worth more than three chapters that I read for coverage.
- Morning attentiveness — the practice of asking, before the day begins, "Father, what are You up to today? Where will I see You? What do You want me to do or be for someone today?" And then paying attention to the answers as the day unfolds.
- Evening review — a brief backward look: Where did I see God today? Where did I miss Him? Where did I act in alignment with what I heard, and where did I not? The evening review develops the pattern recognition that the Footprints exercise trains retrospectively.
### Weekly: The Community Practice
- A genuine Band of Brothers — a small group (three to eight men) who know each other’s full story, who meet with a real container established, who bring real interior material to each other. Not a Bible study group that occasionally gets personal. A genuine community of hearing.
- Shared reporting — at each meeting, each man reports what he heard since the last meeting, what he acted on, and what the results were. This is the accountability structure that turns the obedience channel law from theory into practice. Knowing that I will report my hearing to the brothers next week significantly increases both the quality of my hearing and the likelihood that I will obey what I hear.
One more element of the weekly practice that I have found to be irreplaceable: how the meeting ends matters as much as how it begins.

In our Band of Brothers work, Dave Smith built a closing practice into every session. Before the container closes, each man briefly states what is happening for him right now — not a summary of what was discussed, but what he is feeling in this moment as the meeting ends. This is the same emotional check-in that opened the container, now run in reverse. It grounds the work in the body and surfaces what the session actually did for each man, which is not always what the facilitator planned.

After that, any man who has a blessing to speak over another man does so. Not a prayer for him in general — a specific, witnessed blessing: what you saw happen in that man tonight, or what the Father seemed to be saying about him during the session, spoken directly to him out loud. These are usually short. They are almost always powerful, especially for men who have rarely or never heard another man speak life over them with specificity and authority.

I want to be clear about this: I have seen grown men undone by a thirty-second blessing spoken by a brother who watched them do hard work. Not because the words were eloquent, but because they were specific, true, and given in the context of a community that had just been in the room while the work happened. That kind of blessing carries weight that general encouragement does not. It is the voice of witnessed transformation — you were here, you saw what God did, and now you are naming it back to the man it happened in. That is a spiritual function that almost nothing else in the ordinary Christian community does.

For the training plan, this means: do not let the weekly meeting end when the last process ends. End it when the last blessing has been spoken, and each man goes out carrying what his brothers saw in him that evening.

### Monthly: The Clearing Practice
- A dedicated session for whatever heart maintenance is needed — which might be a GRH completion letter, a TPM session with a trusted partner, a confession to a brother, a Garden of Your Heart encounter. The frequency of monthly is a minimum; some seasons of life require more frequent clearing, particularly during periods of loss, transition, or significant spiritual warfare.
- A review of the current state of each of the Four Connects — am I genuinely connected with Self, with Others, with God, with Mission? Where is the weakest link? What does that weak link need?
### Seasonally: The Deeper Work
- A retreat or extended time — a Wild at Heart Boot Camp, a contemplative retreat, an extended prayer time — that goes deeper than the regular practices can reach. Some layers of knot and soil condition are only accessible in sustained, extended time away from the ordinary demands of life.
- A review of mission clarity — am I still operating in the good works prepared for me? Has the mission shifted? What does the Spirit seem to be calling me into next?
## The Training Mentor
Every serious training program requires a reference point who is further along than you are. For me, that reference point is Jesus. The most productive direction I have found for deepening the training plan is to study Jesus’ ministry with the specific question: what spiritual laws is He operating in here? What is He discerning? How is He responding to what He discerns? The gospels, read through this lens, become not just an inspirational biography but an advanced textbook in the operation of the Laws of the Spirit.

I have had spiritual directors at various points in my life, and each brought me deeper into what God had for me. Spiritual direction is a lost art in the Protestant community. I have had to search hard to find one and went for years in some cases between findings. I have found Nemeck and Combs’ “The Way of Spiritual Direction” to be a great reference for what it should look like and how to recognize it. Vol 5 defines a Formation Companion that integrates multiple mentoring roles that are applicable to our context. I commend that to your perusal if interested.

John 5:19 ESV: “So Jesus said to them, ‘Truly, truly, I say to you, the Son can do nothing of his own accord, but only what he sees the Father doing. For whatever the Father does, that the Son does likewise.’” This is Jesus’ own description of His training plan: attentive observation of the Father, immediate response to what He observes. That is the advanced version of the daily practice I described above.

## Preaching and Worship as Complementary Interventions
Two corporate disciplines deserve specific attention in the training plan because they are often treated as separate activities serving separate purposes, when in fact they are designed to work together as a unit. My research in Christian education identifies them as targeting two different but complementary domains of the same hearing journey.

Preaching primarily targets the cognitive domain. Its goal is to present the Word accurately — to show who God is, what He has done, what He is doing, and what He calls us to. In terms of the Affective Taxonomy developed in Exploration 7, preaching supports Levels 1 and 2: creating awareness and inviting active engagement with the Word. It builds the mental model that frames what is of genuine value. As Harry Shields puts it, the preacher who wants to produce transformation must handle the Word carefully enough to have high confidence that what they are saying is actually what God is saying. The preacher’s own formation is upstream of the congregation’s.

Worship targets the affective domain. Its goal is to move the heart to desire what the mind has been shown. James Smith (2009) identifies rightly-structured worship as the primary mechanism by which people are trained to desire the Kingdom of God — not just to understand it, but to want it. In taxonomy terms, worship specifically supports the critical Level 2-to-3 transition: the shift from responding to external prompts toward intrinsically valuing what God values. The heart that has been genuinely moved in worship is more receptive to hearing at Level 3 and above.

The practical sequencing implication: preaching and worship are most effective when treated as a unit, in sequence. The Word shows what is true and worth desiring; worship is the response that says, “I want this,” and trains the heart to mean it. A community with strong preaching but thin worship tends to produce disciples who know the right things but do not deeply desire them — Level 2 stuck. A community with emotionally powerful worship but shallow preaching tends to produce disciples who feel deeply but are not anchored to the Word — affective without cognitive grounding. Steve Lowe observes that most churches get the order wrong and start with worship before the preaching of the Word; the hearing-first principle of Exploration 1 (Rom. 10:17) suggests why the order matters.

## Suffering as Engine, Not Obstacle
Any honest training plan must account for what happens when the plan is disrupted by suffering. Loss, betrayal, illness, failure, the inexplicable silence of God — these are not interruptions of the spiritual journey. In many cases, they can be a primary engine of creating increasing depth. In terms of the Sower parable, suffering might be considered fertilizer to enrich the soil for future harvest.

Walter Brueggemann’s framework for reading the Psalms identifies three phases: orientation (life is good, praise comes easily), disorientation (things have gone wrong in ways that do not make sense), and reorientation (God meets the person in the disorientation, and a deeper understanding emerges). The Psalms of Lament are addressed to God, not away from Him. The lament is itself an act of faith: I am bringing this to You because I still believe You are the One to bring it to.

Saint John of the Cross describes the Dark Night of the Soul — a period in which all the usual consolations of the spiritual life go dry. Prayer feels empty. Scripture feels flat. John’s observation: this is not punishment but the Spirit stripping away dependence on spiritual consolations so that the faith can be rebuilt on a deeper foundation of intimate connection. The person who stays in the darkness, continues the practices, and refuses to abandon the community will emerge into a quieter and more stable relationship with God than they had before.

In terms of the Affective Taxonomy, what looks like regression during the Dark Night — a Level 4 person apparently back at Level 1, questioning everything — is often the process of moving from Level 4 to Level 5. The internalization is going deeper, not shallower. The soil is being turned more deeply so that roots can go further down. Secular research on posttraumatic growth (Tedeschi & Calhoun) confirms this pattern: the struggle with highly challenging life crises is associated with increased personal strength, changed priorities, and richer spiritual life. The taxonomy, read this way, expects a spiral path rather than a linear one.

The training plan implication: when suffering arrives, first diagnose before applying tools. Is it the consequence of sin (Confession-Restoration, Exploration 4)? A weight-consuming bandwidth (Knot-clearing, Explorations 2-3)? Or the disorientation of a deeper orientation forming — the Dark Night? The diagnostic key is whether the person is turning toward God in the suffering, even in anger or lament? Lament addressed to God is Brueggemann’s disorientation, an engine. Bitterness that closes the door to God is the thorny soil choking the root, and requires the clearing tools of Part I. Applying the wrong tool to the wrong suffering will slow rather than help.

**Proposed Law (Developmental): Operating in the Laws of the Spirit is a learnable skill that develops through intentional, progressive practice across three domains that must all be engaged: cognitive (understanding what is true), affective (developing genuine desire for what is true), and action (habitually doing what has been understood and desired). Preaching primarily develops the cognitive domain; worship develops the affective domain; they are most effective in sequence. The training ****plan suggests**** four timeframes (daily, weekly, monthly, seasonal) and is a system — removing any component degrades the whole. Suffering is not an interruption of the training plan but a potential engine of depth; the diagnostic is whether the suffering drives the person toward God or away from Him.**

**Certainty: 75% ***The three-domain framework (cognitive, affective, and action) is supported by doctoral research applying all three of Bloom’s taxonomy families to spiritual formation. The preaching-worship sequencing insight is from doctoral research in Christian education (Smith, 2009) and is consistent with the hearing-first principle of Exploration 1. The suffering-as-engine insight is consistent with Brueggemann, John of the Cross, and posttraumatic growth research. The specific timeframes remain a working proposal, not **a **scriptural prescription.*
