# Seventh Exploration: Hearing with Understanding — The Experiential Processes


![](images/image-011.jpeg)

## The Goal of Part II
All the clearing work in Explorations 1 through 6 is in service of one thing: a heart that is open enough to hear. But hearing is also a skill — a faculty that develops with practice, that requires specific exercises to grow, and that can remain underdeveloped even in a person whose channel is relatively clear. This exploration describes the primary practices for developing the hearing faculty itself.

## The "Any Doubts?" Process
This is one of the most powerful hearing-development exercises I know, because it targets the specific mechanism by which intellectual assent blocks genuine trust. I may be saying I believe a scripture — "God is good," "I am loved," "He will provide" — while simultaneously holding a doubt about it that I have never examined. That doubt is a below-the-surface brake on the faith that scripture should produce.

The process: a facilitator reads a scripture to the person processing. The person is invited to close their eyes, receive the scripture, and notice any resistance, tension, or doubt that arises. The facilitator asks: "Any doubts?" If yes, the person names the doubt. The facilitator invites them to exaggerate the doubt — make it bigger, louder, more present. Then: "What feeling do you experience with that doubt?" The person names the feeling and dwells in it until they reach the peak of the emotion. Then the scripture is read again. The cycle repeats until the doubt is gone and the scripture can be received without resistance.

What is happening theologically: the doubt is an emotional knot attached to a specific truth-claim. By bringing it fully into awareness rather than suppressing it, I give the Spirit access to it. The exaggeration step is counterintuitive but important — it moves the doubt from a background hum to a foreground reality that can be addressed. The repeated reading of the scripture after the emotional peak creates the conditions for the Spirit to speak the truth of that specific scripture into the specific place where the doubt was held.

***Mark 9:24 (ESV)***

*"Immediately the father of the child cried out and said, 'I believe; help my unbelief!'"*

This is the model. The father did not pretend that the unbelief was not there. He named it, brought it to Jesus, and asked for help with it. That is the "Any Doubts?" process in one sentence. The unbelief that is named and brought to Jesus is the unbelief that can be released. The unbelief that is suppressed out of spiritual performance pressure continues to operate as a brake.

A word about the facilitator’s interior job across all of these processes, because I have seen facilitation succeed and fail on exactly this point. Dave Smith describes it well: the facilitator in a process is listening to two voices at the same time. One is the man in front of him — his words, his body language, where the energy is high and where it goes flat. The other is the Holy Spirit — the quiet prompting that says “stay here,” or “ask him about that,” or “just wait.” The facilitator who is only tracking the man will follow him wherever he goes, including down the well-worn detours that feel productive but don’t reach the root. The facilitator who is only listening inward, without genuine attention to the man in front of him, gives beautiful answers to questions nobody asked.

The dual listening is itself an act of faith on the facilitator’s part. It requires staying genuinely open to hearing something unplanned, which means not coming in with a fixed agenda about where the session needs to go. The Word → Hearing → Faith chain applies to the facilitator just as much as to the person being facilitated. He is, in real terms, in the same position: dependent on the Spirit to speak, and dependent on his own willingness to hear it.

This is why the container conditions matter as much for the facilitator as for anyone else in the room. If he is carrying unaddressed material or has a strong preference about the outcome, his hearing is degraded before the work begins. He will hear what he expects or what he wants, not what the Spirit is actually saying. Getting clear before facilitating is not a courtesy — it is the prerequisite.

## The Garden of Your Heart
This exercise is both a hearing practice and a diagnostic tool. It creates a structured encounter with the Holy Spirit in the interior space of the heart. Here is the full process:

**The Garden of Your Heart — Full Process**

1.  Prepare: Have paper and pen available. Create an environment where participants can be genuinely relaxed and undistracted.

2.  Relax: Guide participants through progressive relaxation, starting at the toes and working upward. The goal is not sleep but a present, alert, quiet state. Breath meditation — focused attention on the in-and-out of the breath — helps anchor the present moment.

3.  Picture: Ask participants to picture their heart as a garden. Let this sit for several minutes. Whatever appears — wild, overgrown, cultivated, barren, seasonal — is information. There is no wrong garden.

4.  Record: Invite participants back to the present moment to record what they saw, heard, smelled, or felt in the garden. The specific sensory details matter; they often carry diagnostic meaning.

5.  Invite: Return to the garden in the same relaxed state. This time, invite Jesus into the garden. Suggest they ask Him to do or say whatever He wants to. Let this sit for several minutes.

6.  Record again: Return to the present moment and record what happened — what Jesus said or did, where He went, what He touched, what He left unchanged.

7.  Share (optional): In a group context, invite anyone who wants to share their experience. The sharing itself often opens additional revelation.

I have run this exercise dozens of times with different groups of men, and the consistency of the results is one reason my confidence in it is relatively high. The garden consistently reveals something the person did not consciously know about their interior state. Jesus’ interaction with the garden is almost always specific, personal, and unexpected. The element of surprise is itself a sign that the Spirit is doing something beyond the person’s projections. I always participate at the same time and have something new and developmental happen in each iteration. I understand this is my heart developing and changing. Such an encouragement when I really don’t notice any changes cognitively.

## The Footprints of God Exercise
This is a backward-looking hearing exercise: instead of asking "what is God saying now?" it asks "where can I see God’s hand in my life story looking backward?" This is important because the ability to recognize God’s activity in the past trains the pattern-recognition faculty that learns to see His activity in the present.

The questions: Where were the turning points in my life that I did not engineer? What were the gifts I did not ask for that arrived at exactly the right moment? Where did something that seemed like a disaster turn into something good? Where was I protected from something I did not know was threatening me? The answers to these questions are a map of God’s personal attention to my specific life. And a person who can see that map tends to have an easier time trusting that the same attention is present now.

***Lam. 3:22-23 (ESV)***

*"The steadfast love of the Lord never ceases; his mercies never come to an end; they are new every morning; great is your faithfulness."*

Looking backward at the footprints is how this truth moves from a doctrine I assent to into a personal reality I have seen. The faithfulness is not abstract; it has specific instances in my specific story.

## PROAPT: The Systematic Hearing of Scripture
A theological question worth addressing directly: what is the relationship between what I am calling ‘personal hearing’ in this framework and the spiritual gifts Paul describes in 1 Corinthians 12–14? Grudem’s treatment in The Gift of Prophecy is helpful here. He distinguishes between Old Testament prophecy — authoritative, infallible, ‘Thus says the Lord’ — and New Testament congregational prophecy, which he describes as Spirit-prompted impressions received and shared in community, subject to testing and weighing rather than immediate acceptance. On Grudem’s account, this kind of receptive hearing is not a specialized gift reserved for a few; it is the normal Spirit-filled life available to every believer in varying degrees of clarity and frequency. I believe PROAPT’s hearing practice operates in this register: not as the reception of new authoritative revelation alongside scripture, but as the Spirit’s personal application of scriptural truth to the specific person in the specific moment. Understanding this prevents two errors: the error of treating personal impressions as authoritative pronouncements, and the error of dismissing the possibility of personal Spirit-speech as something the New Testament no longer supports.

Carolyn and I were taught this at Truro Church in the 1980s and it has stayed with me as one of the most systematic approaches to developing the hearing faculty through scripture study, in particular in a small group setting. The acronym is: Pray, Read, Observe, Apply, Pray again, Tell.

- Pray: Before reading, invite the Spirit to speak. This is not a formality; it is the acknowledgment that I am approaching the Word as someone who expects to hear from it, not merely to extract information from it.
- Read: Read the passage. Not to analyze it immediately — just to receive it. Let it land.
- Observe: What do I actually notice? What stands out? What is strange or unexpected? What do I not understand? Honest observation precedes interpretation.
- Apply: What does this mean for me specifically, today, in my specific situation? This is where the logos becomes rhema — where the general word of scripture becomes a specific word to me. (For the important caution about how I am using the rhema/logos distinction — functionally rather than lexically — see Vol 1, Exploration 1.)
- Pray again: Bring the application to God. Tell Him what you heard, what you are willing to do, what you are struggling to be willing to do. This is where the obedience channel opens.
- Tell: Share what you heard with someone — a spouse, a brother, a trusted friend. The speaking of it solidifies it and creates accountability.
The Tell step is the one most often omitted and the one most important for long-term development. A word heard and spoken to another person has a different staying power than a word heard and kept private. The speaking is an act of faith that the word was real.

## The Hearing Journey as Affective Taxonomy: A Formal Map
Jesus said something remarkable in explaining the Sower parable to His disciples: “Do you not understand this parable? How then will you understand all the parables?” (Mark 4:13). He was identifying it as the meta-discipline — the foundational pattern that unlocks all the others. If that is right, then having a formal map of the soil progression matters more than we usually recognize.

The Affective Taxonomy (Krathwohl, Bloom, and Masia, 1964) provides that formal map. My doctoral research in Christian education has proposed this taxonomy as a defensible framework for describing the observable human side of spiritual formation in the area of hearing and obeying. The compressed research paper is here: A Model of Spiritual Formation for Individuals and Small Groups (MSFIG) -

Here,  in summary, is how the individual stages apply to a person’s journey in learning to hear and obey God:

### Level 1 — Receiving
Bare awareness; willingness to attend. A person at this level is willing to show up, listen, read the passage, and participate at a surface level. Motivation is entirely external. They are at a bible study because someone invited them, or because it fits their schedule, not because they have yet found value in the Word itself. This is the hard path that is on the verge of becoming receptive soil. The diagnostic indicator: they attend but rarely initiate. They listen but rarely bring anything from their own reflection.

### Level 2 — Responding
Active engagement; following through when prompted. The Level 2 person participates in discussion, completes assigned readings, and prays when the group prays. But the motivation is still largely external — social expectations, accountability, and the group's rhythm. Rocky soil territory. The roots are beginning to form, but they have not yet gone deep enough to produce fruit in private, unsupported contexts. The diagnostic indicator: active in the group context, but the hearing and praying largely stop when the external structure stops.

### Level 3 — Valuing (The Critical Transition)
This is the transition from extrinsic to intrinsic motivation — the shift from “I hear God’s word because the group expects it” to “I hear God’s word because I have discovered that it is worth my time and is changing my life.” A Level 3 person reads scripture privately, not because a study guide requires it. They pray on their own. They bring a genuine question to the group, not a curriculum question. This is the floor of good soil, the 30-fold harvest. The diagnostic indicator: consistent private practice, not just group practice.

### Level 4 — Organization
The word is integrated into a coherent value system. Faith now informs vocation, finances, relationships, and daily decisions in a systemic way. A Level 4 person brings their real decisions to God and to the group for genuine discernment, not just prayer support. They are developing what Barna (2003) calls a biblical worldview — not as an intellectual construct but as a governing framework for every domain. The 60-fold harvest. The diagnostic indicator: consistent behavioral evidence in multiple life domains that the Word is governing, not just informing.

### Level 5 — Characterization
Hearing and obeying have determined and controlled behavior for so long that they have become a defining characteristic of the person’s identity. They do not deliberate about whether to obey; they obey as a matter of who they are. The Word has become, as Dallas Willard puts it, not just something they study but the medium through which they live. This is the 100-fold harvest, the mind of Christ. The diagnostic indicator: others describe them primarily in terms of their Christlikeness — it is the first thing you notice about them.

Two cautions: First, progress through these levels is not linear. A Level 4 person who suffers a significant loss may appear to regress to Level 1 or 2 — asking fundamental questions about who God is, why He would allow such a thing. This is not regression; it is what Walter Brueggemann calls the disorientation phase of a deeper orientation. Saint John of the Cross describes this as the Dark Night of the Soul: the person is not losing faith but being stripped of a smaller faith so that a larger one can be formed. The apparent regression is the soil being broken up more deeply so that the roots can go deeper. The external observer who does not understand this will apply Level 1-2 remedies to a person who is actually on the threshold of Level 5.

Second, the Spirit is not constrained by this taxonomy. Sudden, permanent changes of heart are well attested throughout scripture and church history. The taxonomy describes the normal developmental path; the Spirit reserves the right to bypass it.

## The Small Group as a Hearing Community: A Parallel Taxonomy
The same five levels apply to a small group as a collective, not just to an individual. Paul’s instruction assumes this: “When you come together, each one has a hymn, a lesson, a revelation, a tongue, or an interpretation. Let all things be done for building up” (1 Cor. 14:26). A group can be at Level 1 (the leader hears and distributes to passive recipients) or at Level 5 (the group’s culture of hearing and obeying has become so characteristic that every member it has formed now carries that culture into the next community they enter). The five levels for a group:

- Group Level 1: Hearing is mediated through the leader. Members listen. Prayer requests are informational and seldom personal. The group has not yet developed its own collective receptivity.
- Group Level 2: Members begin contributing. The leader asks genuine questions, not curriculum questions. Members build on each other. The dynamics of “each one has” begin to appear.
- Group Level 3: The group trusts each other enough to bring real decisions, not just prayer requests. Corporate listening prayer becomes a practice. The group shares what it hears and compares. Convergences are noted and tested.
- Group Level 4: Individual members’ hearing is naturally submitted to the group for testing. The group’s collective discernment informs individual decisions as a matter of culture, not procedure. The group may hear about itself — its own blind spots and calling.
- Group Level 5: Former members lead new groups that exhibit the same hearing culture, not by following a manual but by having been formed by it. The Level 5 culture is portable. It replicates itself through the people it has formed.
A critical observation: a leader cannot help a group grow to Level 3/4/5 unless the leader has been to at least Level 3 (Internalization) first. The hearing culture is transmitted by presence and formation, not by curriculum.

This same taxonomy is extended to scale at the church-wide level, the denomination level, and, finally, to the one, holy, catholic, and apostolic church in Heart Formation Measurement Theory (HFMT).

**Proposed Law (Developmental): The hearing faculty develops through intentional practice according to a five-stage affective progression (Receiving → Responding → Valuing → Organization → Characterization). The critical transition is from Level 2 to Level 3 — from extrinsic to intrinsic motivation. This same progression applies to small groups as collectives, not just to individuals. The practices in this exploration (Any ****Doubts?,**** Garden of Your Heart, PROAPT) are specifically targeted at supporting the transitions between levels. Progress is spiral, not linear; suffering is a potential engine of depth, not just an obstacle.**

**Certainty: 80****%  ***The** developmental claim — that hearing improves with practice — is confirmed by observation and consistent with the Vol 1 obedience channel law. The specific effectiveness of each exercise varies by person and context; the Any Doubts? process and Garden of Your Heart are the highest-confidence tools from my direct observation. The five-level taxonomy is adapted from doctoral research in Christian education using the Affective Taxonomy (Krathwohl et al., 1964) applied to spiritual formation.*
