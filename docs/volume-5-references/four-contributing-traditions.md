# The Four Contributing Traditions

The four traditions below are offered as contributing streams that inform the Formation Companion role, not as boundaries of what a Companion must or may be. Each has its own history, literature, credentialing infrastructure, and body of practice. The Companion draws on what each offers while remaining grounded in the IJH framework that governs how these contributions integrate.

## Spiritual Direction
Spiritual Direction is the oldest of the four with a lineage reaching back to Ignatius of Loyola and the Jesuit order, the Benedictine order, and the Desert Fathers. Its core posture is attending. The director and the pilgrim recipient listen together for what God is already doing in the pilgrim's life. The director's role does not prescribe or teach but notices, reflects, and invites the pilgrim's deeper attending. Spiritual Director training is typically long, often two to three years in a recognized formation program, often culminating in certification through Spiritual Directors International or a denominational equivalent. The strength of Spiritual Direction is being the most developed tradition focused on contemplative listening and the Companion's own ongoing formation. This tradition synthesizes the default posture of attending before acting, the practice of contemplative silence, and the model of director-having-a-director, i.e., everyone directs, everyone is directed, a form of mutual submission.

## Transformation Prayer Ministry
Transformation Prayer Ministry (TPM), formerly known as Theophostic, is the most protocol-specific of the four. A trained facilitator helps a person identify emotional pain tied to a specific memory, locate the lie and possible vow embedded in that memory, and pray for Jesus to reveal himself and his truth in that memory. That provides a spiritual replacement image, often manifesting as neurologic healing of thoughts and memories. Highly structured, measurable, and reproducible. This is its strength because the process is teachable, making the process consistent across facilitators. However, the process is at risk when applied mechanistically without practiced spiritual discernment. SST's Stage 3.3 describes from the inside what TPM accomplishes as a method. Training is multiplemweeks plus supervised practice, with certification available. What this tradition contributes to the synthesis: a tight protocol for memory-level healing, a clear theology of truth and the real presence of Jesus Christ as the agent of transformation, and a reproducible framework that can be taught to new facilitators without extensive clinical background.

## The Stephen Ministry Tradition
Stephen Ministry is the most widely deployed lay Christian caregiving model in North America, with curricula refined across thousands of congregations since 1975. Fifty hours of initial training, strict confidentiality, referral-based assignment to a care receiver, and a walk-alongside posture for people in grief, crisis, transition, or hardship. The interaction de-emphasizes transformation by the care giver as that is the role Holy Spirit via prayer. The process focusses on reflective listening to establish a faithful Christian presence for someone going through difficulty. Training infrastructure is the most institutionally mature of the four at the Level I tier. What this tradition contributes to the synthesis: the most developed curriculum for training lay people into a defined Christian care role, a robust supervision structure, and a well-tested set of boundaries around confidentiality and referral.

## Group Facilitation (Band of Brothers and Fellowship of the Heart)
The group facilitation tradition that has developed in the Band of Brothers and Fellowship of the Heart contexts is the most group-oriented and tool-diverse of the four. It draws on secular group facilitation theory, the inner healing streams, and Eldredge's framing of formation as mission and calling. This group framework model is strongest at matching process tools or key questions to a specific need expressed by someone in the group. The framework is weakest in established credentialing by Christian sources, though secular certification pathways, such as Shadow Work facilitator certification, provide a credentialed depth. This tradition contributes to synthesis through its capacity to hold the group as a container, a safe place for the confidential, heartfelt exposure of a range of emotions, sins, faults, and joys. The tradition offers a diverse inventory of processes or tools for working into and through these knot-filled aspects and finding release from accompanying harmful wounds and patterns. It also offers an explicit apprentice-to-practitioner progression developed further.

## A Note on the Navigators Tradition
The Navigators tradition, founded by Dawson Trotman in 1933, is less a mode of practitioner role than a method of disciple-making, and so it does not appear as a fifth mode specialization. But two contributions are sufficiently important to be named explicitly. First, its emphasis on scripture memory as a discipline, i.e., the Topical Memory System covers sixty verses across twelve topics, and the discipline is designed to internalize specific scripture so thoroughly that it becomes internalized and load-bearing in daily life. Second is the principle of reproducibility/transferability. Every method, tool, and curriculum is sufficiently simple that the person being discipled can move on to disciple another person. This is the same principle that governs Level I of the Formation Companion, tools that, once seen, can be repeated by someone else after limited training. The synthesis of the four modes in this paper assumes the Navigators' reproducibility principle at Level I and integrates its scripture-memory discipline into one’s cognitive core.
