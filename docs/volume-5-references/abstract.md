# Abstract

This paper proposes the Formation Companion as a synthesis role that stands at the intersection of four established Christian spiritual care traditions. These are Spiritual Director/Direction, Transformation Prayer Ministry, and the Stephen Ministry, centered on reflective listening and group facilitation, as developed in the Band of Brothers and Fellowship of the Heart contexts. All are held together by the framework of the Intentional Journey of the Heart project. This paper shows that these four traditions, developed largely independently over multiple years, converge on a substantial common core of competencies organized across the three learning taxonomies of Bloom, Krathwohl, and Simpson. A three-level development progression is proposed: Companion I (Protocol Competent), Companion II (Mode Practitioner), and Companion III (Integrated Practitioner). Companion  III includes capstone competency manifested by the capacity to discern in a given interactive moment the application of one or more of the four operational modes, and to move between modes without losing grounding within the need or needs presented. The paper also addresses the discerning use of non-Christian-grounded methodologies, such as Shadow Work, and the role of Bloom's taxonomies. These three competency levels require character prerequisites. These will be developed later in a separate paper based on consultations with the Council.

Keywords: spiritual formation, formation companion, spiritual direction, Transformation Prayer Ministry, Stephen Ministry, Fellowship of the Heart, facilitator development, Affective Taxonomy

**Introduction: The Who of Formation**

This paper is derived from and supports the Formation Documents. These documents propose that the progressive internalization of hearing and obeying God is the essence of spiritual formation or becoming more like Christ. The first paper is about the parts that are formed (TA), how the heart forms (HFT), how the soul and spirit mature (SST), and how all three fit together at the individual and in interaction with a small group (MSFIG). This paper addresses the person who helps another on that formation journey.

This proposal was entered through a specific door. For some years, I have been part of a Band of Brothers, a path or tradition that trains men to facilitate the formation of other men in small groups. The training role was called the Weapons Master. Drawn from the military, the imagery was about equipping men with appropriate spiritual and mental weapons to fight their internal and external battles. A paper on that role was first published in February 2005 and was used by a church’s men’s ministry that wanted to establish and support men’s small groups. That job description emerged from training several men on the requirements of that role in mentoring such groups.  Since writing that paper, two events have provided impetus for this paper. First, much of the recent training is explicitly mixed gender, so the Weapons Master paper's masculine framing does not carry over. Second, the 2005 Weapons Master paper understated the role of the small group facilitator's ongoing internalization of hearing and obeying God's word. That was named as a prerequisite but not explicitly developed. Both of these gaps need to be filled.

When the Weapons Master role was aligned with three established Christian spiritual care traditions, Spiritual Direction, Transformation Prayer Ministry, and the Stephen Ministry tradition, something unexpected became visible. These three traditions (within small group dynamics) addressed development independently of each other over many years. However, they converge on a substantial common core. The differences among them, when examined carefully, turn out to be different operational modes, i.e., what the practitioner or facilitator does in a given moment, rather than apparent differences in what the practitioner fundamentally does or seeks to achieve. That common core is proposed here as the Formation Companion. The traditions are four ways the same person can be operating, depending on what the moment and need call for. The Companion at full maturity facilitates the core process with sufficient competence in each of the four modes to discern what a given moment requires.

This framing transcends my original version of a weapons master, which facilitates group interaction in support of Band of Brothers. The result is a proposed synthesis role that places the Formation Companion inside a much older and broader tradition than Band of Brothers alone, draws on the best of four independent streams of Christian tradition, and uses the three-part taxonomy framework that helps make facilitation competencies measurable and developable. The taxonomies reveal the structure beneath separate traditions that appear different on the surface.
