# Theological Anthropology of the Heart, Spirit, Soul, and Mind: Implications for Training in Spiritual Formation

Implications for Training in Spiritual Formation

**John G. Tittle**

*School of Divinity, Liberty University*

## Bridge: How This Paper Relates to the Other Formation Documents
This paper is the conceptual foundation on which the other four Formation Documents — the Heart Formation Theology paper (HFT), the Soul and Spirit Taxonomies paper (SST), the Model of Spiritual Formation for Individuals and Small Groups (MSFIG), and the Formation Companion paper (FC) — are built. Before we can train the heart, the soul, or the spirit, we need to be clear about what each of these actually is scripturally and how they relate to one another. That is the work of this paper. It examines the Old and New Testament development of the terms, engages a representative set of theological voices from the early church fathers through Watchman Nee, truth-tests relevant secular research (Kahneman, Thompson) through the Powlison and Trentham discernment protocols, and arrives at an integrated model: the human spirit within the heart, the heart and mind both within the soul, with each layer carrying its own developmental logic. That is the anthropology the subsequent four papers assume and build on. FC in particular extends the anthropology worked out here into the operational question of how one formed person accompanies another. Readers coming to this volume for the first time are encouraged to read this paper first; readers returning to it after HFT, SST, MSFIG, or FC will find it the place where the original definitions were worked out.

## Abstract
Theological anthropology has been debated for centuries, with multiple views on what scripture means in each verse and each usage. This paper analyzes four elements of the inner man: heart, soul, mind, and spirit, to present a way to understand these in a way consistent with the scriptural usage of the terms. The definitions focus on finding a description that would be useful in spiritual formation training. A starting point for such a model is suggested, and the integration of some possible sources of practice is discussed. Testing the model and extensions of the insights presented are also proposed for future research.

*Keywords: *Theological anthropology, heart, soul, human spirit, mind, spiritual formation

## Theological Anthropology of the Heart, Spirit, Soul, and Mind: Implications for Training in Spiritual Formation
Scripture uses many terms to describe what is translated as inner parts or the inner man (English Standard Bible, 2001/2016, Job 36:38). Soul, heart, spirit, mind, and more terms are used in multiple contexts. Since scripture is accurate and consistent, there is a way to understand and interpret what scripture is saying. This is important because there are implications for teaching spiritual formation if the teaching target is clearly understood. The human spirit, heart, soul, and mind are scripturally different, and these differences require distinct but complementary approaches to a complete spiritual formation curriculum. Also, these differences require complementary training and development in the teacher to lead such a transformation effectively.

## Theological Foundations and Definitions
The composition of this inner man has been discussed since the earliest church times (Kelly, 1978). In general, interpreting what each term refers to in any given context is difficult. It took centuries and multiple church councils to hammer out the essentials and, just as importantly, discard the heresies. It is helpful to look at the Hebrew views and then the developments in Christian views.

### Old Testament
The Hebrew view of anthropology was an inner man and an outer man. The inner man was who the person was, and the outer man was their reputation or how they were known in the world. (Cooper, 2017). It included a view of man as a whole entity. One example is the analysis of the Hebrew term leb or heart. Mitchell (2005) challenged her introductory Hebrew bible class to exegete the term. This journey of discovery is problematic because, as Mitchell describes it, the word is polyvalent, and a search of the Old Testament’s meaning turns up four different views. Her search with the class shows that the heart is the seat of will, emotions, thoughts, and life center. She uses four biblical passages to discuss each aspect in helpful detail. The result of her deliberations is that all four aspects can be present in any passage, with one or more aspects at the forefront, but all four are still present. Her answer to the question, “What does leb mean?” is yes to all. Kelly (1978, p.276) discusses how Augustine saw vestiges of the Trinity in everything and, as such, extended the concept as applied to God to human anthropology as a tri-part being.

Any theological anthropology needs to discuss how we are made in the image of God. Kilner (2015) takes this on with a fresh look and discusses what that means scripturally. He addresses the historical view of the image as how properties of God, such as intellect, thinking, and rationality, exist in humans. He then presents an alternative scriptural definition of the image of God as representing connection and reflection. A parallel to this is a child’s connection to their parent and reflection as they reflect the parent’s properties. He references the multiple ways that Christ is the perfect model of connection and reflection, and we are to grow in his likeness. Scripture uses many terms to describe God’s attributes and Grudem (1994, pp. 185-225) describes those communicable attributes that we, in various ways, are created in his image.

### New Testament
Greek thought, philosophy, and tools strongly impacted New Testament anthropology and theology.

**Kelly**

Kelly (1978, pp. 14-22) shows how the development of theological anthropology was a significant concern and doctrinal development of the early church with the important impact of Greek philosophy. The most direct Greek impact was dualistic: man was body and spirit, where the spirit was good and the body was bad, generally following Plato’s thoughts that ideal forms were perfect and everything material was an imperfect copy. The early patristic fathers inherited this viewpoint and debated what scripture had to say while battling the various heresies in hammering out what was orthodoxy and what was not. Their early views of body and soul were dualistic, but where to place the spirit in the mix was unclear.

**Fokin**

Fokin (2009) traces the development and insights of the theological anthropology duality view of body and soul compared to the trilogy view of spirit, soul, and body. He focuses on the pre-Nicene period thinking up until 325 AD. Overall, he offers that the discussion leaned towards a dual view, but there was a struggle, nonetheless. While upholding the duality of soul and body as central, the difficulty of what the human spirit was continued to present itself. A general view was that the spirit could be understood in multiple ways and was, in some fashion, a part of the soul. The idea that the human spirit was the “upper part,” maybe the intellect, or perhaps that part of the soul in contact with God, were all in contention. This period of debate laid the foundation for the subsequent work over the coming centuries.

**Cooper**

Cooper highlights that faith is necessary to respond to “Who is Man?” (p. 405). He summarizes his view that most Christians would agree that God created man in his image as a unity of a spiritual and bodily being. His summary is that dualistic holism is the only view that includes full scriptural support. His discussion helps understand the history of today’s evangelical views on theological anthropology.

**Grudem**

Grudem (1994, pp. 472-489) starts his discussion by affirming that scripture sees man as an overall unity created in God’s image. He then discusses the human spirit, soul, and body as seen in scripture passages. He first offers arguments for trichomoty focused on four scripture passages in defense and then presents his views of each of those scriptures and how to interpret them differently. After presenting both arguments, he settles on a dualist view of body and soul, suggesting none of the arguments for trichomoty are convincing even though they carry some weight.

**Willard**

Willard’s (2002) insights are that the multiple entities of a human are related, as shown by his figures on pages 21 and 22. These figures compactly show his understanding of the components and relationships of the human person. The figure on page 21 is a series of concentric circles with the spirit (will, heart) in the center; next outward are the mind, body, social, and soul. He clearly states that he believes that spirit, will, and heart are all in the innermost circle and the same fundamental components of the person. Will is about initiating actions, while spirit refers to aspects independent from physical reality. He sees the heart as referring to the center or core. He outlines the features and gives a detailed discussion of each. He mentions that diagrams like this are always incomplete but can convey helpful information.

**Moreland**

Moreland (2015) offers a revision of Willard’s view of the soul. He mostly agrees with Willard’s anthropology but finds the description of the soul inconsistent and incomplete. He offers a modified model that posits that the body and soul are interdependent.

**Nee**

Nee, on the other hand, directly challenges dualistic soul/body anthropology. His three-volume, ten-part book has different chapters that address different aspects of this analysis of the “hidden man.” His distinction between each component starts with the soul being the combination of a spirit from the breath of God and a body from the dust of the earth. The combination is called a “living soul.” (pp. 38-42). Nee carries this through his work, showing the soul as the mediator between the parts. He highlights that scripturally; soul life is the person’s natural life. The body is connected to the world, drawing man away from following the Holy Spirit. The Holy Spirit communicates to the human spirit, and the challenge for the person is listening to and submitting to the leading of the Holy Spirit. He sets the tension of the soul as the mediating entity between these voices. The voice the man chooses to obey is the tension in the soul. He uses “soulish” and “soulical” to clarify the underlying reference word in Greek or Hebrew. Soulish means that man is controlled by his soul and not by the leading of his spirit hearing from God. Soulical means the proper operation of the soul as designed by God. These words parallel the translations of spirituality, which Nee calls the God-consciousness in man. He uses these extra words to clarify a verse where translators use various other words in interpreting a term or a passage. In his view, the Bible is clear on the differences and never confuses the two.

In all this, Nee acknowledges that the work of the Holy Spirit is essential, including understanding what he, Nee, is presenting. He discusses the importance of recognizing the distinctive operation of the Holy Spirit with the human spirit and the person being led by the Holy Spirit. He considers this lack of recognition to impede spiritual maturity. Matters of the Holy Spirit come through the human spirit, and this requires a humble, teachable spirit to receive and be so taught, which is part of growth in Christ. Nee then calls the mind a battlefield and places it as part of the soul with the functions of understanding, will, and desire. The human spirit has the qualities of intuition, communion, and conscience. Listening to the Holy Spirit requires training in recognizing and reacting to those modes of knowing.

Interestingly, Nee does not directly address the place of the human heart in his anthropology. By silence, it is assumed to be the same as the soul, but that is not clearly stated anywhere. It is an interesting omission for such a frequent biblical term. Nee’s anthropology offered here would suggest multiple ways to target and teach different components. For instance, the human spirit must learn to hear from the Holy Spirit. The mind needs to learn how to submit its will to the Spirit’s guidance, choosing to follow that voice instead of the voice of the body, and so on.

### Trinity Development Parallel Discussion
One of the critical discussions in orthodoxy development has been the concept of the Trinity – where scripture never uses the word; the early church spent centuries sorting out how God could be one and three (Kelly, 1978, pp. 83-137). They finally decided both were true, gave it the Trinity label, and accepted that it was a mystery at its heart. This is a both/and approach rather than an either/or approach to discovering a scriptural truth. There is the possibility of a similar method to understand scriptural anthropology.

### Secular Research Findings and Truth Testing
Interesting observations and informative secular research findings are available to help give insight into this research into the inner man.

**Weger and Wagemann**

This editorial frontispiece by Weger and Wagemann (2012) challenges psychological research to include different methodological approaches beyond the physical. As they mention, the tools of cognitive and behavioral sciences are ideally suited to explore the distinction of soul, spirit, and consciousness. Topics that appeared before as intractable might now be usefully explored in a multi-disciplinary approach.

**Kahneman**

Kahneman (2011) is a psychologist who won the Nobel Prize in economics for his work on how people make decisions when given options. His results discussed in this book upended the reigning model of rational human decision-making to maximize perceived utility. He describes his work and uses System 1 and System 2 as labels to tell the story of how humans make decisions. System 1 is quick-acting, intuitive, non-reflective, and controls most of our day-to-day decisions. It also uses little of our cognitive energy or bandwidth to figure things out. System 2 is the reflective, rational, analytical process that is mental energy expensive and more likely to produce good decisions and better results from this more thoughtful process. System 2 is slow but more accurate, while System 1 is fast but sloppy and prone to mistakes of all kinds. Relevant to this research paper is the possibility that Kahneman’s research results have identified what the scripture calls the heart (System 1), or at least the decision-making part, as contrasted to the mind (System 2). Thoughts leading to actions can be seen in both systems.

**Thompson**

Thompson (2010) is a neuroscientist and a Christian. His focus is on how the mind and the brain work. His helpful insight for this research paper is a scientific definition of the mind. “The mind is an embodied and relational process, emerging from within and between brains, that regulated the flow of energy and information (p. 29).” He offers a layered triune brain model with different layers responding to stimuli differently. It suggests ways to understand the heart and mind from secular research findings.

### Truth Testing of Secular Models
Testing any secular model or framework before incorporating elements into a spiritual formation practice is important. Scripture is the reference base but what, if anything is useful from secular sources in understanding this topic?

**Powlison**

Powlison’s (2007) article offers a way to test secular counseling theories. Powlison offers a four-part discussion of this situation. Part two is interesting because his model can be used in contexts like this paper. He offers three priorities for how to frame this epistemological investigation. The first is to ground the topic in scriptural truth, and he suggests systematic theology is the correct source to position the topic. What does scripture say about this model under investigation, if anything? The second priority is to analyze critically, debunk as needed, and expose the underlying assumptions of the secular source. Step three is to extract valuable truths from the model.

These three steps can be used to examine secular research and findings in any field, including scientific research about consciousness theories, mind-brain investigations, and the like. This can inform and possibly shed new insights into what scripture has already said. The Powlison three-step protocol for testing secular offerings for scriptural truths can be applied to Kahneman’s research and others.

**Trentham**

Trentham’s (2019) article is part two of a three-part series on engaging with secular studies from a theological viewpoint. The underlying motivation is that truth can be found in multiple places, and we need a scripturally based way to sort through the truth from the rest. They offer a four-step protocol for this investigation and extraction of truth. Trentham’s process is like the Powlison discernment process. It again helps evaluate the ongoing research into God’s creation by the social sciences to see what, if anything, can inform and progress our growth in becoming more Christlike.

Applying these two tests to Kahneman (2011) and Trentham (2010), there are no violations apparent in either set of research results, and we can consider their insights as possibly informing the scriptural definition of the inner man.

## Anthropology for Spiritual Formation
Integrating the above is an interesting exercise in extracting a useful model for the purposes of focused spiritual formation. Nee’s spirit, soul, mind, and body model gives an analytical, scripturally careful starting point. In summary, in Nee’s view, the human spirit is in communion with the Holy Spirit, the body is in touch with the world, the mind is the reasoning analytic part, and the soul mediates and integrates all these parts. Nee does not address the heart other than in passing and, by implication, equates it to the soul. However, in several other authors’ views, the heart is different but still central to the soul. In these views, the heart is the seat of emotion, faith, connection to others, and beauty (art, music, etc.).

Using the Trinity doctrine developmental model of resolving the different views with a both/and approach could help in this situation. For the purpose of this paper, the model produced needs to help define, practice, and train spiritual formation. The heart has a clear, distinctive role from the overall soul; they are not the same. Using Kahneman’s research and discussion, assuming System 1 reflects the heart, which makes decisions quickly with little deliberation, and System 2 is the analytic mind’s slower and more careful process, the heart is measurably different from the mind and would need different treatment. This reflects Smith’s (2009 and 2013) focus on heart training compared to cognitive training.

This suggests the following integrated model: the human spirit is the innermost place and sits inside the human heart, the overall place of communication with the good, the true, and the beautiful. The heart is wholly contained within the soul and distinct from the mind, which is also contained within the soul. As Willard (2002) mentioned, a figure can help understand the relationship.

Scripture passage saying something about the spirit means that the same statement can be true about the heart and also the soul. The human spirit hears from the Holy Spirit, as discussed by Nee, and the soul decides –either the quick-acting, sloppy heart decision process or the slower, more reflective mind decision process what to do with the hearing from God. So, scripture can say the soul decides, the heart decides, and the mind decides; all can be true simultaneously.

This is a simple example, and more passages need to be explored to test the consistency of this integrated model. If validated through more testing, it can provide a reference base for understanding the implications of practicing and training in spiritual formation.

## Implications for Spiritual Formation
Each part of the inner man needs attention for spiritual formation to be comprehensive. Following are a few of the many possibilities for training the individual parts.

### Smith
Smith (2009) challenges the notion that Christian education is only developing the correct doctrine in students. He focuses on training the heart to love the Kingdom of God by using the heart language of metaphor and parable, as did Jesus. He challenges the prevailing image of humans as primarily thinking animals and poses that we are primarily “affective animals” shaped by what we love and attend to. We live in the stories that we love, including the culture we are surrounded with. These stories are told in images, movies, songs, and places we inhabit. He uses a striking descriptive visit to a mall to show the parable and metaphor of how a culture understands and uses this heart language to shape our actions, particularly buying actions.

Smith challenges us to recognize these impacts all around us and act by using the metaphors and parables of the kingdom of God to draw our hearts toward a Christian education instead of a secular one. He offers an alternative model of a monastic-like embodied learning environment that teaches through daily practice and interaction with others. This is drastic, and it is unclear how this could work out in an authentic Christian University setting, but that is why his series has volume two.

Smith (2013) continues in this second volume of his trilogy with the developmental anthropology thesis on how to shape Christian education. In part one, he focuses on developing practice habits such that the imagination, trained by heart language, orients us to the kingdom of God in our daily lives. He discusses the tension between the intellect and instinct. He suggests we must adopt a bodily position in worship to receive and become what we are designed to be. The second part is called “Sanctified Perception” and is his discussion about how we are “more moved than convinced.” He almost laughs at himself for using analytic words to say we are fundamentally intuitive and how this intuitive grasp avoids our intellectual, cognitive understanding. He compares the liturgies used from multiple sources to show that the rhythm and content have been developed and tested over the centuries because it works. The forms are the Christian story in song, words, images, and actions relived each time we gather to worship. We tell our story to ourselves in the very act of worship, which moves our hearts before we are intellectually convinced. The combination of shaping a Christian worldview from a heart perspective of what we love can also shape a community for missional service, not just for coffee after Sunday service.

### Willard and Johnson
Willard and Johnson (2012) bring the possibly abstract idea of hearing God down to a practical reality of not just hearing God but being willing to live a life that pleases him. It is an ongoing two-way process and a learning process in distinguishing his voice from other sources. As Willard says, this is actually a whole life process, not just a hearing process. He addresses various aspects of guidelines for hearing, how scripture is a source, and how hearing leads to character transformation.

### Yount
Yount (2019) expounds on Willard’s six essential components of the human with a focus on training the mind in the process of spiritual formation. He highlights thoughts discussed by Willard and expands on the four aspects in some detail and how they relate to training. Overall, he is positive and supportive of Willard’s definitions and process but does add his own “Discipler’s Model” to the process articulated by Willard. His focus on just thoughts (the mind) is a factor to consider when using his approach since the relationship between the elements of the spiritual formation may be as important as the elements themselves.

### Krathwold
Training for the mind has a rich secular development history. One significant part of that development was Bloom’s Taxonomy. As Krathwold (2002) notes in his article, the taxonomy impact was gradually understood, and by the article’s date, the taxonomy had been translated into 22 languages and used worldwide. The article discusses the updated taxonomy as of 2001. He relays Bloom’s original idea that it would be a valuable measurement tool. This was accomplished by giving a common language about learning goals, a basis for positioning a course within common educational standards, and a meta-view that helps set any individual course within a more extensive range of possibilities. Definitions were hammered out in six categories of what was eventually called the cognitive domain. As Krathwold points out when used in practice, these categories tended to show that objectives were being used that were too simplistic and offered a way to discuss moving beyond knowledge and recall, the bottom two categories, into comprehension, analysis, synthesis, and evaluation.

The 2001 revision focused on standardizing the categories and adding two new dimension definitions. These suggest a two-dimensional matrix called a Taxonomy Table. Each cell intersects one of the four knowledge elements and another of the six cognitive elements. Table 3 demonstrates this in the article with a worked example. This matrix makes the choices clear for what knowledge is desired and what cognitive process needs to be demonstrated—a simplification of what can be textually a complex concept to communicate and grasp. A desired mix of knowledge and cognitive processes can be easily discussed and developed using this revised taxonomy.

Bloom’s taxonomy offers a possible way to focus on defining best practices in training the integrated model for spiritual formation. The current cognitive taxonomy can be applied to training the mind in spiritual formation-defined content. Developing a separate and individual taxonomy for each of the heart, human spirit, and soul could provide a similar focus for training each part with clarity and measurable progress. Some excellent resources discussed in the following can be used to get started and help define a set of such taxonomies.

### Resources for Training Practices
Training practice resources that use a staged developmental model and have measured the training results are useful starting points for this journey.

**Austin**

Austin (2015) brings an interesting view of sanctification called theosis or union with God, mainly seen in the Eastern Orthodox doctrine. As Austin presents, the process is the work of the Holy Spirit in the believer’s life. The underlying idea is the restoration of the image of God in the believer through intentional spiritual disciplines in cooperation with the Holy Spirit’s work. Another way to discuss this is learning how to adopt the thinking patterns we see in the scripture of Jesus, our model. The practices mentioned are prayer, memorization, zeal, etc.

**Willard**

Willard (1998) offers his perspective on the inner man and the implications for spiritual formation, mainly through the spiritual disciplines. In his view, the spirit (heart and will) is the innermost part of the soul. In this short article, Willard focuses on the soul in spiritual formation. He goes on to discuss three spiritual disciplines that can help with the spiritual formation of the soul. The disciplines are an essential but incomplete necessity for spiritual formation. He states that it also takes scripture and the Holy Spirit.

**Hawkins and Parkinson**

Hawkins and Parkinson (2011) discuss their study called “Move,” initiated by Willow Creek Church, which asked what works and does not work in this large church’s various programs. This study directly analyzed a stage model of growth based on growing maturity in the two greatest commandments of Jesus. The results showed a four-stage growth pattern and the activities, practices, and attitudes that worked in each stage. The activities and practices that thread through the growth stages were grouped under the titles of Spiritual Beliefs and Practices, Personal Spiritual Practices, Organized Church Activities, and Spiritual Activities with others. They also extracted the elements that helped move from one stage to another. For each stage, some subset of these four categories were seen as most helpful. Respondents identified if they were making progress or felt stalled at each stage.

The results have had some challenges. One key one is the need for more transparency in the underlying data that other scholarly analyses could test. Another core challenge is how one pattern can fit the diversity of the church. The results shown so far are generally high impact, but church leadership, as always, seems to be the critical factor in the success of any program.

**Foster**

Foster’s (1998) presentation of 12 classic spiritual disciplines is an essential source for any training plan for spiritual formation. The inner disciplines focus on the heart and spirit. The outer disciplines focus on the heart, soul, and body, and the corporate disciplines focus on shaping a community, as Smith (2009 & 2013) emphasizes.

**Schoen**

Schoen (1987) offers an innovative way to learn things that cannot be taught, such as design. His examples in architecture school, counseling practice, and learning music show a way to understand spiritual formation in a new way. His approach could be understood as learning how to design a life, such as the transformation in spiritual formation, and not just learning further skills or content. He observes that the mutual interaction described in his approach requires the student and teacher to be ready to be changed.

### Summary Applications
The Hawkins and Parkinson (2011) study has a rich set of practices and disciplines involved in growing up in Christ. It can provide a solid starting source for effective practices across all four elements of the inner man considered in this paper. For example, reflective bible study was shown as an effective practice across all four stages of development. Best practices around that skill set and teaching that skill set should combine to have a high impact on progress in spiritual formation. In general, the human spirit training needs to focus on hearing God. Willard and Johnson (2012) presented an excellent discussion on how to train that facility. The heart needs training in recognizing how to connect and love God and others using heart language, as Smith (2009 and 2013) discussed. On the other hand, the soul needs to learn how to integrate sometimes disparate and conflicting voices. Learning how to handle seemingly paradoxical situations would be a valuable skill to learn. Grudem’s Systematic Theology (2004) is one excellent example of examining the various views that conflict over doctrinal points and how to resolve them using scripture as the reference point.

There is a parallel and just as important model of training the teacher/facilitator for spiritual formation. The essential skills might be hearing from the Holy Spirit, spiritual direction, spiritual discernment, and Christian counseling tools and techniques. A parallel taxonomy of teacher skills and knowledge could capture the skill sets needed. The benefit of such a taxonomy for teacher training is similar to the benefits for a spiritual formation student.

There are multiple extensions and follow-ups from the thesis presented in this paper. The next obvious step is to test the model with every scripture that uses heart, soul, mind, and spirit and see if the model helps understand and interpret what scripture says about human anthropology. This would involve hundreds of passages and provide extensive testing. The model would be updated and refined as needed using the basic assumption that scripture is consistent, and assuming a both/and approach will give a result that is useful for spiritual formation training and possibly give some new insights. Including relevant and tested secular sources could provide insight and detail for the expanded model. Another expansion includes considering the scripture’s use of the body and how it relates to the other four elements.

Assuming the above approach produces a useful model with a consistent view of anthropology that can be used as a base for spiritual formation training, there are some natural next steps. An obvious next step is considering how the cognitive taxonomy can/should be applied to this content. Another is to develop Bloom’s Taxonomy for each heart, soul, and spirit element. The development activity itself will help clarify and refine the terminology used and probably the basic anthropology model.

An intriguing and exciting extension is to use Willard’s (2002) inclusion of social - as he defined it - as part of anthropology. This can easily be expanded into considering the spiritual formation of a small group and then scaling it up to the spiritual formation of larger communities. What might be the spiritual formation scriptural equivalents of a small group’s heart, soul, mind, and spirit as they seek to grow into more Christlikeness?

Following Schoen’s formulation and rethinking fresh ways to explore this topic, maybe rephrasing the language to ask, “How can we best train for designing our life to be Christ-like by the power of the Holy Spirit?” All the above possibilities would make an exciting exploration of who we are and who we are called to be.

## Conclusion
The difference between the scriptural language and discussion of the parts of the inner man are essential when considering how to help people grow up into Christ. This is more than just an intellectual exercise; being clear on the focus area and using the right tools and framework for the correct part should make the growth process more efficient. In all, listening to what the Holy Spirit is saying in any situation is critical, and then selecting the right tool to implement what He says. Heart tools are different from human spirit tools, which are different from cognitive mind tools. What is needed is the right tool for the right job.

Even with the use of Greek philosophical tools and views, the fathers always kept returning to scripture, so the passage in 1 Cor. 14:15 (English Standard Bible, 2001/2016) of praying with the spirit and the mind and singing with the spirit and the mind kept the distinctiveness of mind, spirit, and soul on the table for discussion. This period of debate laid the foundation for the subsequent work over the coming centuries, and this discussion continues.

Foster, R. J. (1998). Celebration of discipline: The path to spiritual growth. Harper.

In this classic work, Foster presents 12 spiritual disciplines grouped into three categories. The first set of disciplines he calls inner. These are meditation, prayer, study, and fasting. These disciplines are not generally visible to others. The next set is visible, and they are called outward disciplines. These are simplicity, solitude, submission, and service. The third set is called corporate. These are confession, guidance, worship, and celebration. These 12, as he discusses, are life-changing encounters with God and growth in knowing him and walking in his ways. He also discusses that there are many more. His presentation focuses on simplifying and making these 12 accessible to the everyday person to practice.

Grudem, W. (1994). Systematic Theology. Zondervan.

Grudem presents the wide-ranging topics of systematic theology with clarity and simplicity but not simplistically. Before offering his stance, he presents various major viewpoints on each topic and reference sources for each. Including questions at the end of each chapter invites the reader or study group into a deeper and possibly personal application of the preceding discussion. Relevant scripture memory verses, along with one or more classic hymns, offer more ways to enter the topic in more than just abstract, cognitive ways. His presentation of such a wide range of material in a straightforward, accessible manner has made this book widely popular, with over 400,000 copies sold so far. His viewpoint is always grounded in the essentiality of the truth of scripture.

Multiple chapters are relevant to the topic of this paper, such as Chapter 14 - “God in Three Persons: The Trinity,” Chapter 21 – “The Creation of Man,” and Chapter 38 – “Sanctification (Growth in Likeness to Christ),” but Chapter 23 – “The Essential Nature of Man,” is the directly helpful discussion. This chapter outlines the arguments for dualistic (soul/body) versus trichotomy (spirit/soul/body). Grudem gives the pros and cons of each but prefaces the whole discussion with a critical grounding observation that scripture emphasizes the unity of man and that any discussion of the parts of this unity needs to be held considering that overall focus. The core challenge to trichotomy offered is that scripture uses the terms soul and body in both the Old and New Testaments in similar, even synonymous ways.

Hawkins, G. L., Parkinson, C. (2011). Move: What 1,000 churches reveal about spiritual growth. Zondervan.

This represents a study initiated by Willow Creek Church, which asked what works and does not work in this large church’s various programs. They engaged a research team that used modern analytic tools to answer that fundamental question. The starting point was the challenge to clearly and measurably define what success looked like. The team asked a group led by Dallas Willard to define success in discipleship. They answered that the first and second commandments Jesus gave were the objective. Using this as the starting point, they developed survey instruments that probed spiritual growth and the programs that helped in that journey. The results showed a four-stage growth pattern and the activities, practices, and attitudes that worked in each stage. The activities and practices that thread through the growth stages were grouped under the titles of Spiritual Beliefs and Practices, Personal Spiritual Practices, Organized Church Activities, and Spiritual Activities with others. They also extracted the elements that helped move from one stage to another. Hence the title “Move.” For each stage, some subset of these four categories were seen as most helpful. Respondents identified if they were making progress or felt stalled at each stage. The research also identified the dissatisfiers in the various programs and activities by stage. A concerning finding was that the most mature stage they measured also had some of the most dissatisfied members. Moreover, about 18% of the estimated population felt stalled and did not know what to do next.

The research team has developed a composite “Spirituality Vitality Index” as a simple composite number to indicate progress or lack thereof. The book includes how to interpret the results of a survey and some recommended steps. Training in the tools and coaching churches in planning and implementing actions in response to the results are widely available. This process has been used in over 1,000 churches across multiple denominations as of the 2011 publication date. Numerous dissertations have explored the impacts on various denominational settings. There also have been some challenges to the results. One key one is the lack of transparency in the underlying data that other scholarly analyses could test. Another core challenge is how one pattern can fit the diversity of the church. The results shown so far are generally high impact, but church leadership, as always, seems to be the critical factor in the success of any program. As the authors clearly state, there is no silver bullet; this process is just one tool.

Kahneman, D. (2011). Thinking Fast and Thinking Slow. Farrar, Straus, and Giroux.

Kahneman is a psychologist who won the Nobel Prize in economics for his work on how people make decisions when given options. His results discussed in this book upended the reigning model of rational human decision-making to maximize perceived utility. He describes his work and uses System 1 and System 2 to tell the story of how humans make decisions. System 1 is quick-acting and intuitive but mostly gut-reactive and controls most of our day-to-day decisions. It also uses little of our cognitive energy or bandwidth to figure things out. System 2 is the slower, reflective, rational, analytical process that is mentally expensive and more likely to produce good decisions and better results from this more thoughtful process. System 1 is fast but sloppy and prone to mistakes of all kinds. The text details the biases that cause our decisions to be less than optimal, along with examples of experiments and results that show the biases in action. These descriptions make a very readable flow and vivid picture; anyone will find themselves in the descriptions somewhere. Relevant to this research paper is the possibility that Kahneman’s research results have identified what the scripture calls the heart (System 1), or at least the decision-making part, as contrasted to the mind (System 2). Thoughts leading to actions can be seen in both systems.

Kelly, J. N. D. (2014). Early Christian doctrines (5th;1;). Bloomsbury Academic.

Kelly had multiple insights into the development of early Christian doctrines up until the Council of Nicene. He addresses this early formative period, the various controversies, and how they were resolved. He also discusses how Augustine saw vestiges of the Trinity in everything and, as such, extended the concept of the Trinity as applied to God to human anthropology.

Kilner, J. F. (2015). Dignity and Destiny: Humanity in the Image of God. Eerdmans.

Any theological anthropology needs to discuss how we are made in the image of God. Kilner takes this on with a fresh look and discusses what that means scripturally. He addresses the historical view of the image as how properties of God, such as intellect, thinking, and rationality, exist in humans. He also shows how that view leads to the position that those with less of that property are, in that sense, less than human and, therefore, of less worth. This, then, can lead directly to a basis for eugenics and all the multiplied harm from that position. Several examples, such as the oppression of mentally disabled people and women and the justification for slavery, can all be traced to what was initially intended to be a basis for the value of human life. Kilner presents a scriptural base of the image of God as representing connection and reflection. The parallel is that a child is connected to their parent and reflection as they reflect the parent’s properties. Christ is the perfect model of connection and reflection, and we are to grow in his likeness. Kilner makes the point that the image of God, as previously understood, is not damaged, but the connection is, by the Fall, damaged. Our dignity and destiny are to connect in fellowship with and reflect him in this world. Kilner holds the endowed Forman Chair of Christian Ethics and Theology at Trinity Evangelical Divinity School. He is the director of bioethics degree programs at Trinity International University.

Krathwohl, D. R. (2002). A revision of Bloom’s taxonomy: An overview. College of Education, Ohio State University. doi:10.1207/s15430421tip4104_2

Bloom’s Taxonomy was initially published in 1956, but as Krathwold notes in his article, taxonomy was an unfamiliar term in educational practice, and the community paid little notice. However, the impact was gradually understood, and by the date of the article, 2002, the taxonomy had been translated into 22 languages and used worldwide. The article discusses the updated taxonomy as of 2001. Krathwold was a co-author of the first taxonomy and a co-editor of the revision.

The article discusses the process used and the organization of the first taxonomy. He relays Bloom’s original idea that it would be a valuable measurement tool. This was accomplished by giving a common language about learning goals, a basis for positioning a course within common educational standards, and a meta-view that helps set any individual course within a more extensive range of possibilities. Definitions were hammered out in six categories of what was eventually called the cognitive domain. As Krathwold points out, when used in practice, these categories tended to show that the more simplistic objectives were being used and offered a way to discuss moving beyond knowledge and recall, the bottom two categories, into comprehension, analysis, synthesis, and evaluation.

The revision focused on standardizing the categories by renaming three of the original six and discussing a knowledge dimension with four types of knowledge detailed. A new category in these four was metacognitive knowledge. This was an emerging research topic and had been demonstrated to be an essential awareness for students. Also added was a cognitive process dimension. Verb and noun forms were also standardized across the categories. These two new dimension definitions immediately suggest a two-dimensional matrix called a Taxonomy Table. Each cell intersects one of the four knowledge dimension elements and another of the six cognitive elements. Table 3 demonstrates this in the article with a worked example. This matrix makes the choices clear for what knowledge is desired and what cognitive process needs to be demonstrated—a simplification of what can be textually a complex concept to communicate and grasp. A desired mix of knowledge and mental processes can be easily discussed and developed using this revised taxonomy.

This reviewer has used this taxonomy in his engineering classes to challenge students with a broad range of activities and intentionally constructed lessons. The result was personally effective in reviewing the possibilities as the in-class and homework activities were prepared. Communicating the curriculum to the school leadership and gaining their approval (and sometimes feedback) was also made much more straightforward than it might have been. The Taxonomy Table takes a little work to grasp the language and concept. However, the visualization of the course flow and progress of higher cognitive processing and knowledge levels is very rewarding.

Mitchell, C. (2005). Heart and mind in the Hebrew scriptures. Touchstone, 23(1), pp. 5-13.

Mitchell offers her insights in the essay through an engaging story. She tells of challenging her introductory class in the Hebrew bible by doing an exegesis of Deut. 6:4-6, particularly on discovering what the heart (leb) means. This journey of discovery is challenging because, as Mitchell describes it, the word is polyvalent, and a search of the Old Testament’s meaning turns up four different views. The heart is the seat of will, emotions, thoughts, and life center. She uses four biblical passages to discuss each aspect in helpful detail. The result of her deliberations is that all four aspects can be present in any passage, with one or more aspects at the forefront, but all four are still present. Her answer to the question, “What does leb mean?” is yes to all.

Moreland, J. P. (2015). Tweaking Dallas Willard’s Ontology of the Human Person. Journal of Spiritual Formation and Soul Care, 8(2), 187–202. https://doi.org/10.1177/193979091500800205

Moreland offers a revision of Willard’s view of the soul. Willard was Moreland’s PhD dissertation supervisor. He mostly agrees with Willard’s anthropology but finds the description of the soul inconsistent and incomplete. He offers an alternative model that corrects this concern and reinforces Willard’s intent. He uses mirror neurons as an example of his approach and resolves that empathy, in one case, requires mirror neurons to fire as part of the soul. He proposes that Willard was a substance dualist who would say the person is a spiritual entity and more than a body. Moreland suggests that the body and soul are interdependent, extending that one example to a general statement. He also uses the analogy of a CD. What is on the disk is just groves. It takes the proper equipment to play and make the music.

Nee, W. (1968). The Spiritual Man. New York: Christian Fellowship Publishers.

Nee, the leader of the China Inland Mission, offers a scripturally grounded analysis directly from the original languages. He demonstrates the biblical position of man as composed of body, soul, and spirit, and he challenges the dualistic soul/body anthropology. He uses the translation of soulish to clarify the underlying word in Greek or Hebrew. This clarifies the places where the translators use various other words in interpreting a passage. In his view, the Bible is clear in the differences and never confuses the two.

This is a three-volume, ten-chapter book where different chapters address different pairs of this analysis of the “hidden man.” This work also deals with other topics, such as the scriptural definition of the body, sin, redemption, the fall, salvation, and the cross. His distinction between each component starts with the soul being the combination of spirit from the breath of God and body from the dust of the earth. The combination is called a “living soul,” Nee carries this through his work, showing the soul as the mediator between the two. He highlights that scripturally, soul life is the person’s natural life. He sets the tension of the soul as the mediating agency between the body and the human spirit. The body is connected to the world, drawing man away from following the Holy Spirit. The Holy Spirit communicates to the human spirit, and the challenge is listening to and submitting to the leading of the Holy Spirit.

In all this, Nee acknowledges that the work of the Holy Spirit is essential, including understanding what he, Nee, is presenting. He discusses the importance of recognizing the distinctive operation of the Holy Spirit in communicating with the human spirit and the person being led by the Holy Spirit. He considers this lack of recognition to impede spiritual maturity. Matters of the Holy Spirit come through the human spirit, and this requires a humble, teachable spirit to receive and be so taught, which is part of growth in Christ. Nee spends significant space on the dangers of living what he calls a “soulish life,” the need for the cross, and the importance of choosing rightly. Nee then calls the mind a battlefield and places it as part of the soul with the functions of understanding, will, and desire. The human spirit has the qualities of intuition, communion, and conscience. Listening to the Holy Spirit requires training in recognizing and reacting to those modes of knowing.

Interestingly, Nee does not directly address the place of the human heart in his anthropology. By silence, it is assumed to be the same as the soul, but that is not clearly stated anywhere. It is an interesting omission for such a frequent biblical term. Nee offers many other insights, such as the dangers of walking in the Spirit, Satan’s attacks and accusations, and spiritual warfare. Any chapter of the book is a rich chance for reflection on scripture and a radical life in following Christ. The anthropology offered here would suggest multiple ways to target and teach different components. For instance, the human spirit must learn to hear from the Holy Spirit. The mind needs to learn how to submit its will to the Spirit’s guidance, and so on.

Powlison, D. (2007). Cure of Souls (and the Modern Psychotherapies). The Journal of Biblical Counseling, 25:2. The Christian Counseling and Educational Foundation.

This article is about Christian counseling and targets counseling that Powlison says is secular because evangelicals had no answer with scriptural-based counseling practices. Powlison Offers a four-part discussion of this situation. Part one summarizes the revolution in counseling leading up to this situation. Part two deals with how to investigate and adopt, as appropriate, the truths in non-scriptural models for counseling. He titles part two Epistemological Priorities. Parts three and four then deal with a theory of motivation and the licensing of counselors.

Part two is of interest to this research paper because his model can be used in other contexts. He offers three priorities for how to frame this epistemological investigation. The first is to ground the topic in scriptural truth, and he suggests systematic theology is the correct source to position the topic. What does scripture say about this model under investigation, if anything? The second priority is to analyze critically, debunk as needed, and expose the underlying assumptions of the secular model. The third step is to extract valuable truths from the model.

These three steps can be used to examine secular research and findings in any field, including scientific research about consciousness theories, mind-brain investigations, and the like. This can inform the core thesis and possibly shed new insights on what scripture already has said.

Schoen, D.A. (1987). Educating the Reflective Practitioner: Toward a New Design for Teaching and Learning in the Professions. Jossey-Bass.

Schoen offers an innovative way to learn things that cannot be taught, such as design. His examples in architecture school, counseling practice, and learning music provide a way to understand spiritual formation in a new way. Smith, J. K. A. (2009). Desiring the Kingdom: Worship, Worldview, and Cultural Formation. Baker Academic.

Smith, a professor of philosophy at Calvin College, where he holds the Gary and Henrietta Byker Chair in Applied Reformed Theology and Worldview, challenges the notion that Christian education is only developing the correct doctrine in students. He proposes the need to focus on training the heart to love the Kingdom of God by using the heart language of metaphor and parable, as did Jesus. He challenges the prevailing image of humans as primarily thinking animals and poses that we are primarily “affective animals” shaped by what we love and attend to. We live in the stories that we love, including the culture we are surrounded with. These stories are told in images, movies, songs, and places we inhabit. He uses a striking descriptive visit to a mall to show the parable and metaphor of how a culture understands and uses this heart language to shape our actions, particularly buying actions. We are invited to love the world and the things the world wants us to believe are the good life. We are invited to worship the good things presented to us in these settings.

Smith challenges us to recognize these impacts all around us and act by using the metaphors and parables of the kingdom of God to draw our hearts toward a Christian education instead of a secular one. An example of an immersive Christian education highlights the implication he draws. In Part Two, titled “The Practiced Shape of the Christian Life,” he details this thought. He focuses on worship as a central process of Christian Education. He uses immersive Christian education to show the contrast but parallel experience compared to the secular mall experience. Smith offers a possible way for his discussion of Christian education to be worked out in practice. He provides that the objective of Christian education should be the same as Christian worship: to educate the heart through images, practices, songs, and collective literature and metaphors that speak heart language. He offers an alternative model of a monastic-like embodied learning environment that teaches through daily practice and interaction with others. This is drastic, and it is unclear how this could work out in an authentic Christian University setting, but that is why his series has volume two.

Smith, J.K.A. (2013). Imagining the Kingdom: How Worship Works. Baker Academic.

Smith continues in this second volume of his trilogy, focusing on developing a thesis on how to shape Christian education. In part one, his essential focus is developing practice habits such that the imagination, trained by heart language, orient us to the kingdom of God in our daily lives. He developed a tension between the intellect and the instinct. He suggests we must adopt a bodily position to receive and become what we are designed to be. Part two is called “Sanctified Perception” and is his discussion about how we are “more moved than convinced.” He almost laughs at himself for using analytic words to say we are fundamentally intuitive and how this intuitive grasp avoids our intellectual, cognitive understanding. He compares the liturgies used from multiple sources to show that the rhythm and content have been developed and tested over the centuries because it works. The forms are the Christian story in song, words, images, and actions relived each time we gather to worship. We tell our story to ourselves in the very act of worship, which moves us before we are convinced. He includes considering praying a Christian worldview more than just studying one. The combination of shaping a Christian worldview from a heart perspective of what we love can also shape a community for missional service, not just for coffee after Sunday service.

As seen in the first volume, Smith has a fresh, radical, and back-to-basic agenda to change Christian education’s core assumptions and practices and, in the process, to change our lives. This volume should be fascinating to Christian leaders focused on the tools, approaches, and theory of shaping hearts for the kingdom of God. Smith’s description of offering a toolbox is a great way to understand the power of this volume.

Thompson, C. (2010). Anatomy of the soul: Surprising connections between neuroscience and spiritual practices that can transform your life and relationships. Salt River.

Thompson is a neuroscientist and a Christian. His focus is on how the mind and the brain work. His helpful insight for this research paper is a scientific definition of the mind. “The mind is an embodied and relational process, emerging from within and between brains, that regulated the flow of energy and information (p. 29).” He offers a layered triune brain model with different layers responding to stimuli in different ways.

Trentham, J. D. (2019). Reading the social sciences theologically (part 2): Engaging and appropriating models of human development. Christian Education Journal, 16(3), 476-494.

This is part two of a three-part article on engaging with secular studies from a theological viewpoint. The underlying motivation is that truth can be found in multiple places, and we need a scripturally based way to sort through the truth from the rest. They offer a four-step protocol for this investigation and extraction of truth. The first step is having a solid doctrinal base and a vision of redemptive human development. The second step is to read secular-sourced social science research with an open mind instead of automatically starting to criticize the lack of a scriptural base. The third step he calls reflective discernment. This entails critically and charitably evaluating, looking for what can be used in a scriptural framework. The fourth step is how to apply the truth discovered in the process.

This process is like the Powlison discernment process. It again helps evaluate the ongoing research into God’s creation by the social sciences to see what, if anything, can inform and progress our growth in becoming more Christlike.

Weger, U. W., Wagemann, J. (2021). Soul, spirit, and consciousness in psychology and philosophy. Hogrefe & Huber.

This editorial frontispiece by Weger and Wagemann challenges psychological research to include different methodological approaches beyond the physical. These tools and approaches have been part of theological inquiry for centuries, but, as they mention, these have gradually receded into the background for mainline researchers. As they say, the tools of cognitive and behavioral sciences are ideally suited to explore the distinction of soul, spirit, and consciousness. Topics that appeared before as intractable might now be usefully explored in a multi-disciplinary approach. In this light, they invited papers dealing with this possibility and received two that survived the peer review process and were published.

They position four core questions: how to distinguish between soul, spirit, and consciousness; methods of investigation; variation of understanding of these three concepts across cultures; and how are these concepts understood from a lay viewpoint? These are four interesting questions. The editors have served the community by raising these challenges and publishing these papers.

Willard offers his perspective on the inner man and the implications for spiritual formation, mainly through the spiritual disciplines. Willard, D. (2002). Renovation of the heart: Putting on the character of Christ. NavPress.

Willard’s insights are that the multiple entities of a human are related, as shown by his figures on pages 21 and 22.
