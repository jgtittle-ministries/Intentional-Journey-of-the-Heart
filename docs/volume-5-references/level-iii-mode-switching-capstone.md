# The Level III Mode-Switching Capstone

The capstone competency of Level III is the real-time discernment of which mode a given moment calls for, and the capacity to move between modes without losing grounding. This is the competency that makes the synthesis framing of this paper matter in practice. A Companion who has only one mode is not a Formation Companion, as this paper defines the role; they are a specialist in that mode. A Formation Companion at full maturity reads what the moment is and responds with the mode that serves the moment.

A few illustrations make this concrete. A pilgrim arrives for what was scheduled as a spiritual direction session and discloses a childhood memory that is clearly active, unresolved, and asking for attention. A mature Companion can shift into TPM-style work for that session without losing the spiritual direction frame the pilgrim came with. A pilgrim in a group session who is in acute grief over a loss the group did not know about. A mature Companion can hold the group's work in suspension and shift into Stephen-style presence with that pilgrim, bringing the group back in at the right moment. If a group is plateauing in their formation work, a mature Companion can recognize that what is needed is not a new tool but a season of simpler, spiritual-direction-style attending to what God has been doing that the group has not noticed.

Mode-switching requires three things that cannot be acquired separately. First, it requires genuine competence in more than one mode, which is why it is a Level III competency and not available earlier. Second, it requires enough integration in the Companion's own formation that they can hold the different modes as complementary rather than competing. Third, it requires a nudging from the Holy Spirit. A Companion whose own spiritual direction and TPM training have never been integrated may tend to pick whichever one they are more comfortable with, rather than whichever one the moment calls for. Integration is the Stage 4 to Stage 5 movement of the Companion's own Affective Taxonomy journey and experience. It cannot be reached through training alone.

Referral can be considered as a mode. The most important mode-switch a Companion can make is not between the four Christian care modes but between companioning and referral to licensed professional care. A Companion recognizes when the moment calls for a move outside the Companion's scope entirely and has the clarity to make that referral without hesitation or self-judgment. Referral is not a failure of companioning; it is a competency of companioning. The pilgrim whose situation requires a clinician is best served by a Companion who knows it and says so.
