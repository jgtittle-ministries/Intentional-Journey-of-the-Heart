# On the Discerning Use of Non-Christian-Grounded Methodologies

Several of the tools and methods that appear in Companion practice have origins outside the Christian tradition. For example, Shadow Work is from the Jungian and mythopoetic lineages, elements of Voice Dialogue in Hal and Sidra Stone's Psychology of Selves, the somatic practices underlying Guts facilitation, and Kahneman's System 1 / System 2 framework. The question of how these methodologies should be evaluated and used by a Christian Formation Companion warrants explicit treatment rather than a case-by-case approach.

TA proposed two discernment protocols for exactly this purpose: Powlison's three-step epistemological priorities and Trentham's four-step reading of the social sciences. Both are worth rehearsing here in the context of Companion practice. Powlison's protocol: (1) ground the topic in scriptural truth first, typically through systematic theology; (2) analyze the secular model critically, exposing and assessing its underlying assumptions; (3) extract valuable truths from the model while leaving the assumptions that conflict with scripture. Trentham's protocol is similar in shape: (1) establish the doctrinal base; (2) read the secular research with an open rather than automatically critical posture; (3) apply reflective discernment; (4) apply what is genuinely useful.

This can be applied to Shadow Work, for example. Shadow Work offers a genuinely useful method for naming and honoring the reality of the inner division that scripture calls the old self, the false self, and the flesh. Its practice of bringing disowned material into conscious relationship maps well onto what Vol 2 calls identifying the wound and the false self, and onto SST's Stage 2 of the soul. These methodological contributions are usable. What Shadow Work does not provide, and what the Christian Companion must bring from elsewhere, is the naming of sin as sin, the recognition that some shadow material is fallen nature requiring repentance rather than integration, the identification of the Holy Spirit rather than the integrating self as the agent of transformation, and the refusal of the Jungian metaphysics that treats the collective unconscious as if it were theologically neutral. The Companion who uses Shadow Work tools well does so within a Christian framework they bring to the work, rather than within the Jungian framework that originally produced them.

As a general rule, the Companion is free to use methods whose origins are not Christian, provided the Companion brings a Christian framework to govern how the methods are used, names explicitly what the methods do and do not supply theologically, and is willing to leave behind elements of the original framework that conflict with scripture. The Companion is not free to use methods as if the framework does not matter, or to pretend that neutral-framework methodologies are theologically neutral. Method is never neutral; method always assumes an answer to what the person is, how change happens, and who the agent is. The Christian Companion holds those answers and chooses methods accordingly.
