# The Core Competencies — What the Four Traditions Share

When the four traditions are placed side by side, most of what the practitioner develops for each is the same for all four. Each tradition adds mode-specific competencies on top of the core, but the core itself is what every Formation Companion must develop regardless of which mode they embrace. The core uses three learning taxonomies: cognitive (Bloom), affective (Krathwohl), and psychomotor (Simpson). This is consistent with the three-taxonomy framework proposed in TA and developed in HFT and SST.

## Cognitive Core — What Every Companion Must Know
- Scripture application in depth. Not technical mastery, but deep and current familiarity. The Navigators' scripture-memory discipline is a strong means of entry; other means include long-term engagement with reflective Bible study practices, such as the PROAPT method described in HFT and MSFIG.
- How formation actually happens. Change is slow, transformative trust in God is a nonlinear process, frequently involving regression before advance, and is activated by the Holy Spirit rather than by the facilitator's technique. A Companion who expects quick linear progress will either overstate their own role or become discouraged when the pilgrim's journey does not cooperate with the Companion's schedule.
- Self-knowledge. Their own wounds, patterns, shadows, and gold. A Companion who does not know their own inner landscape of patterns, their shadows from wounds and gold, will enact and project them unconsciously on the pilgrim, usually without realizing it.
- A clear boundary between their role and licensed professional counseling. When to refer, to whom, and how to make the referral without shaming the pilgrim.
- The specific framework of whichever mode(s) they practice. A Companion practicing spiritual direction needs the Ignatian framework. A Companion practicing TPM-style work needs TPM theology and discernment. A Companion doing group facilitation needs to manage process within a container theory and have experienced a range of processes that they can draw on as tools in their inventory, each recommended for helping others see themselves, others, and God with fewer or no hindrances.
- The IJH framework itself. What TA proposes about the inner person, what HFT and SST propose about heart and soul/spirit development, and how the Affective Taxonomy stage of the Companion affects what they can and cannot do. That the four modes hold together yet can be applied differentially offers a lifelong process of developing competency in each.
## Affective Core — What Every Companion Must Be
- A Christ-centered life of their own, not borrowed from the curriculum. The Companion cannot lead the pilgrim into territory the Companion has not walked.
- Affective Level 3 minimum in trust of scripture, measured as described in HFT. A Companion at Level 2 for whom scripture is interesting but not yet personally binding cannot facilitate Level 3 work in another person because they cannot model what they have not done. This is the developmental floor of the role. It is not optional and cannot be finessed. HFT provides the operational definition.
- Humility as the agent of change. The Companion is not the agent; the Holy Spirit is. The Companion creates conditions; the Spirit does the work. A Companion who forgets this becomes either overinvested in specific outcomes or subtly manipulative in pursuit of them.
- Capacity to sit with pain, silence, ambiguity, and slow process without rescuing. Rescuing is how well-meaning Companions interrupt the Spirit's work. The discipline of staying present without fixing is a foundational affective competency developed through practice and simply taught cognitively.
- Ongoing attending to their own shadows. The Companion's unexamined interior does not disappear. It can be enacted on or triggered by the pilgrim. Ongoing spiritual direction, supervision, or a disciplined peer group is the mechanism by which the Companion keeps their own material in their own awareness rather than outside of it.
- Themselves in an ongoing formation relationship. Every Companion has a Companion. Every director has a director. This is non-negotiable. A Companion who themselves is not being companioned has stopped growing, and a Companion who has stopped growing is heading for a ditch.
## Psychomotor Core — What Every Companion Must Do
- Active listening at depth — tracking content, emotion, and Spirit-movement simultaneously. This is the most fundamental skill, the hardest, and the one that cannot be faked. Reflecting back on what was heard engenders clarity.
- Asking open questions rather than questions that require the right answers. A well-formed, open question, a non-judgmental one, opens the pilgrim's interior, and an answer often closes it, even when the answer is correct. The simplest, “Can you tell me more about that?” Most untrained Companions offload questions that require direct answers. Mature Companions ask more open questions (not requiring yes or no answers) and answer their pilgrims' questions with reflection, affirmation, or sometimes not at all.
- Comfort with silence and skillful use of silence. Silence after a pilgrim's disclosure is not empty space to be filled; it is often where the Spirit is doing the work. Learning not to fill with answers or even talking is a learned skill. Eyes can be the window to the soul.
- Real-time discernment, knowing when to intervene, when to let the moment continue, when to redirect, when to invite deeper exploration. This is the integrative competency that Level 3 of the role discusses most directly.
- Prayerful attention throughout the session. The Companion is praying inside, even while speaking; listening, even while asking. These bifurcated yet integrated connections to the Holy Spirit and to the other person are a discipline developed through practice.
- Reflection, notes, and supervision between sessions. The work does not end when the pilgrim leaves. The Companion reviews what happened, what the Spirit seemed to be doing, what the Companion themselves was doing that helped or hindered, and brings the results of that reflection into their own supervision.
- Confidentiality is non-negotiable, held with the same discipline that a clinician's confidentiality is held, with the specific exceptions, such as harm to self or others, as a legal requirement, are clearly understood in advance.
These are the common core binding together all four traditions. A Companion who has developed all of the above is most of the way to being functional in any of the four modes. What each tradition adds on top of the core is mode-specific and less extensive than the core itself.
