# Part IV: Scripture Figures as Case Studies

The entries in this section do not follow standard bibliographic citation format, as the ‘sources’ are biblical texts rather than secondary literature. Each entry identifies the relevant scripture passage(s), provides a brief identification of the figure, summarizes the case study content, and explains the application to the LotS framework. Scripture references follow the English Standard Version (ESV) unless otherwise noted.

## Barnabas
**Author: **Barnabas (also called Joseph, a Levite from Cyprus) was one of the early church leaders at Antioch and a close companion of Paul. Acts 13:1–3 describes the Antioch church in a season of worship and fasting during which the Holy Spirit spoke specifically to the gathered community: ‘Set apart for me Barnabas and Saul for the work to which I have called them.’ The community prayed, fasted, and sent them out.

**Summary: **The Acts 13 passage is a case study in communal hearing: the revelation came not to an individual in private prayer but to the worshiping and fasting community together. The commission was communal (the Spirit addressed the group), the discernment was communal (they prayed and fasted in response), and the sending was communal (the church laid hands on them and sent them). This is hearing in community rather than isolated individual hearing.

**Application: **The Barnabas and Paul commission is cited in Vol 1 and Vol 2 as the paradigmatic example of the Community Dimension of the Obedience Channel (Vol 1, Exp. 6) and the Container Law (Vol 2, Exp. 8). The community was safe (covenanted), present (gathered in worship), clear (fasting), and intentional (submitted to God’s direction) — all four container conditions were met. The result was specific prophetic revelation and missional commissioning. This is the LotS project’s model for how community amplifies rather than replaces individual hearing.

## The Centurion
**Author: **The Roman Centurion who approaches Jesus requesting healing for his servant is a figure that Jesus himself identifies as exhibiting extraordinary faith: ‘Truly, I tell you, with no one in Israel have I found such faith.’ The Centurion’s faith is expressed specifically through his understanding of delegated authority: he tells Jesus he is a man ‘under authority’ with soldiers under him, and that therefore Jesus need only ‘say the word’ and the healing will occur.

**Summary: **The Centurion’s logic is precise: he understands that authority operates through a hierarchy, that one who is properly submitted to authority above them can exercise effective authority over those below them, and that Jesus’ authority over sickness operates the same way. His faith is therefore not simply an emotional trust in Jesus’ goodness but a structural understanding of how spiritual authority works. Jesus commends this understanding as the model of faith.

**Application: **The Centurion is one of the five primary scriptural data points for spiritual force in Vol 3, Exp. 3. He is also the central case study in Vol 1, Exp. 7 (Spiritual Authority — The Force Multiplier). The Centurion’s logic is used to construct the Spiritual Authority law: authority alignment — being properly submitted to the authority above and thereby exercising appropriate authority over those below — is a force multiplier for spiritual effectiveness. This is the Vol 1 Spiritual Authority law expressed as a force-multiplier in the Vol 3 provisional spiritual force equation.

## Elijah
**Author: **Elijah was a ninth-century BC prophet of Israel, widely regarded as the paradigmatic Old Testament prophet. His confrontation with the prophets of Baal on Mount Carmel (1 Kings 18) and his seven-prayer sequence for rain (1 Kgs. 18:41–45) are among the most dramatic accounts of prayer and spiritual power in scripture. Jas. 5:17–18 explicitly references Elijah’s prayer for rain as an example of effective prayer, noting that Elijah was ‘a man with a nature like ours.’

**Summary: **The seven-prayer sequence — in which Elijah sends his servant to look for rain six times before the servant reports a cloud the size of a man’s hand on the seventh — is a case study in resonance accumulation: the spiritual energy of prayer does not dissipate between attempts but accumulates. The phrase in Jas. 5:16 that prayer is effective when it is ‘working’ or ‘active’ (ESV: ‘as it is working’) connects directly to the Elijah account and to the Vol 3 resonance framework.

**Application: **Elijah’s seven-prayer sequence is one of the five primary scriptural data points for spiritual force in Vol 3, Exp. 3 and is the primary case study for Vol 1, Exp. 8 (Prayer as a Resonance Phenomenon). The sequence is used to construct the Resonance Amplification law: prayer energy accumulates rather than dissipating, and the Vol 3 provisional spiritual force equation includes a resonance amplification function h() drawn from the Elijah data point. The Elijah/Elisha double-portion anointing pattern (Vol 1) also illustrates generational transmission of spiritual authority.

## Elisha
**Author: **Elisha was the prophetic successor of Elijah, who requested and received a double portion of Elijah’s spirit (2 Kgs. 2:9–14). The double-portion request and its fulfillment — confirmed by Elijah’s mantle falling to Elisha and the waters parting at Elisha’s command — is the Old Testament’s clearest account of prophetic succession and the transmission of spiritual anointing across generations.

**Summary: **The Elijah/Elisha relationship illustrates two principles simultaneously: the transmissibility of spiritual authority and anointing across a relational/generational connection, and the importance of persistent proximity and pursuit (Elisha refused to leave Elijah’s side throughout the journey to his translation). The double portion — Elisha performed approximately twice as many recorded miracles as Elijah — suggests that spiritual authority can be transmitted and even amplified in succession.

**Application: **Vol 1 references the Elijah/Elisha pattern in the discussion of anointing and generational transmission as a speculative law area and as an illustration of how spiritual authority operates across relational and generational lines. This is connected to the broader Spiritual Authority law (Vol 1, Exp. 7): authority aligns not just vertically (under God) but relationally (in mentoring and succession relationships). The Elisha case study opens the question of whether spiritual authority and anointing are transferable through covenant relationships.

## Nicodemus
**Author: **Nicodemus was a Pharisee and a ‘ruler of the Jews’ — a member of the Sanhedrin and presumably a senior teacher of Israel. His nighttime conversation with Jesus (John 3) is one of the most theologically rich exchanges in the Gospels and includes Jesus’ rebuke: ‘Are you the teacher of Israel and yet you do not understand these things?’ This rebuke presupposes that Nicodemus *should* have been able to understand how things work in the Spirit.

**Summary: **Jesus’ rebuke of Nicodemus is not about his piety or devotion — Nicodemus was a serious student of scripture and a respected teacher. The rebuke is specifically about his failure to understand the mechanics of the Spirit: how one is born again, how the Spirit moves, what the structural relationships are between the natural and spiritual worlds. Jesus implies that this understanding is available, expected of a teacher of Israel, and that Nicodemus’ failure to possess it is a failure of investigation.

**Application: **The Nicodemus exchange is the single most important scriptural anchor for the entire LotS project. It appears in the very first paragraph of Vol 1’s introduction as the motivating text: if the teacher of Israel should have known how the Spirit works, then those who have the full canon, the indwelling Holy Spirit, and two thousand years of the church’s experience should be able to see deeper. This establishes the LotS project’s epistemological posture — not arrogance but informed humility — and grounds the investigation in a dominical expectation that the laws of the Spirit are knowable.

## Paul (Saul of Tarsus)
**Author: **Paul (born Saul of Tarsus) was the apostle to the Gentiles, the author of at least thirteen New Testament epistles, and arguably the most influential human figure in the development of Christian theology. His conversion on the Damascus road, his missionary journeys, and his articulation of justification by faith, the body of Christ, and spiritual gifts shaped the trajectory of the entire Christian tradition. He is referenced in the LotS volumes specifically in the Acts 13 commissioning account alongside Barnabas.

**Summary: **The Acts 13:1–3 account of Paul’s commissioning is discussed under the Barnabas entry above. Additionally, Paul’s New Testament letters provide the primary scriptural grounding for many of the LotS laws: Rom. 10:17 (faith from hearing), 1 Corinthians 13 (faith/hope/love structure), Jas. 5:16 (effective prayer), and 1 Thess. 5:21 (test everything) are all Pauline or associated with Pauline letters and are central to the LotS framework.

**Application: **Paul is referenced across all four volumes as an apostolic authority whose letters provide primary scriptural grounding for the LotS laws. The specific Acts 13 commissioning account is used in Vol 1 (Exp. 6) and Vol 2 (Exp. 8) to illustrate communal hearing and the container conditions for prophetic reception. Paul’s own description of his apostolic calling and the mechanics of his relationship with the Spirit provides important data for the LotS project’s understanding of how authority, obedience, and revelation interact.

## Samuel
**Author: **Samuel was the last judge of Israel and the first of the classical prophets, anointed to speak God’s word to the nation at a pivotal moment in Israel’s history. His rebuke of Saul in 1 Samuel 15 — ‘Has the Lord as great delight in burnt offerings and sacrifices, as in obeying the voice of the Lord? Behold, to obey is better than sacrifice’ — is one of the most direct statements in scripture on the relationship between obedience and acceptable worship.

**Summary: **Samuel’s rebuke identifies the negative case of the Obedience Channel: disobedience to God’s explicit instruction — even when accompanied by religious activity (the sacrifice of what was forbidden) — constitutes rejection of God’s word and results in the withdrawal of His blessing and anointing. The severity of the consequence (the loss of the kingdom for Saul) establishes how seriously the obedience-channel connection is taken in scripture.

**Application: **Samuel’s rebuke of Saul is cited in Vol 1, Exp. 7 as the ‘negative case’ study for the Spiritual Authority law: what happens when authority is misaligned. Saul’s disobedience — substituting his own judgment about what seemed like a better outcome for explicit obedience to God’s instruction — demonstrates that authority alignment flows through the obedience channel. When the obedience channel closes through active disobedience, spiritual authority is forfeited. The Samuel/Saul account provides the clearest scriptural grounding for why obedience is structural rather than merely moral.

## The Woman with the Issue of Blood
**Author: **The unnamed woman in Mark 5 who had suffered from a hemorrhage for twelve years and spent all she had on physicians without improvement reached out in the crowd and touched the fringe of Jesus’ garment. Jesus immediately perceived that ‘power had gone out from him’ and turned to ask who had touched him. The woman, trembling, came forward, and Jesus told her, ‘your faith has made you well.’

**Summary: **This account is notable for two features: first, Jesus’ perception that power (Greek: dynamis) had gone out from him directionally — flowing from Him toward the woman in response to her faith — implies that spiritual force has directionality, conservation properties, and a mechanism of transfer. Second, the account identifies the woman’s faith as the activating condition for the power transfer, consistent with the LotS project’s faith-force relationship.

**Application: **The Woman with the Issue of Blood is one of the five primary scriptural data points for spiritual force in Vol 3, Exp. 3. Her account specifically provides the evidence for the directional and conservation dimensions of the Vol 3 spiritual force equation: power flows out from the source (Jesus) toward the recipient (the woman), is perceived as having left the source, and is triggered by the faith of the recipient. This is the data point that most directly supports the claim that spiritual force has quantitative properties analogous to physical force.
