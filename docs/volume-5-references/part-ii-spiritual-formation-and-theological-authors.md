# Part II: Spiritual Formation and Theological Authors

## Blackaby, Henry T., and Claude V. King
*Blackaby, Henry T., and Claude V. King. Experiencing God: Knowing and Doing the Will of God. Nashville: LifeWay Press, 1990; revised edition, Nashville: B&H Publishing, 2008.*

**Author: **Henry Blackaby (b. 1935) is a Canadian-born Baptist pastor and teacher whose study Experiencing God became one of the most widely used discipleship resources in evangelical Christianity in the 1990s and 2000s. His framework is organized around seven realities of experiencing God, the most distinctive of which is: God is always at work around you; watch to see where God is working, and join Him there. This framework is explicitly testimonial and communal rather than systematic, and has been used in small groups across denominational lines.

**Summary: **In *Experiencing God*, Blackaby and King develop a seven-realities framework for responsive discipleship, the most distinctive claim being that God is always at work around the disciple and the disciple’s task is to watch for where God is working and join Him there. The work is explicitly testimonial and communal rather than systematic, and has been used in small groups across denominational lines for over three decades.

**Application: **Blackaby is not cited by name in the IJH volumes, but his “join God where He is working” framework is so closely parallel to the LotS miracle structure — particularly Vol 3 Exploration 8’s two-stage model (noticing, then responding in obedience) — that readers familiar with Experiencing God will recognize it immediately. The Vol 2 Training Plan’s morning practice (“Father, what are You up to today? Where will I see You?”) reflects the same posture. Vol 1’s Obedience Channel law and Blackaby’s “joining God where He is already working” are, in the LotS taxonomy, the same operational law stated in different registers. I acknowledge this connection so that readers see how the LotS project both builds on and goes further than the Blackaby framework, adding the quantitative and diagnostic dimensions that Experiencing God does not develop.

## Bennett, Rita
**Author: **Rita Bennett (1924–2012) was an American author and speaker who, with her husband Dennis Bennett, was a pioneering figure in the charismatic renewal movement within mainline Protestantism. She wrote and taught extensively on inner healing prayer, particularly addressing the healing of memories, emotional wounds, and the integration of emotional health with spiritual life.

**Summary: **Bennett’s work in inner healing focuses on the role of prayer and the Holy Spirit in addressing emotional wounds that block spiritual growth and intimacy with God. Her approach draws on the conviction that the Holy Spirit can access and heal specific memories and emotional injuries that remain active as blockages in a person’s spiritual and emotional functioning. This work preceded and influenced later developments in healing prayer ministry.

**Application: **Bennett is mentioned in Vol 2 as one of the sources through which the tools for the LotS emotional knot work came to the author — specifically in the tradition of inner healing prayer that addresses grief, shame, fear, and anger knots. The Payne/Bennett tradition is cited as a primary lineage for the inner healing approaches described in Vol 2’s Tool Map (Exp. 6). Bennett’s contribution is primarily to the practical healing dimension of the LotS formation approach rather than to its theoretical framework.

## Bilgere, Dmitri
**Author: **Dmitri is the author of “Gateways to God” (2013), along with multiple trainings and workshops. His personal story is very rich. Since 1989, Dmitri Bilgere has been helping people care for their hearts so they are able to take the daily, excellent actions that move them toward the life they long for. In 1991, he cofounded Shadow Work seminars as one of the original four facilitators. Through 1997, he co-led Shadow Work weekend seminars all over the USA, as well as in places as far-flung as Hundred Mile House, Canada; Worcester, England; and Cape Town, South Africa. He also co-led Shadow Work Basic, Advanced, and "Container Building" facilitation trainings -- some of them up to 10 days in length -- in the United States and Canada. In 1994, he began leading the Inner King Training, which he has co-led ever since across the USA. In 1998, Dmitri's book, "Beyond the Blame Game: Creating Compassion and Ending the Sex War in Your Life," was published. John Lee, author of the best-selling book "The Flying Boy," called it "a must read." It has been used as a text in classes at the University of Michigan, Pierce College, Pacific Lutheran University, and Texas Wesleyan University. Dmitri has also spent a total of more than four months in South Africa running workshops and coaching people. During his 1997 visit, he ran South Africa's first-ever men's weekend at a remote cabin in the Drakensburg mountains. Since the early 2000s, Dmitri has been working with Christian and non-Christian group, helping people truly experience the love, blessing and mercy of God.

**Summary:** Through Scripture, stories, examples and exercises, “Gateways to God” reveals a pathway through the five Christian Gateways to a life of God’s love and God’s mercy—not just as an idea, but as a continuous, moment-by-moment experience.

You’ll discover:

- How to seek God’s love through your problems—rather than in spite of them.

- How to receive God’s gift of grace—rather than feeling bad about Christ’s suffering that paid for that gift.

- How to experience the flow of God’s love for you—rather than living from a “Christian Checklist” of what you should say, do, and feel.

- How to be made new by repentance—rather than suffering unending remorse.- How to overflow with mercy—rather than living as if parts of you are too terrible for God to forgive.

Gateways to God takes Christians on a daring new journey of living life every moment from the experience of God’s love.

**Application:** Dmitri’s contribution to my personal spiritual journey is without measure. I reference this book because it directly applies to the practice of hearing and obeying God and to cleaning up the sins and weights we carry. I have attended every workshop that I could that Dmitri has led and have been enriched by the clarity and directness of his teaching. Shadow work processes, Inner King Training, and self-facilitation trainings are just a few that had a particularly large impact on me in my journey.

## Bramer, Paul
*Bramer, Paul. “Effective Christian Education: Challenging the Norms.” Christian Education Journal (Series 3) 5, no. 1 (2008): 82–99. See also contributions to Zondervan Handbook of Christian Education. Grand Rapids: Zondervan, 2015.*

**Author: **Paul Bramer is a scholar in Christian education and spiritual formation who has written on the integration of affective and cognitive domains in Christian learning contexts. His work engages the gap between knowledge acquisition and genuine formation — between knowing the truth and being transformed by it — and draws on the Bloom taxonomy family to describe what holistic Christian education requires.

**Summary: **Bramer argues that Christian education has had a tendency to focus on cognitive knowledge acquisition, while Christian formation much more deliberately brings in the affective domain. His work develops the Bloom-taxonomy-family case for what a genuinely holistic Christian education requires: simultaneous engagement of the cognitive, affective, and psychomotor dimensions, not cognitive content delivery alone.

**Application: **Vol 3 Exploration 9 cites Bramer’s observation that “Christian education has had a tendency to focus on cognitive knowledge acquisition, while Christian formation much more deliberately brings in the affective domain.” This anchors the three-taxonomy argument in Vol 3 — that genuine movement toward the Glory attractor requires simultaneous engagement of the cognitive, affective, and psychomotor dimensions. Bramer provides scholarly grounding for what the LotS project describes experientially: that the Saturday morning workshop was always a formation community, not a lecture series, because formation requires more than the cognitive domain can supply.

## Eldredge, John
**Author: **John Eldredge (b. 1960) is an American Christian author, counselor, and founder of Wild at Heart Ministries in Colorado Springs, Colorado. His books have reached millions of readers worldwide. His central concern across his body of work is the recovery of the heart — from the wounds that suppress it, the false self that manages around it, and the enemy who contests it — and its restoration to the life Jesus described in John 10:10. His work draws on narrative theology, inner healing prayer, and a careful reading of the masculine heart’s design desires as reflections of the image of God.

**Primary Works Referenced in LotS: **Eldredge, John. *Wild at Heart: Discovering the Secret of a Man’s Soul.* Nashville: Thomas Nelson, 2001.

Eldredge, John. Waking the Dead: The Glory of a Heart Fully Alive. Nashville: Thomas Nelson, 2003.

Eldredge, John, and Stasi Eldredge. Captivating: Unveiling the Mystery of a Woman’s Soul. Nashville: Thomas Nelson, 2005. (Referenced in Vol 1 Supplemental Exploration on the three design desires, for the parallel discussion of the feminine heart.)

**Summary: **Wild at Heart develops three interlocking arguments directly relevant to this project. First, the masculine heart carries three structural desires — a battle to fight, an adventure to live, and a beauty to rescue — that reflect the image of the God who made it. These are not personality preferences; they are design features. Second, every man receives a wound, typically early and from someone he needed to be safe, and that wound delivers a verdict against the central question of the masculine heart (Do I have what it takes?). The unaddressed wound produces a false self — a coherent alternative identity organized around managing the wound rather than living from the Spirit-led self. Third, the false self can be lived in for decades while the man maintains outward spiritual compliance; what breaks its hold is not behavioral reform but the Father speaking a specific true answer to the question the wound lodged.

Waking the Dead extends these arguments in two directions particularly relevant to Vol 2. First, it makes the theological case that the heart — restored in Christ — is fundamentally good, and that this is the most contested truth in the Christian life, because everything the enemy does is aimed at getting the man to disbelieve or ignore it. Second, it provides the most useful treatment I have found of the enemy’s strategy: not the dramatic temptation to gross sin, but the patient campaign to keep the heart suppressed, isolated, misdirected, or convinced that healing is unavailable. These two arguments together provide the theological and spiritual warfare framework for Vol 2 Explorations 2A and 2B.

**Application: **Vol 1, Supplemental Exploration: The three structural desires of the masculine heart are treated as design-level structural laws, grounded in the image-of-God argument of Gen. 1:26–27.

Vol 2, Introduction: Eldredge’s work is acknowledged alongside Dave Smith and Leanne Payne as part of the formative lineage of this project’s inner healing framework.

Vol 2, Exploration 2A: The wound-and-false-self framework is developed as a formal Diagnostic Law (the Identity Blockage Law), drawing primarily on Wild at Heart, chapters 3–5.

Vol 2, Exploration 2B: The enemy’s strategy against the heart, as developed in Waking the Dead, chapters 9–11, provides the spiritual warfare framework for the adversarial dimension of knot formation and persistence.

Vol 2, Exploration 5 (callout): The core question of the masculine heart (Do I have what it takes?) and the Father’s answer to it are connected to the God-Connect in the Four Connects sequence.

**A Personal Note: **Eldredge writes from the same conviction that drives this entire project — that the life Jesus invites us into is far larger than the life most of us are actually living, and that the gap is addressable. His books are not difficult reading, but they are searching. I have rarely given Wild at Heart to a man without watching something in him recognize the territory before he could argue about it. That recognition is itself a kind of evidence. I recommend both volumes without reservation, as companions to the more systematic framework developed here.

## Ignatius of Loyola
**Author: **Ignatius of Loyola (1491–1556) was a Spanish priest, theologian, and founder of the Society of Jesus (Jesuits). His *Spiritual Exercises* is one of the most influential works on Christian spiritual direction and formation in the history of the church. Ignatius developed the Examen — a structured daily prayer practice of reviewing the day in God’s presence — as a central formation discipline within his larger framework of discernment and interior freedom.

**Summary: **The Ignatian Examen is a structured five-step prayer practice typically done at the end of each day: becoming aware of God’s presence, reviewing the day with gratitude, attending to emotions and movements, choosing one feature of the day to pray about, and looking toward the following day. The Examen trains attentiveness to the movements of the Spirit in ordinary experience and supports the development of discernment over time.

**Application: **The Ignatian Examen is referenced in Vol 4’s Individual Testing Protocol as one of the qualitative assessment approaches used in the LotS research design: specifically, the Examen applied to a full year of documented spiritual journey is proposed as part of the Month 12 endpoint assessment. This places the Examen within the LotS framework as both a formation practice and a reflective assessment instrument, consistent with the LotS project’s commitment to practices that are simultaneously formative and documentable.

## John of the Cross, Saint
**Author: **Saint John of the Cross (Juan de la Cruz, 1542–1591) was a Spanish mystic, Carmelite friar, and Doctor of the Church, widely regarded as one of the greatest spiritual directors and poets in the Christian tradition. His *Dark Night of the Soul* is the classic theological account of the purifying process through which God draws the soul from attachment to spiritual consolations and self toward pure union with Himself.

**Summary: **John of the Cross describes the Dark Night as a period in which a person’s accustomed sense of God’s presence, spiritual satisfaction, and consolation is withdrawn, producing what feels like spiritual desolation and abandonment. He argues that this withdrawal is not punishment or spiritual failure but the mechanism by which God purifies attachment to the gifts of God rather than God Himself. The Dark Night is a stage transition — a necessary suffering — on the path to deeper union.

**Application: **Saint John of the Cross is cited in Vols 2 and 3 to provide classical theological grounding for the LotS claim that trials and apparent desolation function as formation engines rather than formation interruptions. His description of the Dark Night maps directly onto the LotS framework’s account of disorientation phases (following Brueggemann) and the suffering-as-stage-transition mechanism (LotS-F3). Vol 3 cites John of the Cross alongside Brueggemann and the MOVE study as converging evidence for the centrality of suffering in spiritual development.

## Lewis, C.S.
*Lewis, C.S. The Last Battle. New York: Macmillan, 1956. See also Mere Christianity. New York: Macmillan, 1952, and The Weight of Glory and Other Addresses. New York: Macmillan, 1949.*

**Author: **C.S. Lewis (1898–1963) was a British literary scholar, Christian apologist, and author who stands as one of the most influential Christian voices of the twentieth century. His theological writing was produced from an Anglican sensibility that engaged both Catholic and Protestant streams, and his fiction — particularly the Chronicles of Narnia — gave imaginative form to ideas he explored more systematically in his apologetic work. Philosophically trained at Oxford, Lewis was unusual among popular Christian writers in his willingness to reason carefully about the nature of joy, desire, moral experience, and the afterlife.

**Summary: **Across the works cited here — *The Last Battle*, *Mere Christianity*, and *The Weight of Glory* — Lewis develops three arguments particularly relevant to this project: that the deeper one moves into God the more real and full life becomes (the “further up and further in” direction of Aslan’s country); that there is a qualitative difference between the things God does for us and the enjoyment of God himself; and that human longing for the transcendent (Sehnsucht) is itself a data point about the kind of reality we inhabit.

**Application: **The Intro closes with Lewis’s phrase from The Last Battle — “Further up and further in” — as the invitation into the IJH journey. In its original context the phrase is spoken as the characters move deeper into Aslan’s country after death, discovering that the new Narnia is more real and more full than anything they had experienced before. This captures exactly what the LotS project reaches for: not a thinning down of Christian experience into principles, but an expansion — a life that is fuller, more real, more alive the closer it comes to its source. Lewis’s concept of Sehnsucht (longing for the transcendent), developed in The Weight of Glory, runs as an implicit undercurrent through the Beauty axis of Vol 3’s TFT framework. His distinction in Mere Christianity between “the things God does for us” and “the enjoyment of God himself” parallels the LotS distinction between operating in the laws as a spiritual technology versus the laws as the description of a relationship.

## Payne, Leanne
**Author: **Leanne Payne (1932–2015) was an American author, teacher, and founder of Pastoral Care Ministries, widely regarded as one of the most significant voices in the modern inner healing prayer movement. Drawing on C.S. Lewis, Charles Williams, and the Anglican sacramental tradition alongside clinical insights, she developed a framework for healing prayer that addresses gender identity wounds, shame, and the recovery of wholeness in Christ.

**Summary: **Payne’s approach to inner healing prayer centers on the practice of listening prayer — bringing specific wounds, memories, and lies to God in prayer and listening for His response — as the primary mechanism for emotional and spiritual healing. She emphasized the necessity of working with a trained prayer companion, the reality of demonic accusation in shame and fear wounds, and the role of the body and imagination in receiving God’s healing presence.

**Application: **Payne is explicitly named in Vol 2 as one of the primary sources through which the inner healing tools in the LotS framework came to the author. The Payne/Bennett tradition is cited in the introduction to Vol 2 and in the Tool Map (Exp. 6) as the lineage from which the inner healing approaches to grief, shame, and fear knots are drawn. Payne’s emphasis on listening prayer as the healing mechanism is consistent with the LotS project’s broader claim that hearing God’s voice is the primary formation mechanism.

## Nee, Watchman
*Nee, Watchman. The Spiritual Man. New York: Christian Fellowship Publishers, 1968. See also The Release of the Spirit. Indianapolis: Sure Foundation, 1965.*

**Author: **Watchman Nee (1903–1972) was a Chinese Christian teacher and church planter whose written work became foundational in evangelical and charismatic circles worldwide, particularly on the subject of the tripartite nature of the person. Writing from within a broadly Reformed and charismatic framework shaped by the Plymouth Brethren tradition and the Chinese indigenous church movement, Nee distinguished between spirit, soul, and body with a precision that has influenced several generations of spiritual formation teachers.

**Summary: ***The Spiritual Man* develops a tripartite anthropology in which spirit, soul, and body are distinct but related dimensions of the person. Nee’s central claim is that spiritual life begins with the regenerated spirit, not with mental assent or behavioral change, and that the mature Christian life involves the human spirit leading the soul and the body into alignment with God. His account of the contest between the spirit (oriented to God) and the body (oriented to the world) with the mind as the contested middle ground has shaped several generations of formation teaching.

**Application: **Vol 1 Exploration 2 cites Nee directly on the tension that arises when the human spirit is listening to God while the body remains oriented to the world and the mind becomes the contested middle ground. His tripartite anthropology is the closest historical precedent for the nested person structure developed in that Exploration. His insistence that spiritual life begins with the regenerated spirit, not with mental assent or behavioral change, aligns exactly with the inside-out transformation principle that runs through Vols 1 and 2. The Spiritual Man is the key text, though it is long and demanding; readers may find The Release of the Spirit a more accessible entry point to the same core ideas.

## Nemeck, Francis Kelly, and Marie Theresa Combe
*Nemeck, Francis Kelly, and Marie Theresa Combe. The Way of Spiritual Direction. Collegeville, MN: Liturgical Press, 1985.*

**Author****s****: **Francis Kelly Nemeck (1931–2013) was a Carmelite priest and theologian; Marie Theresa Combe was his collaborator in a series of works on the contemplative life and spiritual direction. Their writing comes from within the Catholic mystical tradition, particularly the Carmelite stream, but has been used broadly across denominational lines by people seeking to understand the nature and practice of spiritual accompaniment.

**Summary: ***The Way of Spiritual Direction* develops a Carmelite-tradition account of spiritual direction as accompaniment rather than instruction and as discernment rather than advice. Nemeck and Combe argue that the director’s task is to help the directee learn to hear from God for themselves, not to direct their lives; and they describe the practical conditions (qualification of the director, disposition of the directee, rhythm of meeting) under which that accompaniment bears fruit.

**Application: **Vol 2 Exploration 10 recommends The Way of Spiritual Direction as the best reference for understanding what genuine spiritual direction looks like and how to identify a qualified director — noting that spiritual direction is “a lost art in the Protestant community” and that the author went years between finding directors. For IJH readers considering a spiritual director as part of the training plan, this is the most practical starting recommendation. The Nemeck-Combe framework for what a director does (accompaniment, not instruction; discernment, not advice) aligns well with the LotS understanding of the hearing process as something that develops in relationship.

## Prince, Derek
**Author: **Derek Prince (1915–2003) was a British-born Bible teacher, author, and international Christian minister regarded as one of the twentieth century’s most influential Bible expositors. A Cambridge-trained philosopher who came to faith during World War II, he taught extensively on foundational doctrines including faith, hope, love, healing, spiritual warfare, and the gifts of the Holy Spirit. His teaching ministry spanned six decades and reached millions of people through radio, books, and recordings.

The definition is important for the LotS project’s Third Exploration (Faith, Hope, and Love) in Vol 1.

The definition helps clarify how hope functions in the faith-generating cycle: not as uncertain wishing but as forward-looking confidence grounded in God’s demonstrated character. Identifying the correct definition is essential to tracing the causal relationships accurately.

## Tedeschi, Richard G., and Lawrence G. Calhoun
*Tedeschi, Richard G., and Lawrence G. Calhoun. Trauma and Transformation: Growing in the Aftermath of Suffering. Thousand Oaks, CA: SAGE Publications, 1995.*

**Author: **Richard Tedeschi and Lawrence Calhoun are psychologists at the University of North Carolina Charlotte who developed the concept of posttraumatic growth (PTG) — the positive psychological change that can emerge from the struggle with highly challenging life circumstances. Their work documents that a significant proportion of trauma survivors report increased personal strength, changed priorities, new possibilities, enhanced relationships, and richer spiritual or existential awareness as outcomes of navigating severe adversity. PTG is distinct from resilience (returning to baseline); it is genuine growth beyond the pre-crisis level.

**Summary: ***Trauma and Transformation* develops the concept of posttraumatic growth (PTG) — positive psychological change emerging from the struggle with highly challenging life circumstances. Tedeschi and Calhoun document that a significant proportion of trauma survivors report increased personal strength, changed priorities, new possibilities, enhanced relationships, and richer spiritual or existential awareness as outcomes of navigating severe adversity. PTG is distinct from resilience (returning to baseline); it is genuine growth beyond the pre-crisis level.

**Application: **Both Vol 2 Exploration 10 and Vol 3 Exploration 7 cite Tedeschi and Calhoun’s research in support of the “suffering as formation engine” argument — specifically, the claim that the Dark Night of the Soul and Brueggemann’s disorientation phase can produce greater capacity than the pre-suffering baseline. Their secular research provides empirical confirmation for what the Christian formation tradition has described theologically for centuries. For IJH readers skeptical of the theological argument, the Tedeschi-Calhoun data offers a different kind of grounding. For the Vol 4 testing program, their PTG scale is potentially relevant as one component of the outcome measurement battery.

## Tittle, John G.
**Author: **John G. Tittle (JGT) is the author of the Intentional Journey of the Heart (IJH) volumes and of the five companion Formation Documents (TA, HFT, SST, MSFIG, FC) included in this volume. This entry collects his five papers as a single author reference in Part II for ease of lookup; the full texts appear later in this volume in the “Reference Material Used in LotS” section.

It arrives at an integrated model in which the human spirit sits within the heart; the heart and the mind both sit within the soul; and the soul is expressed through the body. Each layer carries its own developmental logic and its own relationship to the Spirit’s work.

**Application: **The Theological Anthropology paper is cited in HFT and SST as the paper that “laid the conceptual foundation” for their taxonomies, and it is referenced in Vol 1’s Second Exploration (Spirit, Heart, Soul, and Body) as the source of the nested-containment model proposed there. ## Wesley, John
**Author: **John Wesley (1703–1791) was an Anglican clergyman, theologian, and the founder of Methodism. His theological method drew on four sources of authority — scripture, tradition, reason, and experience — which twentieth-century Methodist scholars labeled the Wesleyan Quadrilateral. Wesley himself never used this term, but the framework has become widely adopted as a description of his epistemological approach and is used broadly across evangelical and mainline Protestant theology.

**Summary: **The Wesleyan Quadrilateral identifies four sources and criteria for Christian theological reflection: scripture (the primary authority), tradition (the accumulated wisdom of the church), reason (logical coherence and critical reflection), and experience (personal and communal encounter with God). These four sources are understood to be in dialogue with each other, with scripture as the norming norm. The quadrilateral is not a framework of equal authorities but a description of how theological discernment actually works.

**Application: **Vol 4, Section 2 (Epistemological Foundations) notes that the LotS project’s four sources of authority — scripture, reason, tradition, and experience — map closely onto what has come to be called the Wesleyan Quadrilateral. This observation situates the LotS epistemological approach within a well-established theological tradition and provides methodological accountability: the testing program in Vol 4 draws on all four sources, with scripture as the norming authority and experience as the testing ground for the laws’ validity.
