## Abstract
This paper argues that the Affective Taxonomy of Krathwohl, Bloom, and Masia — describing how people progressively internalize values until those values become a stable, characteristic way of life — provides a defensible and scripturally coherent framework for describing the observable human side of spiritual formation. Grounded in the Parable of the Sower (Matt. 13:1–23; Mark 4:1–20), the taxonomy's five levels (Receiving, Responding, Valuing, Organization, Characterization) map to the progressive receptivity of the human heart to God's Word, with the 100-fold harvest representing Stage 5 Characterization. The framework is integrative (uniting individual and corporate formation), generative (reinterpreting preaching, worship, and suffering within a single model), and extensible (applicable across denominational and cultural contexts). The Holy Spirit remains the irreducible primary agent; the taxonomy describes the human side of that divine work, individually and collectively, in the fundamental spiritual discipline of hearing and obeying God.

## 1. Introductory Framing (Key Sources)
The paper navigates competing approaches to spiritual formation using David Powlison's three epistemic priorities: (1) establish what Scripture says; (2) understand the theory or discipline in depth, including its assumptions; (3) extract what aligns with scriptural truth and integrate appropriately ("Cure of Souls," 269–271). Four empirical/theoretical frameworks are evaluated against this grid:

Christian Schwarz, Natural Church Development (ChurchSmart, 1996; 7th ed. 2006): Eight quality characteristics of healthy churches — empowering leadership, gift-oriented ministry, passionate spirituality, effective structures, inspiring worship, holistic small groups, need-oriented evangelism, loving relationships — with holistic small groups as the single quality most correlated with overall health across cultures. Organizing framework: the Trinitarian Compass, which maps all NCD tools onto the three classical transcendentals (Truth, Goodness, Beauty) corresponding to the three persons of the Trinity — the same coordinate axes as TFT. Diagnostic logic: the minimum-factor strategy (Liebig’s Law applied to church health), identifying the rate-limiting quality characteristic as the first intervention target. The Energy Trilogy (2021–2022) deepens the theological foundation of the NCD framework by recovering the NT energeia vocabulary and grounding the energy paradigm in Paul’s letters. Survey database: 70,000+ churches, 84 countries, 112 denominations. Criticism (Ellas & Yeakley, JASCG 10:2, 1999): methodological concerns and lack of analytic transparency; (9Marks): hermeneutical concerns about use of biotic metaphors as interpretive frameworks for Scripture.

Richard Foster, Celebration of Discipline (HarperSanFrancisco, 1998): Groups classic spiritual disciplines into inner, outer, and corporate — useful taxonomy of practices but does not address motivation or internalization progression.

Paul Bramer, "Christian Formation: Tweaking the Paradigm" (CEJ, Fall 2007): Identifies that Christian education over-emphasizes cognitive acquisition; proposes the Affective Taxonomy as the more appropriate framework for formation. This paper builds directly on Bramer's proposal.

## 2. Theological Background (Compressed Summaries)
### 2.1 Old Testament Theology of Spiritual Formation
Key argument (Averbeck, Lowe): OT spiritual formation is primarily corporate, focused on forming Israel as "a kingdom of priests and a holy nation" (Exod. 19:6) through covenantal obedience; the Holy Spirit is the primary agent, working through life-giving, empowering, cleansing (Ezek. 36:25–28), and heart-renewing functions that point forward to NT fulfillment. Key OT-to-NT bridge: Averbeck notes the combination of divine activities in Ezekiel 36 constitutes the regenerating and renewing of hearts that both testaments describe, with Jesus applying it directly to Nicodemus (John 3:5–15). Critical distinction (Lowe): OT anointing for leadership and prophecy (Saul, David, Eli) served collective formation, not individual spiritual development — gifting is not the same as personal formation. Brueggemann's orientation–disorientation–new orientation framework (Psalms) provides the pastoral template for redemptive suffering as a formation engine; John of the Cross's Dark Night of the Soul (Bk. 1, Ch. 6) applies the same dynamic at the individual level: staying in the desert of dryness enables a depth of trust unavailable to those who avoid it.

### 2.2 New Testament Theology of Spiritual Formation
Key argument (Bock, Thompson, Morrow): The NT continues and expands the Spirit's formation work, now extending individually to each believer at Pentecost while remaining fundamentally corporate in purpose — the Spirit empowers not primarily for miracles but for a righteous, God-honoring life lived in community (Titus 2:11–14). The church as a countercultural community of radical diversity (Lydia, the Roman jailer, the slave girl — Acts 16) becomes the visible witness: Thompson notes Paul assumes the gospel's transforming power will attract outsiders without direct evangelism commands (Paul, 170). The Parable of the Sower (Matt. 13:1–23) appears in this context: Jesus knew not everyone would receive his words, and his remark in Mark 4:13 — "How then will you understand all the parables?" — identifies hearing and obeying as the foundational, meta-discipline of spiritual formation. Suffering as formation: Peter (1 Pet. 1:6–7), James (1:2–4), and Paul (Rom. 8:28) all treat disorientation (Brueggemann) as a formation mechanism, not an interruption.

### 2.3 Pauline Theology of Spiritual Formation
Key argument (Lowe & Lowe, Thompson): Paul's theology is irreducibly corporate — the individual believer apart from the community is inconceivable to Paul (Thompson, Paul, 56). Formation occurs through a "syn-" ecology of interdependence (Lowe & Lowe, Ecologies, 50–63), with gifting distributed across the body for collective edification, not individual distinction (Eph. 4:1–16; 1 Cor 12–14). Individual dimensions: Lowe & Lowe identify six developmental dimensions (physical, intellectual, emotional, social, moral, spiritual), visualized as a palm with spiritual formation as the integrating center; 1 Timothy 3 addresses the same six dimensions for leadership selection. Central formation challenge (Nee, The Spiritual Man, 29–32): the war between the human spirit (in contact with the Holy Spirit) and the body (in contact with the world) — whether we listen to God's voice or the world's is the perpetual struggle. Watchman Nee (Normal Christian Church Life, 115): denominational division fractures the local-universal church unity Paul assumed; the local church is all believers in a locality.

### 2.4 Thinking Theologically / Ecology of Spiritual Formation
Key argument (Lowe & Lowe, Ecologies): Jesus and Paul used nature parables because God works the same way to bring natural growth as he does spiritual growth — creation itself is God speaking (Rom. 1:19–21). Hearing and obeying is the foundational discipline: Deut. 28:1–68 ties all blessings and curses to hearkening; Jesus in John 15:3–17 grounds abiding in his love in keeping his commandments; Willard (Hearing God, 195) states the Word "enters the heart and from there is able to progressively transform the whole personality." The three levels of good soil (30-, 60-, 100-fold) point to progressive depths of internalization — Brueggemann's disorientation/suffering can act as fertilizer enabling movement from 30-fold toward 100-fold. MOVE and NCD empirical models: practices and identified stages are valuable, but both rely on single-point self-report surveys that can only capture Stage 1–2 behaviors, not the motivation and internalization of Stages 3–5.

## 3. The Central Argument: Affective Taxonomy Applied to Spiritual Formation
### 3.1 Theoretical Foundation
Source: Krathwohl, Bloom & Masia, Taxonomy of Educational Objectives: Affective Domain (David McKay, 1964), pp. 165–185.

Core claim (Bramer): Christian education has over-focused on cognitive acquisition; spiritual formation requires the affective domain — the progressive internalization of values.

Biblical grounding: The Parable of the Sower (Matt. 13:1–23) is the primary scriptural test of the taxonomy. Jesus' interpretive key in Mark 4:13 ("How then will you understand all the parables?") makes hearing and obeying a meta-discipline — the foundation for all spiritual discernment and all other disciplines.

Theological grounding: The taxonomy describes the human side of a Trinitarian work (Averbeck). The Holy Spirit is the primary agent; the taxonomy is descriptive, not prescriptive. Sudden and permanent changes of heart (sovereign Spirit work) can occur at any level. The taxonomy is humble about what it can measure: "The LORD sees, not as man sees" (1 Sam. 16:7).

Epistemic method: Powlison's three-step grid — (1) Scripture establishes the framework (Sower parable), (2) the secular taxonomy is examined on its own terms, (3) what aligns with scriptural truth is extracted and integrated.

### 3.3 Key Observations on the Taxonomy
Pharisee counter-example: The Pharisees scored perfectly on every Stage 2 behavior — attendance, tithing, prayer frequency, Scripture knowledge, public righteousness — yet Jesus declared tax collectors and prostitutes would enter the kingdom before them (Matt. 21:31). This is the fundamental danger of confusing behavioral compliance (Stage 2) with character transformation (Stage 5).

Humility marker: The closer one gets to Christ, the more aware one becomes of the gap. This mark of humility — the opposite of Pharisaical self-assurance — may be one of the major indicators of genuine progress.

The Holy Spirit's role at every transition: Each level shift requires the convicting work of the Spirit (John 14:8–11) and a cross-focused response. Formation is not simply progressive — it requires dying and rising.

Small group leadership constraint: A leader cannot help a group grow into Stage 5 if they have not been there themselves. Any new group will begin with a mix of people at different levels; the leader's own formation is the binding constraint.

Measurement challenge (and humility): The deeper levels are partially opaque to external observation by design. Measurement is possible (Hubbard, How to Measure Anything, 1–48) but requires clarity about what is being asked and appropriate humility about what instruments can and cannot detect.

## 4. A Trio of Taxonomies
Bloom's original cognitive taxonomy (1956; updated 2001, Anderson & Krathwohl) and the psychomotor taxonomy (Simpson, 1972) work alongside the Affective Taxonomy as three interdependent domains. Krathwohl emphasized (Affective Domain, 45–62) that cognitive and affective are inseparable: you cannot value what you do not understand, and understanding alone does not produce motivation to internalize.

Formation model: The three taxonomies operate as an integrated system.

•        Cognitive domain (preaching, teaching, Scripture study): Builds the mental model of who God is, what He has said, what is of value. Provides content for the affective domain to internalize.

•        Affective domain (internalization, motivation, heart transformation): The taxonomy proposed in this paper. Driven by the Spirit's convicting and renewing work. Describes why people pursue formation and how deeply they have internalized it.

•        Psychomotor domain (practices, liturgy, embodied worship): Structures the body and habits for formation. James K.A. Smith (Desiring the Kingdom, 17–18): rightly structured liturgy at physical and cognitive levels invites a heart response in the affective domain — an action in the cognitive/psychomotor to invite affective internalization.

Affective measurement gap: The affective taxonomy has been the slowest adopted in educational practice (Potgieter et al., Nurse Education in Practice 86, 2025) precisely because of measurement difficulty — not because it is unimportant, but because it is hardest to observe. This paper claims the difficulty is worth the effort for church leaders serious about disciple formation.

## 5. Testing the Model: Preaching and Worship
Preaching operates primarily in the cognitive domain: presenting correct doctrine about who God is and what Christ has done. Within the trio-taxonomy model, preaching creates the mental model of what is valuable and to be internalized — a cognitive invitation to see with spiritual eyes. Shields ("Preaching and Spiritual Formation," in Foundations, 254): transformation requires God's Word; "the one who preaches for transformation must study the Bible in such a way that there is a high degree of confidence that what the preacher says is also what God is saying." Importantly, Shields (250–251) applies this first to the preacher's own formation — the preacher must be transformed before delivering cognitive content. Lowe observation: most churches get the order wrong — starting with worship before the Word. Correct order: Word first, as training in hearing God's voice, then worship as the natural response.

Worship is the whole-person response to internalizing truth about who God is: loving God with heart, soul, mind, and strength (Mark 12:30). Smith (Desiring the Kingdom, 17–18): liturgy is a psychomotor/cognitive structure that invites people to desire the Kingdom — an embodied invitation to affective-level formation. Averbeck ("Worship and Spiritual Formation," in Foundations, 62–63): "Worship is about the presence of God and our engaging with that presence. Spiritual formation is about how to actually go about engaging with the presence of God, walking with him day by day... in worship, prayer, obedience, witness, spiritual disciplines." Taxonomy application: worship that remains at Stage 2 (attendance, following liturgy from obligation) is Pharisaical. Worship as formation operates at Stage 3 and above — moving from performing worship to entering worship from the heart.

### 5.3 Integration: Neither Alone Is Sufficient
Neither preaching nor worship alone produces Stage 5 formation. The Parable of the Sower makes clear that the heart's state determines what is received. The model's integrative claim: all spiritual disciplines can be examined as interactions with the three taxonomies, each supporting or hindering progressive internalization. The human role is to put ourselves in situations where we are willing to listen more deeply and obey more quickly — individually and corporately.

## 6. Integrating Challenges
### 6.1 Willard vs. Friesen: Quotidian Guidance
Willard (Hearing God, 276–280) proposes cultivating a continuous conversational relationship with God, including seemingly trivial daily decisions. Friesen (Decision Making and the Will of God, 82) critiques this as treating God mechanistically — mature believers should not need divine input on trivial matters. Taxonomy resolution: both critiques are correct at different levels. Willard's practice is training for movement from Stage 2 (doing it because an expert says to) to Stage 3 (doing it because I've found the payoff worth it). Friesen is critiquing Willard for not describing Stage 5 — but Willard is not claiming to describe Stage 5; he is describing a practice for earlier-stage formation. Brother Lawrence (Practice of the Presence) supports the same method: repetition until presence-awareness becomes automatic (Stage 5). The taxonomy turns the debate into an invitation to ongoing dialogue about which practices are most effective at each level.

### 6.2 Suffering as Formation Engine
Suffering is not an interruption of the formation journey — it is potentially its engine. Brueggemann (Spirituality of the Psalms, 8–12): the disorientation phase (lament psalms) always focuses on God, who can redeem even this. John of the Cross (Dark Night, Bk. 1, Ch. 6): the pilgrim must recognize the dark night and remain in it to receive what God has on the other side. Secular parallel: Tedeschi & Calhoun's posttraumatic growth (Psychological Inquiry 15:1, 2004) — positive change emerges from the struggle with highly challenging life crises, including increased appreciation for life, more meaningful relationships, richer spiritual life.

### 6.3 Non-Linear Progress: Spiral Rather Than Staircase
The original taxonomy implies linear step-by-step progression; real formation looks more like a spiral. A Stage 4 person suffering a tragic loss (e.g., death of a child) may appear to regress to Stages 1–2 — asking foundational questions about who God is and why they should even attend to his words. This should be understood as Brueggemann's disorientation: not regression but an invitation into a deeper relationship. The taxonomy needs extension to account for this: progress can be real even when it externally resembles reversal. This non-linearity is also why the affective domain is hard to measure — Potgieter et al. (2025) confirm this is the central challenge for adoption in educational practice. Hubbard's assurance (How to Measure Anything, 1–48): clarity about what is being asked makes measurement possible; the taxonomy provides that clarity.

## 7. Expansion Into Other Domains
### 7.1 Church-Wide Extensions
The individual and small-group taxonomy can extend to church-wide actions supporting formation. Preaching and liturgy can include church-wide teaching in practicing the presence, listening prayer, identifying God's voice, gifts identification and deployment. Both MOVE and NCD identify similar practices; their weakness is measurement (single-point self-report captures only Stage 1–2 behaviors). The Energy Trilogy represents Schwarz’s attempt to address this weakness at its root — by grounding NCD’s empirical findings in a theological account of how divine energy actually operates in the formation process, so that the framework can explain why certain practices correlate with outcomes, not merely document that they do. The taxonomy provides a framework for designing church programs at specific formation levels, not just delivering content.

### 7.2 Denominational and Cross-Cultural Extensions
Foster's six Streams of Living Water (Streams, 1998) represent recurring Holy Spirit revivals over 2,000 years of church history, each emphasizing different aspects of formation: Contemplative, Holiness, Charismatic, Social Justice, Evangelical, Incarnational. The Social Justice stream (liberation theology): adds recognition of how Jesus enters into suffering of the marginalized (Matt. 5:2–11). Orthodox/Catholic traditions: spiritual direction (Nemeck & Coombs, Way of Spiritual Direction, 83–84) is specifically about helping the pilgrim learn to hear from God for themselves — a direct application of Stage 3–5 formation. Asian Christian perspectives (Chan, Spiritual Theology; Nee, Normal Christian Life): the taxonomy's Western formulation would need cultural extension; cross-cultural dialogue about areas of agreement and disagreement could validate or correct the framework.

### 7.3 Possible Extensions and Open Questions
Stage 0 / Stage -1: Could there be a Stage 0 (hard heart, unwilling to engage) grounded in Prov 1:8 ("fear of the LORD is the beginning of wisdom")? A Stage -1 of active resistance (C.S. Lewis, Screwtape Letters as reference)?

Other disciplines: How does the Affective Taxonomy apply to growth in the Fruit of the Spirit (Gal. 5:22–23)? To the three eternal virtues of faith, hope, and love (1 Cor. 13:13)?

Universal-church application: Thompson (Paul, 247): the consciousness of the universal church "liberates the local community from narrow parochialism" — the taxonomy could serve as an integrating framework for what all those who call upon the Lord's name are doing together, across denominations and geographies.

## 8. Conclusion
Spiritual formation is growth in love of God and neighbor — the two great commandments — showing up in behavior from the heart, not performance. The central insight of this paper is that hearing and obeying God is not one discipline among many: it is foundational to all other disciplines, the meta-discipline Jesus identified in Mark 4:13. The Affective Taxonomy, mapped to the Parable of the Sower, provides a descriptive, biblically-grounded heuristic for the human side of the Spirit's formation work.

The taxonomy is not the agent of transformation — that work is irreducibly the Holy Spirit's in individuals and collectives, such that both are transformed in the process. But the taxonomy names what observable human progress looks like, enables leaders to design formation environments appropriate to each level, and integrates individual, small-group, and church-wide formation into a single coherent framework.

At its deepest level, Stage 5 describes what a person and a small group look like when love of God and neighbor has stopped being a commandment to be obeyed and has become simply who they are. The Spirit, working through Word, community, suffering, preaching, worship, and more, has formed it in them.
