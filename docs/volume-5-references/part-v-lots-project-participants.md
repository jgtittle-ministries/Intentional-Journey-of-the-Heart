# Part V: LotS Project Participants

The entries in this section document individuals who participated in the original Laws of the Spirit workshop community or whose personal contributions are referenced in the volumes. These are not academic citations but acknowledgments of the community context in which the LotS project was tested and refined.

## Custer, John
**Author: **John Custer was one of the early participants in the Laws of the Spirit workshop community and is referenced in Vol 3 in connection with the descriptions of those early Saturday morning workshop sessions. He is identified as a person whose characterization of those early sessions — the ethos of honest, curious, disciplined exploration — is worth preserving.

**Summary: **Custer’s participation represents the kind of witness-based testing that Vol 4 identifies as the first and irreplaceable form of LotS research: personal and experiential testing within a committed community. His experience and perspective contributed to the refinement of principles in the workshop’s early years, when the discipline of not accepting any principle unless it could be confirmed from personal experience or scripture was being established.

**Application: **Vol 3 references what Custer described from those early Saturday mornings as a touchstone for the project’s original ethos: the Saturday morning workshop was always more interested in honest exploration than in the appearance of having arrived. This orientation — certainty held appropriately, speculation labeled as speculation, and exploration valued over conclusion — is the epistemological spirit of the entire LotS series.

## Larry (Workshop Member)
**Author: **Larry is identified in Vol 4 as one of the three men who responded to Amanda’s January 2003 newsletter article and joined the original Laws of the Spirit workshop group along with Tom and John (the author). He is identified by first name only throughout the volumes.

**Summary: **Larry’s contribution to the original workshop is described in Vol 4 as bringing scripture to bear on proposed principles: he would push back on a proposed law by bringing a relevant scripture that qualified or nuanced the claim. This represents the scriptural testing dimension of the original workshop’s peer-review process — the discipline of grounding every proposed law in the biblical text.

**Application: **Vol 4 describes the original workshop group’s methodology in terms that modern research methodology would recognize as a purposive sample with a structured peer-review process. Larry’s scriptural grounding contribution was one of the three primary forms of testing the group exercised. His participation is part of the evidentiary foundation for the LotS laws as community-tested rather than individually derived.

## Tittle, Amanda
**Author: **Amanda Tittle  is the author’s daughter, identified as the person who wrote the January 2003 article in the Christ Church (Northern Virginia) community newsletter that invited participation in the Laws of the Spirit project. The article introduced the vision that the spiritual world operates as orderly and knowable as the physical world that the same God created, and invited members of the congregation to join a workshop to explore that vision together.

**Summary: **The 2003 newsletter article is the earliest public document of the LotS project. Amanda’s articulation of the vision — that the spiritual world should be as orderly and knowable as the physical world created by the same God — is described in Vol 3 as planting the seed of the entire quantitative program. The vision she described has driven the project for over two decades.

**Application: **Amanda is referenced in Vol 3 as the original documenter of the vision that became Vol 3’s governing premise, and in Vol 4 as the person who published the invitation that assembled the original workshop community. Her contribution is foundational to the entire project. The author notes in Vol 3 that he is still working on the vision she described in 2003, more than twenty years later.

## Tom (Workshop Member)
**Author: **Tom is identified in Vol 4 as one of the three men who responded to Amanda’s 2003 newsletter article and joined the original LotS workshop group. Like Larry, he is identified by first name only throughout the volumes.

**Summary: **Tom’s contribution to the original workshop is described in Vol 4 as the experiential counter-example: he would push back on a proposed principle with a counter-example from his own experience — cases where the proposed law did not seem to hold. This represents the experiential testing dimension of the workshop’s peer-review process and was essential to preventing the group from confirming only what it already believed.

**Application: **The original workshop’s discipline of honest counter-example — willingness to say ‘I don’t see that’ or ‘that hasn’t been my experience’ — is identified in Vol 4 as the methodological constraint that produced a set of claims that had survived genuine scrutiny from four different men with four different life histories. Tom’s experiential pushback was one of the primary mechanisms of that scrutiny. His participation is part of the community-testing foundation of the LotS laws.
