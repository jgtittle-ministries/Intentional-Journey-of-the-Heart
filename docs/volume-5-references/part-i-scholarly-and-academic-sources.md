# Part I: Scholarly and Academic Sources

## Barna, George
**Author: **George Barna is a widely cited Christian researcher, professor at Arizona Christian University, and founding director of the Barna Research Group and later the Cultural Research Center at Arizona Christian University. He has spent decades conducting empirical studies on the beliefs, practices, and worldviews of American Christians.

**Summary: **In this book Barna examines the results of large-scale survey research into how holding a biblical worldview — defined by affirmative answers to seven foundational questions — produces measurably different life outcomes. He found that only approximately six percent of self-identified born-again Christians answer all seven questions affirmatively, and that this group shows dramatically lower rates of divorce, substance abuse, and incarceration compared to both nominal Christians and non-believers. Barna argues that transformed behavior follows transformed thinking, and that the goal of Christian formation is producing this integrated biblical worldview.

**Application: **The LotS project uses Barna’s biblical worldview framework as the Stage 5 endpoint descriptor of the Affective Taxonomy developmental arc. In Vol 2, Exp. 7 and Vol 4, Section 3, the fully formed Stage 5 Characterization level — in which a person’s values, desires, and behaviors are integrated around scripture — is explicitly described as Barna’s (2003) biblical worldview “fully embodied.” This provides a concrete, research-grounded definition of the formation destination that the entire LotS measurement protocol is oriented toward.

## Bloom, Benjamin S.
**Author: **Benjamin S. Bloom (1913–1999) was an American educational psychologist at the University of Chicago who led the committee that produced the landmark Taxonomy of Educational Objectives in 1956. His work fundamentally restructured how educators think about learning by distinguishing between cognitive, affective, and psychomotor domains. The cognitive taxonomy was revised in 2001 by a group of his former colleagues.

**Summary: **Bloom and his collaborators developed three parallel educational taxonomies — cognitive (1956, revised 2001), affective (Krathwohl, Bloom, and Masia, 1964), and psychomotor (Simpson, 1966) — that together describe the full range of human learning development. The cognitive taxonomy, from Knowledge through Evaluation (revised: Remember through Create), has been the most widely used. The affective taxonomy, which Bloom co-authored, became foundational to the LotS project’s developmental framework.

**Application: **Bloom’s three taxonomies are cited in Vol 3 as expanding the LotS project’s scope considerably, providing a research-grounded framework for thinking about formation across all three human domains. Vol 4 references doctoral research applying all three of Bloom’s taxonomies to spiritual formation practice. The cognitive taxonomy informs the LotS project’s understanding of how scriptural knowledge must ultimately be translated into affective and behavioral transformation — a limitation of purely cognitive engagement with scripture.

## Brueggemann, Walter
**Author: **Walter Brueggemann (b. 1933) is William Marcellus McPheeters Professor Emeritus of Old Testament at Columbia Theological Seminary and one of the most influential Old Testament theologians of the twentieth century. He is particularly known for his work on the Psalms, on prophetic imagination, and on the relationship between lamentation and spiritual formation.

**Summary: **Brueggemann’s framework for reading the Psalms identifies three movements: orientation (settled confidence in God’s order), disorientation (the experience of loss, suffering, or divine hiddenness), and new orientation (a reordering of life around a deeper trust forged through the disorientation). This framework treats suffering and lament not as interruptions to spiritual growth but as its primary engine. The disorientation phase, when navigated with honest prayer addressed to God, functions as a formation crucible.

**Application: **The LotS project draws on Brueggemann extensively across Vols 1–3 to ground the claim that trials and suffering are not obstacles to formation but its primary catalyst (LotS claim LotS-F3). Vol 2 uses Brueggemann’s disorientation framework to explain why the suffering exploration in the Training Plan section belongs at the center of the formation model rather than at its edges. Vol 3 formalizes Brueggemann’s disorientation phase as a formation engine driving stage transitions in the Affective Taxonomy. Vol 4 cites Brueggemann alongside posttraumatic growth research as supporting evidence for the trial-as-formation-catalyst claim.

## Einstein, Albert
**Author: **Albert Einstein (1879–1955) was a German-born theoretical physicist who developed the special and general theories of relativity, transforming the foundations of physics and demonstrating that Newtonian mechanics, while accurate within certain limits, is a special case of a more general framework. He is widely regarded as the most influential physicist of the twentieth century.

**Summary: **Einstein’s work demonstrated that Newtonian mechanics — which had been the complete and unchallenged description of physical reality for over two hundred years — was not wrong but incomplete. From inside the Newtonian frame, relativistic effects appear impossible or anomalous. From inside the Einsteinian frame, Newtonian mechanics is seen as a valid approximation at low velocities. This expansion of framework without discarding the prior framework is the key conceptual move.

**Application: **The LotS project uses Einstein’s relationship to Newton as a structural analogy in Vol 1’s introduction to explain how new explorations may refine or expand the working axioms without discarding them. The author explicitly models the LotS epistemological stance on this pattern: the four foundational axioms are held at “90% certainty” with the explicit acknowledgment that further exploration may refine the general framework just as Einstein refined Newton. This analogy appears in the introduction to Vol 1 and shapes the project’s approach to certainty ratings throughout.

## Feynman, Richard P.
**Author: **Richard P. Feynman (1918–1988) was an American theoretical physicist at the California Institute of Technology, Nobel Laureate in Physics (1965), and one of the most gifted scientific communicators of the twentieth century. His Messenger Lectures at Cornell, published as *The Character of Physical Laws*, addressed fundamental questions about why mathematics so precisely describes the physical universe.

**Summary: **In this work Feynman raises what he calls the ‘unreasonable effectiveness of mathematics’ in describing physical reality — a phrase originated by physicist Eugene Wigner — and candidly admits he does not know the full answer. He explores how physical laws are discovered, how they are structured, and what it means for mathematics to be the language of nature. This question — why does mathematical structure map so precisely onto physical reality? — lies at the heart of Vol 3’s governing premise.

**Application: **Vol 3, Exp. 1 opens with a direct citation of Feynman’s question about the unreasonable effectiveness of mathematics. The author proposes a theological answer: mathematics describes the physical world so accurately because the physical world was spoken into existence by the Logos, the eternal Word, and the mathematical structure of creation is a trace of the structure of the Logos. If that is correct, the spiritual world — which is more fundamental than the physical world — should have mathematical structure deeper than what physics has yet found. This is the governing premise of Vol 3’s entire quantitative program.

## Foster, Richard J.
**Author: **Richard J. Foster (b. 1942) is an American theologian, author, and founder of Renovaté, an international Christian organization focused on spiritual formation. A Quaker by tradition, Foster is widely regarded as one of the most important Protestant voices on the classical spiritual disciplines, having reintroduced them to evangelical audiences through his landmark 1978 work *Celebration of Discipline*.

**Summary: **Foster is referenced in the LotS volumes specifically for his definition of a miracle. He offers what the author describes as the most compact and useful definition available: a miracle is the direct intervention of God in natural processes in a way that accomplishes His stated will. This definition is notable for locating the miracle within the theological framework of divine will and obedience rather than treating it primarily as a suspension of natural law.

**Application: **Vol 3, Exp. 8 (The Miracle Frame and Spiritual Force Dynamics) builds its definition of a miracle on Foster’s formulation as a starting point. The definition is important to the LotS project because it connects miracles directly to the Obedience Channel law: miracles occur when spiritual force, flowing through a cleared channel in the right direction (toward what God is already doing), exceeds the threshold required for God’s stated will to be enacted in the natural world. Foster’s definition makes the miracle logically continuous with the other laws rather than an exception to them.

## Friesen, Garry
**Author: **Garry Friesen is a theologian and professor whose 1980 work challenged what he called the ‘traditional view’ of divine guidance — the idea that God has a specific individual will for each decision in a believer’s life which must be discerned before acting. Friesen argued instead for a ‘wisdom view’ in which God’s moral will is revealed in scripture, and within the bounds of that moral will believers are free to make wise decisions without requiring a specific divine communication for each choice.

**Summary: **The Friesen position represents the more rationalist end of a spectrum in evangelical theology about how God guides believers. His view has been influential in Reformed evangelical circles and stands in significant tension with the LotS project’s emphasis on active, real-time hearing of God’s voice as the primary formation mechanism. The debate between Friesen’s position and the position represented by Dallas Willard (and the LotS project) is one of the significant unresolved tensions in evangelical spirituality.

**Application: **The Willard-Friesen debate is explicitly referenced in the LotS Formation Documents (MSFIG) as a key structural claim that requires resolution: the LotS project aligns with Willard’s position that God speaks specifically and personally, while acknowledging Friesen’s concern about subjectivism. The Obedience Channel law (Vol 1, Exp. 6) — which states that obedience to prior revelation opens the channel for further revelation — presupposes the Willardian position. Vol 2 notes this debate throughout the formation document connections.

## Galilei, Galileo
**Author: **Galileo Galilei (1564–1642) was an Italian mathematician, physicist, and astronomer widely regarded as the father of modern observational science. His foundational contributions to kinematics — the description of motion without reference to its causes — provided the empirical and mathematical foundation on which Newton later built his dynamics.

**Summary: **The relationship between Galileo’s kinematics and Newton’s dynamics is the specific analogy invoked in the LotS volumes. Galileo described how objects move; Newton explained why they move by introducing force, mass, and acceleration. Newton’s dynamic laws cannot be defined without Galileo’s kinematic framework beneath them. This hierarchical relationship between descriptive and explanatory frameworks is directly relevant to Vol 3’s program.

**Application: **Vol 3, Exp. 3 (Spiritual Force: The Analytical Core) uses the Galileo-Newton relationship as a structural analogy for the LotS project’s own two-level program: Vols 1 and 2 described the kinematics of spiritual formation (how things move), while Vol 3 attempts to build the dynamics (why they move, with what force, at what rate). The author cites this analogy as evidence that the Vol 3 quantitative program is ambitious but not structurally unprecedented.

## Hall, Todd W.
**Author: **Todd W. Hall is Professor of Psychology at Rosemead School of Psychology, Biola University, and a leading researcher in the psychology of religion and spirituality. He is the developer of the Spiritual Transformation Inventory (STI), an empirically validated instrument for assessing dimensions of spiritual growth, and has published extensively on the integration of relational neuroscience, attachment theory, and spiritual formation.

**Summary: **Hall’s Spiritual Transformation Inventory is a multi-domain self-report instrument that assesses spiritual growth across five dimensions. The STI has been administered in research with several thousand participants, providing significant normative data on patterns of spiritual development. His work bridges empirical psychology and Christian spiritual formation in ways directly relevant to the LotS project’s quantitative ambitions.

**Application: **Vol 4 draws on Hall’s (2017) STI as one of the primary structural sources for the Personal Spiritual Formation Assessment (PSFA) instrument developed in Section 3. The LotS adaptation borrows the five-domain structure while replacing Hall’s domain content with Affective Taxonomy stage structure and Image of God reflection categories. Vol 4 also cites Hall as providing the most sophisticated existing instrument for operationalizing spiritual transformation in a measurable way, while noting that the LotS project requires modifications specific to its framework.

## Hawkins, Greg L., and Cally Parkinson
**Author: **Greg L. Hawkins served as Executive Pastor of Willow Creek Community Church in South Barrington, Illinois, and led its REVEAL research initiative. Cally Parkinson served as Brand Manager for REVEAL. Together they directed the most comprehensive empirical study of spiritual growth in Christian congregations yet conducted, surveying more than 80,000 people across approximately 1,000 churches in the MOVE study.

**Summary: **The MOVE study identified five stages of spiritual growth (Exploring Christ, Growing in Christ, Close to Christ, Christ-Centered, and Christ is My Life) and found that movement between stages correlates with specific spiritual disciplines. The single most significant finding was that reflective Bible study — defined as reading and reflecting on scripture with the intent to apply it to life — is the one discipline present and predictive of movement at every stage and every transition. No other discipline showed this universal pattern.

**Application: **This finding is one of the most heavily used research citations in the LotS project. Vol 3 cites it as providing research precedent for locating reflective Bible study (the PROAPT process in the LotS framework) as the central formation discipline. Vol 4 uses it as the empirical grounding for LotS claim LotS-H4 (reflective Bible study is the primary discipline advancing heart formation at every stage), cites the MOVE study’s stage descriptions as confirmation of the Affective Taxonomy framework, and uses the MOVE finding about trials as a stage-transition catalyst as supporting evidence for LotS-F3.

## Krathwohl, David R., Benjamin S. Bloom, and Bertram B. Masia
**Author: **David R. Krathwohl was a collaborator of Benjamin Bloom and professor of education who led the development of the affective domain taxonomy published in 1964 as the companion volume to Bloom’s 1956 cognitive taxonomy. Bertram B. Masia was also a collaborator in this project. The affective taxonomy describes how people develop in their emotional, attitudinal, and values-based responses to learning content.

**Summary: **The Affective Taxonomy describes five hierarchical levels of affective development: Receiving (bare awareness and willingness to attend), Responding (active engagement and some compliance), Valuing (internalizing a value as one’s own), Organization (integrating multiple values into a coherent value system), and Characterization (the value system becoming the consistent basis for behavior and identity). The transition from Stage 2 to Stage 3 (Responding to Valuing) is identified as the critical transition, representing the point at which external compliance becomes internal conviction.

**Application: **The Affective Taxonomy is arguably the single most important external framework in the LotS project. It is introduced in Vol 1 as an organizing scaffold for the Parable of the Sower, formalized in Vol 2, Exp. 7 as the developmental framework for the entire formation journey, used in Vol 3 as the primary measurement protocol for spiritual distance, and applied as the central assessment dimension in Vol 4’s Individual Testing Protocol (Section 3b). The five-stage LotS formation arc maps directly onto the five Affective Taxonomy levels, providing the project with an empirically grounded, behaviorally observable developmental sequence.

## Maxwell, James Clerk
**Author: **James Clerk Maxwell (1831–1879) was a Scottish mathematical physicist who unified the theories of electricity, magnetism, and light into a single framework expressed in four differential equations now bearing his name. Maxwell’s equations are widely regarded as the most elegant and powerful unification in the history of classical physics, and they directly implied the existence of electromagnetic waves traveling at the speed of light.

**Summary: **Maxwell’s achievement — taking disparate empirical observations about electricity and magnetism and expressing their unified structure in four compact equations that predicted entirely new phenomena — is the gold standard analogy for what a mature quantitative framework can accomplish. The equations do not merely describe observed phenomena; they predict phenomena not yet observed and reveal the deep structure connecting what seemed like unrelated forces.

**Application: **Maxwell’s equations are referenced in multiple volumes as the paradigmatic example of what the LotS quantitative program is working toward. Vol 1 asks, as an open question: ‘What are the field equations? Maxwell unified electricity and magnetism.’ Vol 3’s governing premise uses Maxwell as the benchmark: the God who created a universe precise enough to be described by Maxwell’s equations does not seem like a God who would create a spiritual world governed by vague, unstructured forces. This single sentence motivates the entire Vol 3 quantitative project.

## Newton, Isaac
**Author: **Isaac Newton (1643–1727) was an English mathematician and physicist whose *Principia Mathematica* (1687) established the three laws of motion and the law of universal gravitation, providing the foundational framework of classical mechanics. Newton built his dynamics on the kinematic foundations laid by Galileo, demonstrating that force, mass, and acceleration could be related in precise mathematical laws.

**Summary: **Newtonian mechanics was the complete and unchallenged description of physical reality for over two hundred years before Einstein showed it to be a special case of a more general relativistic framework. The Newtonian framework remains valid and useful at low velocities; it was refined but not discarded. Additionally, Newton’s logical structure — beginning from axioms (the three laws) and deriving consequences — is the model for the LotS project’s own axiomatic approach.

**Application: **Newton is referenced in Vol 1 in the introduction where the author borrows the language of axioms from mathematics and physics: just as Euclidean geometry and Newtonian mechanics work from a small set of starting assumptions and build outward, the LotS project works from four foundational axioms held at roughly 90% certainty. The Einstein-Newton relationship (refinement without discarding) is used to model the LotS epistemological stance. In Vol 3, the Galileo-Newton kinematic/dynamic relationship is used to frame the relationship between Vols 1–2 (kinematics) and Vol 3 (dynamics).

## Schwarz, Christian A.
**Author: **Christian A. Schwarz (b. 1956, Germany) is the founder and president of NCD International and one of the most widely read figures in empirical church development research. His NCD framework has been implemented by more than 70,000 churches in 84 countries and 112 denominations. His work is grounded simultaneously in empirical research (survey methodology, longitudinal tracking), the classical philosophical tradition (the three transcendentals of Truth, Goodness, and Beauty), and the Reformed-Evangelical-Charismatic theological tradition. His most recent work — the Energy Trilogy (2021–2022) — grounds the NCD framework in the New Testament’s own vocabulary for divine energy at work in the believer.

**Summary: **Natural Church Development (1996; 7th ed. 2006) identifies eight quality characteristics of healthy churches: empowering leadership, gift-oriented ministry, passionate spirituality, effective structures, inspiring worship, holistic small groups, need-oriented evangelism, and loving relationships. These are not a program to be implemented but qualities to be grown organically. The research finding with the strongest implication for the LotS project: holistic small groups is the single quality characteristic most correlated with overall church health across cultures. The minimum-factor strategy, adapted from Liebig’s Law of the Minimum in biochemistry, identifies the single least-developed characteristic as the one requiring targeted attention first — on the principle that raising the rate-limiting variable produces disproportionate overall gains. Schwarz calls the resulting growth movement “all-by-itself growth”: once the constraint is removed, the system grows by its own internal momentum. The six biotic principles (interdependence, multiplication, energy transformation, multi-usage, symbiosis, functionality) describe the structural properties of healthy organic growth. The Trinitarian Compass organizes all NCD tools around the three classical transcendentals (Truth, Goodness, Beauty) mapped onto the three persons of the Trinity — the same coordinate axes that TFT uses as its primary fields.

**Criticism: **Ellas & Yeakley (JASCG 10:2, 1999) raise methodological concerns about sampling and analytic transparency. Critics from a confessional Protestant perspective (notably 9Marks) have raised hermeneutical concerns about Schwarz’s use of natural and biotic metaphors as interpretive frameworks for Scripture. Both critiques deserve acknowledgment. Neither invalidates the empirical value of the survey data, and neither addresses the structural convergence between the Trinitarian Compass and TFT’s transcendental framework, which rests on independent philosophical grounds.

**Application: **The NCD framework is most directly relevant to Vols 3 and 4. Vol 3’s TFT framework uses the same transcendental triad — Truth, Goodness, Beauty — as Schwarz’s Trinitarian Compass, a convergence that warrants explicit attention because the two arrived at it independently. Vol 3’s systems dynamics model (Seventh Exploration) maps directly onto Schwarz’s six biotic principles, which describe at the church level the same structural properties the LotS model is trying to develop at the individual and small-group level. Vol 4’s testing program can draw on the NCD minimum-factor diagnostic logic for small-group health assessment, on the NCD 3 Colors of Community instrument as an external validity check on the GSFP, and on NCD’s research trajectory — 120 churches in 1996, 70,000 by 2016 — as the closest existing model for the staged multi-site program proposed in Vol 4, Section 5.

His *Cultural Liturgies* trilogy, of which *Desiring the Kingdom* is the first volume, draws on Augustine, Merleau-Ponty, and Charles Taylor to argue that human beings are fundamentally desiring creatures whose formation is shaped not primarily by belief but by practice and habit.

**Summary: **Smith argues that formation operates primarily through what he calls ‘cultural liturgies’ — repeated practices that shape desire, imagination, and ultimately behavior below the level of conscious reflection. The goal of Christian formation, on Smith’s account, is what he calls the ‘sanctified imagination’: the reorientation of a person’s deepest desires toward God and God’s kingdom through sustained embodied practice. This is not merely intellectual assent but a transformation of what one loves.

**Application: **Vol 2 and Vol 3 cite Smith (2009) at the critical juncture of the Affective Taxonomy’s Stage 3 (Valuing) — the point at which the mind has been shown the truth and the will must be persuaded to desire it. Smith’s concept of the sanctified imagination is used to describe the formation goal at this transition: not just knowing what is true or being able to respond appropriately, but actually coming to desire what the mind has been shown. This is the heart of the LotS formation program and one of its most important theoretical grounding points.

## Wilber, Ken
**Author: **Ken Wilber (b. 1949) is an American philosopher and the developer of Integral Theory, a meta-framework that attempts to synthesize insights from Eastern and Western philosophy, psychology, science, and spirituality into a coherent whole. His AQAL (‘All Quadrants, All Levels’) framework organizes reality across four quadrants (interior/exterior, individual/collective) and multiple developmental levels, providing a map for integrating diverse perspectives.

**Summary: **The AQAL framework’s four-quadrant grid — individual interior (subjective), individual exterior (behavioral/objective), collective interior (cultural), and collective exterior (social/systemic) — provides a useful organizational scaffold for examining any phenomenon from multiple perspectives simultaneously. Wilber’s framework is explicitly humanistic and post-metaphysical in its philosophical grounding, treating the developing self as the organizing center of the entire architecture.

**Application: **The LotS project used Wilber’s AQAL framework as an organizing scaffold in Vol 1, and the relationship with this framework is one of the most explicitly discussed methodological tensions in the series. Vol 1 notes that while the four-quadrant grid is useful as an organizing tool, Wilber’s metaphysical framework is not compatible with the LotS project’s Trinitarian Christian grounding — in the LotS project, the organizing center is not the developing self but the triune God who constitutes persons through relationship. The author explicitly flags this incompatibility and the need to use the AQAL grid as a tool while replacing its metaphysical base.

## Willard, Dallas
**Author: **Dallas Willard (1935–2013) was Professor of Philosophy at the University of Southern California and one of the most influential evangelical Christian thinkers on spiritual formation, discipleship, and the nature of God’s communication with believers. His work bridged rigorous academic philosophy and accessible spiritual guidance, and he is regarded as a foundational voice in the contemporary spiritual formation movement.

**Summary: **Willard argued throughout his career that the Christian life involves an ongoing, real-time conversational relationship with God in which God speaks specifically to individuals through scripture, circumstances, other people, and direct inner communication. He contrasted this with purely rationalist approaches to divine guidance and argued that the will — the spirit of a person — must be directed toward God through disciplined practice. His concept of ‘the direction of the will’ is particularly important to the LotS project.

**Application: **Willard is referenced across Vols 1–3 as the primary theological grounding for the LotS project’s claim that God speaks specifically and that hearing is the primary formation discipline. The Obedience Channel law (Vol 1, Exp. 6) is substantially grounded in Willard’s understanding of how prior obedience opens the channel for further revelation. The LotS Formation Documents (MSFIG) directly engage the Willard-Friesen debate (see Friesen entry), taking Willard’s position. Vol 2 describes Willard’s practice of attending to the Spirit in small decisions as a model for the daily hearing practice in the Training Plan.
